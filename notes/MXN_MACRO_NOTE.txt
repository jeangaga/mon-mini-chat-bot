MXN — Rolling Macro Note — Mexico

As of date: 16 May 2026
Horizon: 4-week impulse window → 3–6 month macro scenario

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ↓ Weak-growth regime; contraction risk rising but not yet recession base case
Labor: ↓ Formal-employment quality deteriorating
Inflation: ~ Cooling, but above-target and non-core/geopolitical risks still binding
Financial cond: ~ Restrictive real rates support duration; MXN carry lower quality
Policy bias: Hold / plateau after terminal cut
Political risk: ↑ Ratings / USMCA / fiscal-Pemex risk now active

Executive Summary

Mexico is now in a delivered terminal-cut / weak-growth / credibility-sensitive plateau regime. Banxico has cut to 6.50%, and the 3–2 vote plus explicit hold guidance make 6.50% the dominant terminal-rate assumption for 2026. The debate has shifted from whether Banxico had one final cut left to whether weak growth and softer core inflation can reopen easing in 2027.

The growth impulse has weakened, but the correct framing is not outright recession yet. Q1 GDP contracted more than expected, April manufacturing PMI fell deeper into contraction, and March industrial output confirmed that manufacturing and construction remain the main hard-activity drags. The private forecast distribution has moved lower, with several 2026 growth estimates around 1.0–1.2%, while the official growth path remains materially higher. That official/private gap is itself now part of the credibility setup.

Labor is a more advanced warning signal than the earlier draft allowed. Formal job creation has weakened materially, March hiring was weak, and manufacturing employment has been contracting annually for a prolonged period. Mexico still retains a low unemployment-rate cushion and high absolute formal-employment stock, but the marginal signal is deterioration in employment quality, not labor-market reacceleration.

Inflation is cooling enough to justify pause-and-watch, not enough to restart a clean easing cycle. April CPI and PPI validated the final cut, but headline and core remain above target, Banxico raised near-term headline inflation forecasts again, and non-core fuel / energy risks remain live through the Middle East channel. The binding constraint is now credibility under sticky-but-cooling inflation, not classic stagflation.

MXN remains a carry currency, but the carry is now lower quality. The rate differential still supports the peso, and the end-cycle signal reduces disorderly-easing risk. But S&P’s negative outlook revision, Pemex/fiscal rigidity, USMCA tariff uncertainty and weaker domestic growth all increase the risk premium. Mbonos have a cleaner weak-growth duration story than MXN spot, where the trade is now carry versus sovereign / trade / credibility tail risk.

Growth / Activity

Growth has moved from contested stagnation to a clearer weak-growth regime. Q1 GDP was the decisive hard-data update: output contracted 0.8% q/q and slowed to 0.1% y/y, with weakness across services, industry and primary activity. That damages the earlier offset narrative where services and domestic demand could absorb manufacturing softness.

The manufacturing tape confirms the weakness. April PMI fell to 47.7, marking a deeper contraction with weaker output, new orders, export sales and employment. The March PMI improvement to 48.9 looked like slower deterioration; April removed that comfort. The industrial side is still the clearest weak pocket, but Q1 GDP shows the slowdown is no longer confined to one sector.

March industrial output reinforced the same regime, falling 0.6% m/m and 1.3% y/y. Construction remains the domestic-demand drag, while manufacturing still fails to provide a floor. Mining and utilities offer offsets, but they do not change the macro read.

The scenario distribution is lower but still two-sided. Private forecasts have moved toward roughly 1.0–1.2% growth for 2026, while official forecasts remain materially higher. That divergence matters because it creates a policy credibility problem: the market is increasingly pricing weaker activity than the official baseline. Stabilization requires retail, services, remittances, construction and manufacturing orders to stop confirming the downside impulse. A recessionary reweighting requires Q2 GDP, labor and consumption to validate Q1 as persistent rather than front-loaded weakness.

Labor / Employment

Labor is now a core transmission channel, even if the unemployment-rate level still provides a cushion. The marginal signal has deteriorated through formal employment, weak March job creation, and persistent manufacturing employment contraction. This is more than a soft PMI employment line; the formal-sector labor tape is consistent with the broader manufacturing / investment drag.

The key issue is employment quality rather than outright labor-market collapse. Mexico still has low unemployment and a large formal-employment stock, so the labor setup does not yet force a recession call. But formal job creation has softened, manufacturing employment has been contracting annually for many months, and the employment impulse is moving in the same direction as GDP, PMI and industrial output.

This matters for Banxico because labor is the bridge between weak activity and a reopened easing debate. A soft activity tape alone can be handled with restrictive-but-paused policy. A labor-confirmed slowdown would make the 6.50% plateau harder to defend, especially if core inflation continues to ease and MXN remains orderly.

Inflation

Inflation has shifted from blocker to conditional constraint. April headline CPI eased to 4.45%, core slowed to 4.26%, and PPI cooled to 2.6% y/y. That was enough to validate the May cut and remove the immediate hawkish tail from the prior March inflation/PPI shock.

The relief is not clean. Headline and core remain above Banxico’s target band midpoint, and near-term headline forecasts were revised higher again. The core path is better behaved, but the headline/non-core channel remains vulnerable to fuel, transport and Middle East-linked energy pressure. High-octane gasoline strength in April is a reminder that non-core shocks are not just theoretical.

The PMI inflation signal creates a margin-pressure problem rather than a simple CPI pass-through problem. Input prices rose sharply while output prices remained subdued, which points to firms absorbing costs instead of passing them fully into measured inflation. That is less damaging for CPI in the short run, but more damaging for margins, investment and employment.

The right inflation framing is sticky-but-cooling, not uncontrolled stagflation. Banxico can hold at 6.50% if core continues to ease and MXN stays stable. It cannot reopen an easing sequence while headline forecasts are being revised higher, long-term expectations concern dissents, and geopolitical energy risk remains part of the reaction function.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

Banxico’s latest policy meeting was on 7 May 2026. The Board cut the reference rate by 25bp to 6.50% in a 3–2 vote, with two members preferring to hold. The cut acknowledged weak Q1 activity, softer April inflation and still-restrictive monetary conditions. The guidance was the key signal: maintaining the current rate was presented as appropriate, converting the decision into an end-cycle cut rather than a renewed easing phase.

The reaction function is best described as credibility-anchored, with growth as a second-order input. Banxico is not ignoring weak activity; the cut itself proves growth matters. But the dissents, long-term inflation-expectations concern, higher near-term headline forecasts and explicit Middle East-risk caveat show that inflation credibility dominates the next phase.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION: Banxico holds at 6.50% through the next 3–6 months. The dominant 2026 market assumption is terminal rate at 6.50%, with any additional easing more likely a 2027 debate than an immediate 2026 base case. The path becomes more dovish if Q2 activity confirms the Q1 contraction, formal labor deteriorates further, core/services inflation continues to cool and MXN remains stable. The path becomes more hawkish if core stalls, non-core fuel/energy pressure broadens, MXN weakens, or ratings / fiscal risk raises the credibility cost of easing.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end has moved from terminal-cut pricing to plateau pricing. Weak GDP, PMI, industrial output and labor quality support duration, but Banxico’s hold guidance caps a clean 2026 easing path. Receivers need either a materially weaker growth/labor impulse or more convincing core/services disinflation to extend beyond the plateau.

Real rates remain restrictive and are part of the growth drag. At 6.50% against still-elevated inflation, policy is restrictive enough to pressure domestic demand, construction, investment and financing-sensitive activity. That gives Mbonos a cleaner macro argument than MXN: duration can benefit from weak activity even if Banxico does not immediately cut further.

MXN carry remains alive but lower quality. The rate differential still matters, and the cycle-end signal prevents the peso from trading like a policy-error currency. But carry appeal has weakened at the margin, and the market is now watching the Fed differential, USMCA headlines, ratings risk and fiscal/Pemex liabilities alongside Banxico.

The S&P negative outlook revision is now an active FX/rates constraint, not background noise. With two major agencies negative and fiscal consolidation vulnerable to low growth, MXN is more exposed to sovereign-risk premium. A forced-seller scenario is not the base case, but the asymmetry has worsened: weaker growth helps bonds until it starts damaging fiscal credibility.

Political / Policy Events

Political and policy risk is now regime-relevant through USMCA, ratings and fiscal/Pemex channels. USMCA is the most immediate binary catalyst. The first official bilateral negotiating round is scheduled for the week of 25 May in Mexico City, with rules of origin and industrial-goods treatment central to the negotiation. Tariff relief for steel and autos is not assured, which keeps investment uncertainty alive.

The sovereign-risk layer has tightened. S&P’s negative outlook revision shifted ratings from latent risk to active macro constraint. The key channel is slow fiscal consolidation under weak growth, with Pemex and CFE contingent liabilities aggravating fiscal rigidity. That matters for both Mbonos and MXN because the policy mix is now judged against sovereign credibility, not just inflation and growth.

There are partial offsets, but not yet enough to dominate the risk premium. Plan México, infrastructure investment, World Cup-related demand and the Mexico-EU trade-deal track can support H2 activity and diversify the policy narrative. They matter as upside scenario inputs, but the near-term market catalyst is still USMCA and ratings credibility.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The public tape broadly validates the core regime: Banxico has delivered the terminal cut to 6.50%, the 3–2 vote and explicit hold guidance have shifted the rates debate from “one more cut” to “plateau through 2026,” and the main institutional baseline is no longer an active easing cycle. BofA, Banorte, Rabobank and BBVA converge around a 6.50% hold for the rest of 2026, with the only real dovish reopener pushed into late-2026/2027 and conditional on core convergence plus Fed easing. The market is therefore not trading Mexico as a clean recessionary easing story; it is trading weak-growth duration support against a credibility-anchored central bank. That confirms the Mbonos-vs-MXN asymmetry: bonds have a cleaner weak-growth argument, while MXN remains carry-supported but lower quality because the marginal narrative has shifted toward carry erosion, fiscal/rating risk and USMCA/tariff event risk rather than pure rate-differential support.

The main tension is that the public narrative is softer than a pure “stagflation-lite” frame. Inflation is still above target, Banxico raised near-term headline forecasts again, the dissents were tied to long-term expectations, and the Middle East / Hormuz channel is explicitly in the reaction function; but April CPI/PPI cooling and weak Q1 demand allow Banxico to argue that demand-side price pressure is absent. The right inflation read is therefore “sticky-but-cooling with non-core/geopolitical caveats,” not uncontrolled inflation pressure. Growth is also more nuanced than a simple recession call: private forecasts have moved down toward roughly 1.0–1.2%, the official forecast gap has become a credibility issue, and formal labor evidence is weaker than the first draft implied; but low unemployment, record formal-employment stock, remittances/consumption unknowns, Plan México, World Cup demand and possible H2 investment support keep the distribution two-sided. The debate is less about whether Mexico is weak and more about whether weakness remains bond-positive slack or turns into sovereign-credit / fiscal credibility deterioration.

The near-term scenario breakers are concentrated. The 25 May USMCA round is the key MXN binary: constructive rules-of-origin / tariff language can compress investment-risk premium, while a contentious round or no tariff relief keeps MXN as the adjustment variable. The S&P negative outlook has moved ratings from background to active macro constraint; Fitch following, Pemex liabilities crystallizing, or fiscal consolidation slipping would turn weak growth from duration-positive into sovereign-spread / MXN-negative. The next CPI prints decide whether the April inflation truce extends or whether fuel / energy pass-through reappears; Q2 GDP, formal employment, retail/services and remittances decide whether the 6.50% plateau remains credible or whether the market starts to price a 2027 reopener. The unresolved market question is not the May cut — that is done — but whether Mexico stays a credible high-real-rate carry market with weak growth, or becomes a policy-constrained low-growth sovereign-risk story.

Bottom Line

Mexico is in a delivered terminal-cut regime with weak growth, deteriorating formal-employment quality and sticky-but-cooling inflation. Banxico is now a credibility-anchored central bank at 6.50%, with growth relevant but not sufficient on its own to reopen cuts. Mbonos have a cleaner weak-growth duration argument than MXN, where carry is still alive but increasingly exposed to ratings, USMCA and fiscal/Pemex risk. The main scenario risk is no longer the May cut; it is whether weak growth stays bond-positive or becomes sovereign-credit negative.

Base case: Banxico holds at 6.50% through 2026; Mexico remains weak-growth but not recession base case, with duration supported, MXN lower-quality carry, and the next regime break driven by USMCA, ratings risk, labor confirmation and the May/June inflation path.

Executive Summary

Activity has deteriorated beyond a narrow industrial wobble: industrial output and IGAE remain weak, formal labor creation has softened materially, employer registrations are in a long contraction, consumption has lost momentum, and services no longer provide a clean offset. The base case is stagnation risk, but not yet a consensus recession call.

A constructive institutional view still sees 2026 growth near 1.6–1.8%, supported by consumption resilience, gradual investment recovery, Plan México and World Cup-related demand. That does not remove the weakness in industry, labor and consumption, but it means the macro call should be framed as low-growth / stagnation risk with a contested recovery path, not a settled downturn.

Banxico is now in terminal-cycle mode. The 26 Mar cut to 6.75% and the Governor’s 28 Apr communication point to one final 25bp cut, most likely May or June, that could end the easing cycle around 6.50%. The reaction function is growth-tolerant, but not deeply dovish: policy remains restrictive, core has improved, MXN is orderly, and Banxico is trying to finish normalization before services inflation, food/energy pressure or external shocks close the window.

MXN remains resilient because the market still sees the easing path as controlled. The peso’s 17.25–17.50 range and still-wide carry cushion show no credibility break. The asymmetry is nevertheless more fragile: if the final cut occurs with stable MXN and softer core, carry remains valid; if services inflation, oil/Hormuz pressure or USMCA uncertainty re-intensify, the market can reprice from “terminal normalization” to “policy constraint / credibility risk.”

Growth / Activity

Growth is weak, but the correct framing is contested stagnation rather than one-way collapse. Industrial output and IGAE remain the hardest negative signals. February IGAE again failed to show recovery, and industrial output confirmed that the manufacturing side remains impaired. The signal is persistent weakness, not a fresh break every month.

The weakness has broadened enough to matter. Services nowcasts have softened, private consumption has lost momentum, and formal employment quality has deteriorated. That reduces confidence in the earlier offset narrative where services and households could absorb the industrial drag. The economy still has buffers, but they are thinner.

The counterweight is that several institutional forecasts remain more constructive than the hard-data impulse. A 2026 GDP path around 1.4–1.8% is still plausible if consumption stabilizes, investment recovers gradually, US demand holds, Plan México supports fixed investment, and USMCA negotiations de-escalate. The base case should therefore remain low-growth / stagnation risk, with recession risk conditional on confirmation from labor, consumption and services rather than industrial data alone.

Labor / Employment

Labor is now a core macro transmission channel. Formal job creation weakened materially in Q1, manufacturing employment has remained in annual contraction, and employer registrations have been contracting for a prolonged period. This is not just a soft monthly labor patch; it is a structural employment-quality warning.

The key issue is the composition of labor weakness. Manufacturing employment weakness fits the industrial and investment drag. Employer-registration contraction suggests the corporate formation / formal-sector channel is under pressure, which matters for investment, hiring and domestic-demand persistence.

This labor deterioration strengthens the case for Banxico to finish the easing cycle. It does not yet justify a deeper easing cycle because inflation is not clean and MXN credibility still matters. A discrete payroll or employment break would be the trigger that shifts the growth regime from stagnation risk toward recession risk.

Inflation

Inflation is two-sided, with core improving but the underlying services / food / energy floor still material. First-half April core inflation was the key dovish unlock: it softened to a five-month low and preserved the May/June final-cut window. This makes inflation a constraint, not a blocker.

The constraint is the composition. Services inflation remains sticky enough to keep the disinflation process incomplete. Food, restaurants / lodging and energy-linked categories remain uncomfortable, while the Middle East / Hormuz overlay keeps transport and oil pass-through risk alive. March PPI is a warning flag, but the more important policy channel is whether these pressures spill into services, expectations or FX pass-through.

Banxico can look through non-core pressure if core continues to improve and MXN stays firm. The hawkish risk is not headline inflation alone; it is a services re-acceleration or energy shock that validates the dissenting view and forces a May hold or a more explicit pause after one final cut.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

Banxico’s latest policy meeting was on 26 Mar 2026. The Board cut the policy rate by 25bp to 6.75% in a 3–2 decision. The decision reflected weak activity, an orderly exchange rate, still-restrictive monetary conditions and a view that prior inflation-risk tests had been sufficiently cleared to permit another step toward neutral.

The forward signal is terminal. Banxico is not guiding an open easing sequence. The language around “an additional” cut, the split vote and the Governor’s 28 Apr communication point to one final 25bp move that may end the easing cycle. Policy would still be restrictive at 6.50%, but the bar for further cuts after that is materially higher.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION: Banxico delivers one final 25bp cut to 6.50% in May or June, then pauses. The path becomes more dovish only if labor, consumption and activity deteriorate more clearly while core and services inflation keep softening and MXN remains stable. The path becomes more hawkish if services inflation re-accelerates, oil/Hormuz pressure persists, MXN weakens, or the dissenting credibility argument gains traction. The central bank is reacting most to weak activity, still-restrictive real rates, MXN stability and the risk that external shocks interrupt disinflation before normalization is complete.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is trading terminal-cycle pricing. One final 25bp cut is the central path; a May hold is a live risk if inflation composition worsens, but a deeper easing cycle needs a broader labor / demand break. Receivers require confirmation that services disinflation is durable and that the labor weakness is broad enough to justify more than 6.50%.

MXN remains a carry-supported currency. Spot strength around the 17.25–17.50 zone shows that the market is not yet treating Banxico’s cut as a policy error. The rate differential remains wide, and the softer April core print allowed the peso to absorb the terminal-cut narrative.

The asymmetry is narrower than spot suggests. Carry works while the final cut is framed as controlled normalization. It becomes vulnerable if the market shifts toward forced easing, USMCA stress, oil-driven inflation or a credibility challenge from services persistence. Financial conditions therefore remain supportive for MXN but restrictive enough to keep pressure on the real economy.

Political / Policy Events

Political and policy risk is regime-relevant, but the direction of USMCA risk improved at the margin in late April. The review remains an active investment and manufacturing channel, with uncertainty around rules of origin, supply-chain security and renewal terms weighing on investment decisions. But the Greer–Sheinbaum / Greer–Ebrard sequence and the scheduling of a formal bilateral negotiating round for the week of 25 May reduce the near-term risk of an uncontrolled escalation.

That makes USMCA a two-way MXN catalyst rather than a purely negative overlay. Constructive procedural progress can compress risk premium and support MXN despite weak domestic data. A failed or contentious round would quickly revive the investment-drag and currency-risk channel.

The Middle East / Hormuz shock remains the other live policy variable. It matters through oil, transport costs, food / energy inflation and Banxico’s reaction function. A contained shock allows the final cut; a renewed oil leg higher can delay it or force a hawkish pause.
