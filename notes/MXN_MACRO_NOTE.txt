MXN — Rolling Macro Note — Mexico
As of date: 28 Apr 2026
Horizon: 4-week impulse window → 3–6 month macro scenario
4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD
Growth: ↓ Stagnation broadening beyond industry
Labor: ↓ Formal job creation weakening
Inflation: ~ Core improving; services/food/energy still constrain
Financial cond: ~ MXN strong / carry supported, but trade and oil tails active
Policy bias: Final-cut zone; easing cycle near terminal
Political risk: ↑ USMCA/T-MEC and Middle East energy shock are active macro channels
Executive Summary
Mexico is no longer just an industrial-softness story. The activity drag has broadened from manufacturing and investment into weaker services, softer consumption and deteriorating formal employment. Industrial output and IGAE still define the hard-data weakness, but labor, consumption and nowcast services data make the stagnation call more durable than a single-sector wobble.
Banxico has shifted into the final stage of the easing cycle rather than an open-ended easing regime. The 26 Mar cut to 6.75% showed a more growth-tolerant reaction function, but the statement language and institutional pricing point to one additional 25bp cut, likely May or June, before a pause around 6.50%. The Board is not ignoring inflation; it is judging that policy remains restrictive, core is improving, and the Middle East shock / weak activity mix justifies moving closer to neutral before the window closes.
The binding constraint has changed. It is not simply “sticky core blocks cuts.” Core has softened enough to keep the last cut alive, with 1H April core falling to a five-month low. The constraint is now whether services inflation, food/fuel pressure, oil/Hormuz risk and MXN pass-through stay contained long enough for Banxico to finish the cycle without damaging credibility.
MXN is not yet trading like a credibility-fragile currency. The peso has remained strong around 17.25–17.40, supported by the still-wide rate differential, carry demand and softer April inflation. The risk is more asymmetric than spot price action suggests: carry remains validated while the final cut is seen as controlled normalization, but the trade becomes vulnerable if USMCA/T-MEC uncertainty, oil shock or labor deterioration turns the easing narrative from “final normalization” into “growth-driven forced cuts.”
Growth / Activity
Growth has moved from industrial underperformance to broader stagnation. Industrial output remains the clearest hard-data weak point, with February output down more than expected and the manufacturing cycle still impaired. February IGAE at -0.3% y/y confirmed that recovery expectations had failed again, but the important nuance is that the print was weak and flat rather than a fresh collapse.
The weak pocket is no longer only industry. Services have started to lose their buffer role in the near-term nowcast, private consumption has softened, and investment is under pressure from trade-regime uncertainty. The domestic-demand cushion is therefore thinner than earlier in the year. This matters because Mexico’s previous macro resilience rested on the idea that services and labor could offset industrial drag; that offset is now less reliable.
The structural channel is investment and trade uncertainty. USMCA/T-MEC risk is no longer only a background political premium: it is operating through delayed investment, supply-chain caution and manufacturing employment weakness. Mexico’s 3–6 month growth scenario is therefore stagnation with recession-tail risk, not clean downturn yet, but also not a temporary industrial patch.
Labor / Employment
Labor has become a relevant confirmation signal. Formal job creation weakened materially in Q1, manufacturing employment remained in contraction, and employer registrations deteriorated. The manufacturing labor signal is consistent with the industrial data rather than contradicting it.
The key transmission is not a sudden layoff shock across the whole economy. It is a slow deterioration in formal employment quality and manufacturing labor demand, which reduces the credibility of any “services-led offset” narrative. Employment weakness also raises the probability that consumption softness is not purely temporary.
For Banxico, this strengthens the growth-side case for finishing the easing cycle, but it does not create a license for aggressive cuts. Labor deterioration supports one final normalization move if inflation cooperates; it would require a broader employment break to re-open a deeper easing path.
Inflation
Inflation is two-sided. The dovish part is core: 1H April core inflation fell to a five-month low, and the softer print materially supported the case for another cut. This is the key reason the May/June easing window remains open.
The constraint is services, food and energy rather than broad core acceleration. Services inflation remains too sticky to declare victory, food inflation is still uncomfortable, and the Middle East / Hormuz overlay keeps energy and transport pass-through risk alive. March headline inflation and the PPI surge reinforced the discomfort, but the more decision-relevant issue is whether these shocks leak into services/core and expectations.
The inflation regime is therefore not “solved,” but it is no longer strong enough to block one final cut by itself. Banxico can look through non-core pressure if core continues to moderate, MXN stays firm and oil does not extend the shock. The hawkish risk is a renewed services or energy pass-through impulse that makes the March dissent look prescient.
Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption
Banxico’s latest policy meeting was on 26 Mar 2026. The Board cut the policy rate by 25bp to 6.75% in a 3–2 decision. The decision was a reaction-function break: Banxico moved despite elevated headline inflation because activity had weakened, policy remained restrictive, the exchange rate was orderly, and the Middle East shock increased the value of moving policy closer to neutral before external conditions worsened.
The vote split matters, but the forward guidance matters more. The Board shifted toward evaluating the timing of an additional reference-rate cut, not an open sequence of reductions. That points to an easing cycle in its terminal phase. Policy is still restrictive, but the gap to neutral is now smaller, and the burden of proof for additional cuts after the next move is higher.
CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION: Banxico’s base case is one final 25bp cut to 6.50% in May or June, then a pause. The path becomes more dovish only if labor and activity deteriorate more clearly while core/services inflation continues to soften and MXN remains stable. The path becomes more hawkish if services inflation re-accelerates, oil/Hormuz pressure persists, MXN weakens, or the Board decides that the March split exposed too much credibility risk. The central bank is reacting most to the interaction of weak activity, still-restrictive real rates, MXN stability and the inflation implications of the Middle East shock.
Rates / FX / Financial Conditions
The front end is trading a terminal-cut regime, not a deep easing cycle. Weak activity, labor deterioration and softer core inflation support one more cut, while services inflation, food/fuel pressure and the split Board limit the case for aggressive receivers. The curve needs evidence of a broader labor break or sustained core disinflation to price more than a terminal 6.50% path.
MXN remains supported by carry. The peso’s strength around 17.25–17.40 shows that markets are not yet treating Banxico’s cut as a credibility error. The still-wide rate differential, stable exchange rate and softer April core print keep the carry trade alive.
The risk is not spot weakness today; it is scenario convexity. MXN is resilient while cuts are framed as controlled normalization into weak growth. It becomes vulnerable if USMCA/T-MEC escalation, oil/Hormuz shock or services inflation forces Banxico to choose between currency credibility and growth relief. Financial conditions are therefore supportive for the currency but still restrictive for the real economy.
Political / Policy Events
Political and external-policy risk is regime-relevant. USMCA/T-MEC review risk is now an active investment channel, not just a tail hedge. Uncertainty around renewal terms, rules of origin, supply-chain security and potential U.S. pressure is already weighing on investment and manufacturing decisions.
The Middle East conflict is the second active policy shock. It matters through oil, transport costs, inflation expectations and Banxico’s reaction function. The March cut was partly justified by the need to position policy for a more uncertain external environment, but the same shock also raises the risk that headline and services inflation become harder to control.
The political / policy layer therefore cuts both ways. USMCA uncertainty weakens growth and supports a dovish policy impulse, while oil/Hormuz risk and MXN pass-through constrain how far Banxico can ease without damaging credibility.

Debate / Public Tape Tension
The dominant market narrative broadly validates the weak-growth / terminal-easing setup. Banxico is viewed as having resumed easing because activity is weak, policy remains restrictive and the exchange rate is orderly, while the softer April core print keeps one more cut alive. The peso’s strength shows that markets still see the move as controlled normalization rather than forced easing, with carry supported by the still-wide rate differential.
The key tension is that the macro data are weaker than the currency. Labor, consumption, services and investment now argue for a broader stagnation regime, but MXN has not priced a credibility break. That makes the trade less about whether Mexico is slowing and more about whether Banxico can finish the cycle at 6.50% without re-opening inflation or FX risk.
The unresolved issues are narrow but material. Services inflation must keep moderating, oil/Hormuz pressure must not broaden, and the USMCA/T-MEC process must avoid becoming an investment and currency shock. A soft final cut with stable MXN keeps the base case intact; a services-inflation rebound or trade-policy escalation turns the current carry resilience into a vulnerable asymmetry.
Bottom Line
Mexico is in a broadening stagnation regime, but not an MXN credibility-break regime. Banxico is close to the end of the easing cycle, with one final 25bp cut more likely than an open sequence. Core inflation has improved enough to allow that move, while services, food, energy and MXN pass-through keep the pause threshold high afterward. MXN carry remains supported by spot strength and the rate differential, but the asymmetry is worsening because labor, investment and trade-policy risk are now moving against the macro story.
Base case: one final Banxico cut to 6.50%, then pause; weak Mexican growth, resilient but lower-quality MXN carry, and rising sensitivity to USMCA/T-MEC and oil-shock tails.
