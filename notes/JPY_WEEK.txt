A. JAPAN WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS
The multi-week block covering 15 February to 6 March delivered the most analytically rich Japan data window of the full file, and its net message was a meaningful upgrade to the Japan growth picture without a corresponding inflation acceleration that would force BoJ urgency. The Q4 GDP preliminary had disappointed badly in the initial release (0.1% QQ versus 0.4% consensus, annualised 0.2% versus 1.6%); the subsequent revision on 10 March — outside this block but providing the context — upgraded that to 0.3% QQ and 1.3% annualised on a capex-led basis. Within this block, the data that built toward that revision were already pointing in the right direction: MOF business capex rose 6.5% YY versus 2.9% prior, machinery orders had already shown a large December rebound (19.1% m/m, 16.8% YY), and flash then final PMIs confirmed manufacturing in firm expansion territory (52.8 flash, 53.0 final) while services and composite held above 53.
The household demand side remained the weak flank. Retail sales beat expectations for January (1.8% YY versus -0.4% consensus), and chain store sales improved to 2.7% YY from flat. But housing starts remained negative YY (-0.4%), January industrial output rebounded but missed consensus materially (2.2% m/m versus 5.3%), and the jobs-to-applicants ratio softened marginally (1.18 versus 1.19 consensus) while unemployment edged up to 2.7%. The January household spending release — arriving the following week outside this block — would later confirm that consumption remained negative YY despite improved worker income. The services-strong, household-uneven, capex-improving picture that defined Japan throughout the file therefore continued, but with the capex and investment side making a more credible case for sustained momentum than at any prior point in the monitoring window.
On inflation, the picture stayed balanced in a way that preserved BoJ optionality without forcing urgency. Tokyo CPI for February softened to ex-fresh food 1.8% from 2.0%, and February CGPI printed -0.1% m/m and 2.0% YY — softer than consensus on both — with government fuel subsidies helping suppress the pipeline but yen-based import prices accelerating as an underlying risk. Nationwide CPI for January showed core at 2.0% in line with the downward trajectory flagged since the December Tokyo print. The net inflation read is moderation rather than re-acceleration, consistent with the BoJ's own H1 2026 forecast but not so weak as to rule out a later-year normalization move.
The closing regime is: Japan firmer on growth than the initial Q4 GDP print suggested, capex improvement gaining credibility, household demand still the unreliable component, pipeline inflation softening but import-price risk alive, and the BoJ retaining June-to-October 2026 normalization optionality without urgency.
