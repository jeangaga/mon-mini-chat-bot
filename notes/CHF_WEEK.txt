The Board held at the lower bound, as universally expected, but the operative signal was the explicit statement that the SNB's willingness to intervene in the foreign-exchange market had increased given the conflict in the Middle East. That framing confirmed that Switzerland's primary policy response channel to the geopolitical and energy shock is FX management, not rate adjustment. At 0%, the SNB has no conventional rate room to respond to tightening financial conditions through the rate instrument, so the intervention signal is the functional equivalent of a policy lean in other DM central banks.

Switzerland sat entirely outside that dispersion: no domestic overheating problem, no second-round inflation concern, no capacity-pressure hike debate. The Swiss regime is watchful hold with FX optionality as the active instrument, and the geopolitical shock reinforced rather than disrupted that configuration.

A. SWITZERLAND WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS
The week of 5–9 January established the Swiss macro baseline entering 2026: a SNB in a clearly dovish configuration, with near-zero inflation, manufacturing in contraction, and retail demand soft despite a prior-month beat. The three releases arriving across the week pointed uniformly in the same direction — an economy that was not generating the domestic demand or price pressure that would justify any tightening, and that was providing the SNB with maximum room to maintain its low-rate posture or ease further if growth concerns deepened.
The December CPI print was the most important single release of the week: 0.1% YY versus 0.1% consensus and 0.0% prior — inflation barely positive and consistent with the SNB's structurally low-inflation environment. The monthly print at 0.0% versus -0.1% consensus was a marginal upside versus expectations but confirmed the deflation-risk-not-intensifying read rather than introducing any inflation concern. Manufacturing PMI surged to 49.7 from 45.8 consensus — a sharp positive surprise that lifted the index close to the expansion threshold and materially reduced the most pessimistic growth read. But the PMI remaining below 50 kept the contractionary regime intact, and the Retail Sales November print at -1.1% YY versus 2.8% prior confirmed the domestic demand picture was soft. The net read entering the post-January assessment period was: SNB comfortable, inflation stable at near-zero, manufacturing less weak than feared but still contractionary, and domestic demand softening.

SIGNAL TENSION CHECK
Main contradiction: Manufacturing PMI surged 3.9pp above consensus to 49.7 — removing the most pessimistic growth read — while retail sales softened from 2.8% to -1.1% YY, pointing to a household demand picture that deteriorated sharply from the prior month despite the manufacturing stabilisation.
Why it matters: The divergence between manufacturing stabilisation and retail softening is the key interpretive tension for the SNB's growth assessment. If manufacturing leads retail (as the PMI improvement implies forward demand is recovering), the growth picture may stabilise without requiring additional SNB easing. If retail leads (as the demand softening implies the consumer channel is not validating the manufacturing survey), the growth picture remains fragile and SNB dovish bias deepens.
Implication: SNB holds with a dovish bias. The Feb 15 – Mar 6 data block will provide the first comprehensive post-January activity assessment with GDP, KOF, retail, and PMI all arriving together. That cluster is the next meaningful Switzerland data event in the file.
