INDIA / INR MACRO NOTE — RESILIENT GROWTH, FX-STRESSED SUPPLY SHOCK, HAWKISH-TAIL HOLD

As of: 29 Apr 2026
Horizon: 3–6 months
Central bank: Reserve Bank of India
FX focus: INR pressure, oil / CAD channel, RBI FX management, WPI-to-CPI pass-through

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ Resilient, but downgraded and partly front-loaded
Labor: ~ Mixed — formal hiring firm, informal-sector risk rising
Inflation: ↓ WPI shock, CPI still contained but moving toward RBI midpoint
Financial cond: ↓ INR stress, outflow pressure, active FX management
Policy bias: Hold longer, hawkish tail; easing optionality very conditional
Political risk: ~ No domestic political shock, but fuel-price shielding now macro-relevant

Executive Summary

India remains resilient, but the regime is no longer clean quality growth. The last four weeks shifted the macro centre of gravity from strong growth with inflation monitoring to resilient activity under an oil / FX / inflation supply shock. April PMIs show that the activity floor is intact, but part of the rebound reflects precautionary production, inventory rebuilding and supply-chain hedging rather than a pure final-demand acceleration. At the same time, WPI jumped to a 38-month high, RBI lifted its forward inflation path, INR traded close to record lows, and authorities moved into a much more active FX-management regime.

The binding constraint is now the oil / CAD / INR / imported-inflation channel, with WPI-to-CPI pass-through partly buffered by fiscal and administered-price policy. That buffer matters: fuel-tax cuts, OMC under-recoveries and export duties suppress immediate retail-price pass-through. But it does not remove the shock. It migrates the pressure into fiscal space, OMC balance sheets, potential subsidy/capex trade-offs, the current account and the rupee risk premium.

The RBI base case is prolonged hold with hawkish tail, not delayed easing. The central bank is unlikely to hike mechanically into a supply shock unless second-round effects, CPI expectations or INR pressure become persistent. But the distribution has moved materially away from rate-cut optionality. For INR, relative growth quality is no longer the dominant market frame; oil-import vulnerability, portfolio outflows, current-account risk, REER valuation and RBI volatility management now dominate.

Growth / Activity

Growth remains resilient, but the absolute outlook has been reset lower. Earlier in the year, India had the cleanest combination in the EM file: strong PMIs, broad industrial strength, investment-led GDP and low CPI. That regime has not collapsed, but the forward growth profile has deteriorated. RBI cut FY27 growth to 6.9% from 7.6%, and public forecasts now span a wide range, with downside concentrated in energy costs, external demand, informal-sector absorption capacity and the risk that fiscal resources move from capex toward price stabilization.

The April PMI rebound is real but not clean. Manufacturing rose to 55.9, services to 57.9 and composite to 58.3, with hiring at a 10-month high and strong manufacturing export orders. That keeps India out of a weak-growth easing regime. But finished-goods inventories rose at the fastest pace since 2015, and part of the output rise reflected firms accelerating production ahead of possible supply disruption. The right read is resilient activity with front-loading, not a clean reacceleration of domestic final demand.

The sector split remains important. Manufacturing is the swing sector because it captures both the rebound and the shock hedge: firms are producing, hiring and building stocks, but also facing elevated input costs. Services remain strong in level terms, but services-export momentum has softened and March services already showed weaker domestic new business. The growth floor is intact, but the quality of growth is less certain than the headline PMI level suggests.

Labor / Employment

Labor is not the binding policy variable, but it is no longer irrelevant. March unemployment rose to 5.10%, urban unemployment reached 6.8%, and labor-force participation eased to 55.4%. That is not a labor shock and does not force RBI support, but it prevents the PMI rebound from being read as a broad overheating signal.

The key labor nuance is formal-sector survey strength versus informal-sector fragility. April PMIs showed firmer hiring, which supports the growth-resilience thesis. But the supply shock hits fuel, transport, small firms and informal activity faster than it hits large formal PMI respondents. That creates a headline-versus-underlying divergence: formal PMIs can remain strong while the shock is already damaging household purchasing power, margins and informal employment absorption.

For policy, labor remains secondary. It does not justify easing because growth is still too resilient, and it does not generate wage-driven inflation pressure. It mainly limits how far RBI can lean hawkish if the inflation shock remains imported and supply-driven rather than domestically demand-led.

Inflation

Inflation is now a pipeline, expectations and policy-buffer problem — not simply a CPI-level problem. March CPI at 3.40% remains below the 4% midpoint and is not, by itself, a hawkish shock. But the trajectory has changed materially since January, and RBI’s own forward path is no longer benign: FY27 CPI is around 4.6%, with Q3 at 5.2% and core around 4.4%. Under the renewed 4% ±2% inflation-targeting framework for 2026–31, that Q3 path matters: it would sit well above the midpoint and close enough to the upper half of the tolerance band to make second-round effects a policy issue.

WPI is the visible shock channel. March WPI jumped to 3.88%, the strongest reading in more than three years, with crude petroleum / natural gas, fuel and power, manufactured products and food-related components all contributing. The signal is not demand overheating; it is an imported energy and supply-chain shock entering wholesale prices. PMI input costs remain elevated, and firms have only partially passed those costs through.

The crucial nuance is administered-price shielding. The government has chosen to absorb part of the oil shock through excise cuts, OMC under-recoveries and export duties rather than allowing retail fuel prices to adjust fully. Reported under-recoveries are large — around ₹26/litre on petrol and ₹81.90/litre on diesel, or roughly ₹2,400 crore per day combined. This weakens near-term CPI pass-through, but it creates a different macro risk: if oil stays high, pressure migrates into fiscal space, OMC losses, subsidy risk, capex trade-offs and INR / CAD vulnerability. The binding question is therefore not only “does WPI pass into CPI?” but “how long can buffers prevent pass-through without creating a larger macro-financial cost?”

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest RBI meeting was on 8 Apr 2026. The MPC held the repo rate at 5.25%, kept the standing deposit facility at 5.00%, the marginal standing facility / bank rate at 5.50%, and maintained a neutral stance. The policy signal was a supply-shock hold: growth was revised lower, inflation revised higher, and the governor framed the West Asia shock as a risk to input costs, energy, transport, insurance, FX stability and second-round inflation expectations.

Policy is restrictive, but the transmission problem is not conventional demand overheating. RBI is trying to avoid two mistakes: easing into an imported inflation / INR shock, and hiking aggressively into a supply shock that monetary policy cannot directly reverse. That argues for hold, liquidity and FX tools first, with rates used only if second-round effects, inflation expectations or INR pressure become persistent.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION: RBI stays on hold over the next 3–6 months, but the distribution is skewed toward prolonged hold / hawkish tail rather than delayed easing. A more dovish path requires oil to fall, INR to stabilize, April/May CPI to stay below or close to 4%, WPI to moderate, and PMI strength to prove front-loaded rather than demand-led. A more hawkish path requires sustained oil pressure, CPI crossing and staying above the 4% midpoint, INR weakness forcing broader pass-through, or evidence that inflation expectations are becoming embedded. The central bank is reacting most to second-round inflation risk, INR stability and external-financing pressure, not to growth alone.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is no longer trading a clean delayed-cut story. The market regime is prolonged hold with hawkish-tail risk, even if formal hikes are not the central expectation. The policy-rate distribution is wide: hold longer is the centre, liquidity tightening and higher effective money-market rates are the likely first tools, and outright hikes are a tail if oil, INR and CPI expectations reinforce each other.

The rates asymmetry depends on the oil / CPI / INR cluster. If CPI remains near or below 4%, WPI moderates and INR stabilizes, the front end can rebuild some easing premium later. If CPI moves into the upper half of the tolerance range, WPI remains high and INR stays near record lows, the market shifts further toward higher-for-longer, tighter liquidity and a higher inflation-risk premium. RBI’s Q3 FY27 CPI path near 5.2% is already a warning that the inflation problem is not only backward-looking.

INR is the weakest part of the macro setup. The currency has traded near record lows and has been under sustained pressure despite India’s growth premium. The flow picture is poor: FPI outflows were about $16.5bn in FY25-26, with a further $5.4bn in FY26-27 through 6 Apr, and April outflow pressure was large enough to challenge the prior full-year stress reference point. REER is also materially below its long-term average, around 92.7 versus 98.3, which creates a two-sided tension: the currency is cheap in real-effective terms, but cheap can stay cheap if oil, outflows and intervention constraints dominate.

RBI intervention should be read as volatility-curbing, not hard level-targeting. Net open-position caps, temporary NDF restrictions and subsequent partial rollback show active management of disorderly FX pressure, but not a commitment to defend one fixed INR level. That distinction matters: INR can still drift weaker if the move is orderly. The toolkit also has costs — RBI forward exposure, bank balance-sheet pressure and thinner INR liquidity. Financial conditions are therefore tightening through FX risk premium, external funding and hedging costs rather than through domestic credit collapse.

Political / Policy Events

There was no domestic political event that changed the regime through elections or institutional risk. The relevant policy development is fiscal and administrative: the government has chosen to shield households from the oil shock through fuel-tax cuts, OMC absorption and export duties. That is macro-relevant because it suppresses direct CPI pass-through while shifting the cost to public-sector balance sheets, fiscal space and potentially future capex allocation.

This policy shield is supportive for near-term CPI and household real income, but it complicates the medium-term scenario. If oil normalizes, it validates the buffer and prevents monetary overreaction. If oil stays high, the buffer becomes a fiscal risk and weakens the quality of growth by increasing the chance that resources move from investment / capex toward subsidies and price stabilization.

The external-policy channel is also live. West Asia matters to India through crude imports, fertilizer, remittances, transport and insurance costs. The shock is not just an oil price; it is a broader external terms-of-trade and supply-chain risk that feeds into INR, CAD, inflation expectations and RBI credibility.

5 Key Releases / Events

RBI Policy Decision — 8 Apr 2026
The reaction-function anchor. RBI held at 5.25%, kept a neutral stance, cut growth, lifted inflation and moved into explicit supply-shock risk management. The message was not dovish patience; it was wait-and-watch with second-round inflation risk.

RBI / Governor Communication — 21 Apr 2026
The clearest policy nuance. The governor framed second-round effects as the real risk: a supply shock can become embedded in the general price level if disruption persists. That shifts the regime toward soft-hawkish hold and makes inflation expectations central.

WPI Inflation — 15 Apr 2026
The key inflation shock. WPI rose to 3.88%, a 38-month high, with energy, fuel, manufactured products and food-related pressure. It does not force a CPI shock mechanically, but it confirms that upstream inflation is no longer benign.

HSBC Flash PMIs — 23 Apr 2026
The key growth-quality test. Manufacturing, services and composite all beat materially, but finished-goods stocks rose at the fastest pace since 2015 and the production surge partly reflected supply-disruption hedging. Growth resilience is real; demand quality remains less clean.

RBI FX Measures / INR Market Regime — 27 Mar to 21 Apr 2026
The FX-regime event. Net open-position caps, NDF restrictions and subsequent partial rollback show that INR pressure is being managed through direct tools as well as reserves and communication. The key point is not a hard peg; it is volatility management under structural oil / flow pressure.

ARC

Data window: 5 Jan 2026 to 16 Jan 2026
India opened with low CPI and first upstream caution. CPI at 1.33% left RBI with full patience, while WPI at 0.83% introduced the initial pipeline-risk signal without changing the reaction function.

Data window: 19 Jan 2026 to 6 Feb 2026
The perfect-hold regime was confirmed. PMIs accelerated, industrial production beat broadly, and RBI held unanimously while upgrading growth and keeping inflation projections benign. Strong growth and low CPI coexisted without forcing either cuts or hikes.

Data window: 16 Feb 2026 to 6 Mar 2026
Growth stayed exceptional, but the inflation pipeline worsened. Q3 GDP beat at 7.8%, PMIs remained strong and WPI accelerated again. The CPI/WPI divergence became the key forward risk.

Data window: 9 Mar 2026 to 20 Mar 2026
Inflation moved closer to the RBI target zone. CPI rose to 3.21%, WPI accelerated to 2.13%, and labor improved. The policy problem shifted from growth support to inflation-pipeline management.

Data window: 23 Mar 2026 to 3 Apr 2026
The external shock began to hit survey quality and FX conditions. March PMIs softened, domestic demand lost momentum, input costs rose sharply and INR pressure intensified. India stayed expansionary, but resilience became more expensive to defend.

Data window: 6 Apr 2026 to 24 Apr 2026
The regime became resilient growth under FX-stressed supply shock. RBI held, WPI surged, April PMIs rebounded with front-loading, and policy moved into active inflation-expectations / FX-volatility management. The macro mix is now hold-longer with hawkish tail, not easing-friendly hold.

Trigger Map

More dovish / easier policy / cuts pulled forward
Oil falls materially, Hormuz / supply-chain stress fades, INR stabilizes without heavy intervention, and April/May CPI stays below or close to 4%. WPI moderates quickly, OMC under-recoveries stop widening, and PMI strength proves mainly inventory front-loading rather than durable demand. Under this path, RBI can keep policy on hold now and reopen easing optionality later without losing inflation credibility.

Hold-longer / mildly hawkish reweighting
CPI crosses 4% and stays in the upper half of the target range, WPI remains elevated, input costs stay high and INR remains near record lows. RBI continues using FX and liquidity tools, but market pricing shifts further toward prolonged hold, tighter effective money-market conditions and a higher inflation-risk premium. This is the current central case.

Hawkish tail / formal tightening risk
Oil remains above the comfort zone, INR depreciation broadens imported inflation, current-account expectations deteriorate, and inflation expectations begin responding to the supply shock. CPI approaches the RBI’s projected upper-half path, second-round effects become visible, and liquidity / FX tools prove insufficient. Under this path, a 25–50bp hike or equivalent tightening through liquidity becomes live.

More constructive growth / risk-on scenario
April PMI strength proves durable rather than front-loaded, exports and domestic new orders remain firm, fiscal fuel shielding prevents CPI pass-through without materially damaging capex, and INR stabilizes. India then regains a cleaner growth-differential premium versus weaker EM peers, with rates stable and risk assets supported by growth rather than policy easing.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The dominant public framing broadly validates the move away from clean goldilocks. India is still resilient, but its relative-quality status is now contested rather than automatic: growth forecasts have been cut, equity allocation language has softened, inflation projections are higher, and INR has become the binding stress channel. April PMIs confirm that the economy is not breaking, while WPI, RBI communication and FX measures confirm that the inflation / currency constraint is no longer theoretical.

The key tension is whether the shock should be priced through monetary policy or through policy buffers. WPI and PMI input costs argue for higher pass-through risk, but fuel-tax cuts, OMC under-recoveries and administered-price decisions are deliberately suppressing the direct CPI channel. That makes CPI a lagged and policy-filtered variable rather than a pure market-price signal. The market can therefore remain split between a rates-hawkish reading of the inflation pipeline and a more benign view that fiscal absorption prevents the shock from becoming a monetary-policy problem.

The unresolved scenario breakers are April CPI, oil persistence, INR intervention intensity, portfolio flows and the durability of PMI demand. A CPI move above 4% with sustained INR weakness would push the regime closer to hawkish hold or formal tightening risk. A sharp oil reversal with contained CPI would restore some delayed-easing optionality. The biggest medium-term risk is that fiscal shielding protects near-term CPI but weakens the macro mix through OMC losses, subsidy pressure, capex diversion, higher hedging costs and a wider current account.

Bottom Line

India is still resilient, but the regime is more fragile, more FX-led and less clean than the earlier growth-quality story implied. Strong PMIs and investment-led resilience prevent a weak-growth easing call, while WPI, RBI inflation projections and INR stress prevent a benign hold narrative. The RBI base case is prolonged hold with liquidity / FX tools first, not rate cuts. The risk distribution is skewed toward hawkish tail if oil, INR and CPI expectations reinforce each other. INR is not a clean carry/growth expression here; it is a volatility-managed currency under oil / CAD / flow pressure, with real-effective undervaluation acting as a stabilizer only if external pressure fades.

Base case: resilient but downgraded growth, CPI buffered but rising, RBI hold-longer with hawkish tail, and INR vulnerable unless oil and external-flow pressure fade.
