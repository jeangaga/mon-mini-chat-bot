#### **Norway - CPI (Jan) - 08:00 CET** ‚ö†Ô∏è MAJOR SURPRISE
**Headline YoY:** +3.6% vs +3.1% poll | Prior: +3.2%

**Commentary:** Inflation accelerated to 3.6% YoY (highest in 4 months), well above 3.1% expected. MoM surge of 0.6% (vs 0.1% prior) marks largest monthly gain since late July. Core inflation jumped to 3.4% YoY with MoM at +0.3% against -0.1% consensus - a 40bp miss indicating broad-based pressure. Sectoral breakdown shows housing/utilities accelerated to 4.3% (vs 3.9%), transport spiked to 4.4% (vs 2.7%), restaurants/accommodation at 5.8% (vs 5.1%). Food moderated to 4.2% (vs 5.3%). SSB data shows labor market cooling with vacancies down 5.1% QoQ to 85,000 in Q4 2025. Norges Bank stated in January that "core inflation has been around 3% since autumn 2024, which is now a fairly long period" and maintained December forecast of 1-2 cuts in 2026. Post-data, Nomura revised to expect ONLY ONE cut in December 2026 (vs prior call for more), citing sticky wage growth.

**HF Take:** **MAJOR HAWKISH SHOCK.** This completely changes Norges Bank calculus. Headline +50bp miss (3.6% vs 3.1%), core +40bp miss (3.4% vs 3.0%), and critically the MoM core at +0.3% vs -0.1% expected is a 40bp surprise indicating this isn't statistical noise - it's broad-based acceleration. The combination of: (1) resilient mainland GDP (+0.4% QoQ), (2) accelerating inflation (3.6% highest in 4 months), (3) sticky core (3.4%), and (4) services inflation running 5.8% removes ALL easing urgency. Market had been pricing gradual cuts based on December guidance (1-2 in 2026); this forces immediate repricing. Nomura's revision to only one cut in Dec 2026 is the new baseline. Some analysts (Handelsbanken, DNB Carnegie, SEB) still calling for June cut to 3.75% followed by pause, but this data argues for Mar hold, Jun hold, and potential hike discussion if next prints don't moderate. NOK strengthening materially on this. **Critical for DM divergence trade** - if ECB cutting in March and Fed cutting later in 2026 while Norges Bank holds or hikes, NOK becomes carry trade destination. This is a regime shift for Norway monetary policy - from "gradual easing" to "wait and see" or potentially "one and done hold."

#### **United States - Retail Sales (Dec) - 14:30 EST** ‚ö†Ô∏è MAJOR MISS
**Headline MoM:** 0.0% vs +0.4% poll | Prior: +0.6%  
**Ex-Autos MoM:** 0.0% vs +0.3% poll | Prior: +0.4% (rev)  
**Retail Control:** -0.1% vs +0.4% poll | Prior: +0.2% (rev)

**Commentary:** Significant deceleration in consumer spending entering 2026. Headline flat vs +0.4% expected and +0.6% prior; ex-autos flat vs +0.3% expected; critically, the control group (used for GDP) contracted -0.1% vs +0.4% expected - a 50bp miss and rare for this stable series. November revised down (ex-autos from +0.6% to +0.4%). Circana data shows combined five weeks ending Jan 3: revenue flat, unit demand -1%. By segment: food/beverage revenue +2% (units flat), non-edible CPG dollars +1% (units -3%), discretionary merchandise dollars -2% (units -5%). Full year 2025: 2% dollar growth, flat units. Consumer survey shows "bought on impulse" fell to <20% for most categories - lowest on record. Top purchase reason was self-gifting, not impulse. New product SKUs now 2% of discretionary sales vs 5% historical norm (same as pandemic lows). Price elevation curtailing purchases despite continued spending.

**HF Take:** This is structural, not cyclical. The control group contraction is critical - feeds directly into GDP consumption calculations, putting Q1 2026 estimates at risk. Atlanta Fed GDPNow will revise lower. But deeper issue: impulse buying collapsed to <20% of purchases (vs historical norms much higher) and new product penetration at pandemic emergency levels (2% vs 5%). This indicates exhausted consumers prioritizing necessities over discretionary. The fact this occurred during holiday shopping season ("busiest shopping periods") is the red flag - if consumers won't impulse-buy during holidays, when will they? Unit sales diverging from dollar sales (-1% units vs flat dollars) confirms price elevation driving the wedge. Consumers spending what they must but buying less. Combined with ECI wage moderation (+0.7% vs +0.8%), this points to weakening consumption into 2026. If Thursday CPI confirms disinflation, Fed has clear justification to cut - weak consumption + moderating wages + low imported inflation + disinflation = dovish cocktail. USD bearish, rates rally scenario building.
