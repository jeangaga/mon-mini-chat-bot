A. CAD WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

CAD moved from conditional resilience into a stagflation-light / BoC-look-through regime, with the Bank of Canada scout reinforcing the dual-direction reaction function but creating a material CPI reconciliation issue against the existing CAD release archive. The BoC’s 29 April hold at 2.25% was explicitly nimble: oil persistence could require hikes, while US trade/tariff deterioration could require cuts. Macklem’s framework was not dovish; it was conditional, with “consecutive increases” preserved as a hawkish tail if higher oil became generalized inflation.

The in-week macro tape still argues for look-through more than hawkish repricing. Labour weakened, CFIB confidence collapsed, real retail volumes fell, and the release archive’s CPI block showed softer BoC core / median / trim despite headline gasoline pressure. Producer prices then complicated the dovish read through strong IPPI. The new BoC scout strengthens the dovish interpretation of the central-bank layer by arguing that April CPI materially undershot the MPR and that preferred core measures reached five-year lows, but its CPI figures conflict with the release archive’s CPI block. Until reconciled, final CAD regime should be treated as: BoC dual-direction optionality with easing bias rising, but not a clean cut signal unless the CPI discrepancy is resolved in favour of the scout’s much softer inflation read.

B. CAD MACRO SIGNAL SCOREBOARD

Growth: ~
Supporting evidence:
GDP rose 0.2% m/m in February, manufacturing PMI jumped to 53.3, trade swung to a C$1.78bn surplus, Ivey SA rose to 57.7, wholesale trade rose 1.9%, housing starts beat at 279.3k and building permits rose 10.3%. But the quality weakened: trade was energy/gold-led, manufacturing sales were petroleum/coal-heavy, retail volumes fell despite nominal sales strength, and CFIB confidence collapsed to 46.26. This is the cleanest cyclical deterioration input in the CAD tape and gives the BoC a slack buffer against energy-driven inflation pass-through.

Inflation: ~ / DATA CONFLICT
Supporting evidence:
The existing CAD release archive shows April CPI as a softer-underlying but not collapsed inflation print: headline CPI rose 2.8% y/y, below the 3.1% poll, while BoC core fell to 2.1%, median to 2.1% and trim to 2.0%. Producer prices then rose 2.0% m/m after March was revised hotter to 2.8%, creating a producer-consumer wedge. The new BoC scout, however, states April headline CPI was 1.7% y/y and that all BoC preferred core measures were at five-year lows, dramatically below the April MPR’s projected near-3% peak. This discrepancy must be reconciled before upgrading CAD to a clean disinflation regime. Directionally, both versions support BoC look-through; the disagreement is magnitude.

Financial Conditions: ~
Supporting evidence:
BoC held at 2.25%, building permits and housing starts partially offset prior construction weakness, and wholesale / PMI data reduced near-term recession fear. But labour slack, CFIB collapse and falling real retail volumes argue against tight financial demand conditions. CAD financial conditions are therefore two-sided: not recessionary enough for a clean easing impulse, not strong enough for hawkish repricing.

Policy Constraint: ~ / easing bias rising
Supporting evidence:
BoC’s 29 April decision was explicitly nimble and two-sided: oil persistence is hike risk; trade / tariff drag is cut risk. The subsequent release archive increasingly favoured look-through: labour weakened, core CPI cooled and real retail volumes fell. The BoC scout reinforces this by documenting the MPR / deliberations framework and arguing that April CPI falsified the hawkish-tail inflation scenario. But because the scout’s CPI level conflicts with the release archive, the safest read is not “June cut locked”; it is “hold base case weakened, cut probability rising, hawkish oil scenario much harder unless May CPI / IPPI pass-through reaccelerates.”

Canada — BoC Rate Decision (Apr / 30 Apr ref date)
Release Date: 29 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 15:45
Importance: ****

Reuters Data

BoC Rate Decision: 2.25% | Prior: 2.25% | Poll: 2.25% | Surprise: 0.000%

Professional Market Commentary

The Bank of Canada held the overnight rate at 2.25%, with the Bank Rate at 2.5% and the deposit rate at 2.20%. The official statement framed Canada as caught between two external risks: higher oil prices from the Middle East conflict and trade-policy uncertainty with the US. Macklem’s opening statement emphasized that policy has been held at 2.25% since October and that changes can be small if the base case holds, but that uncertainty is unusually elevated and policy may need to be nimble.

The scout tape captures the market debate well: most Canadian houses treat the decision as a steady/nimble hold, while the market shifted more hawkish intraday as oil risk lifted the probability of later hikes.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a two-sided hold, not a dovish hold.

The BoC is not pushing an easing story because the inflation forecast has moved up with gasoline and oil. But it is also not validating an unconditional hike path because trade uncertainty and tariff risk remain downside-growth shocks.

The important distinction versus the BoE is symmetry. The BoE has an explicit internal hike dissent; the BoC has an explicit conditional framework. Oil persistence is hike risk; sharper trade restriction is cut risk.

State transition: continuation

Context from prior regional live file: Canada’s prior DM sequence was already a stagflationary survey mix, with soft activity indicators and firmer cost pressure. The BoC hold extends that regime rather than resolving it. Canada remains a nimble-hold case where the next move depends on whether oil inflation or trade drag becomes dominant.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

CAD is not a clean directional policy trade from the decision alone.

The rate path is now explicitly contingent: April/May CPI can push the market toward H2 hike risk, while CUSMA/tariff deterioration can move it toward cut optionality. The near-term market risk is that swaps over-price the oil channel if core breadth does not follow gasoline higher.
