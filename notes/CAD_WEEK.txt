A. CAD WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

Canada’s three-week tape resolved into a hard-hold / mixed-growth / pipeline-inflation regime. Activity weakened first through live and rate-sensitive channels: Ivey SA fell below 50, the leading index slowed sharply, building permits dropped materially, and housing starts then confirmed an actual construction slowdown. But the weakness was not synchronized: February manufacturing and wholesale sales rebounded, small-business confidence partially recovered from the March trough, employment stabilized after heavy prior losses, and February retail spending remained resilient, albeit with modest real-volume quality. The growth signal was therefore softer than the headline consumer/goods data implied, but not weak enough to force a BoC growth-response narrative.

The inflation signal stayed two-sided. March CPI delivered a classic energy-over-core split: headline inflation rose on gasoline, while ex-gasoline CPI slowed, median held, trim eased, and BoC core m/m softened. But that look-through comfort was challenged by still-firm wage growth and a sharp producer-price shock, with industrial product prices up 2.4% m/m and raw-material prices up 12.0% m/m. The resulting CAD regime is BoC hard hold: consumer core still lets the Bank look through the initial energy shock, but producer-cost and wage channels make May CPI/pass-through monitoring decisive. CAD is not getting a clean growth-positive impulse, but it is also not receiving a clean dovish-rate release.
