A. EUR WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

The week completed the bloc’s move into a stagflation-warning regime. Growth weakened in both surveys and hard demand data: euro-area sentiment deteriorated, German and French household demand disappointed, France’s industrial base stayed soft, Spain lost some of its resilience, and labor moved one step weaker as euro-area unemployment ticked up. At the same time, March inflation re-accelerated meaningfully in Germany and at the euro-area aggregate, with France also moving higher, leaving the ECB with weaker activity but no clean inflation relief. The manufacturing rebound did not overturn that diagnosis because much of the headline strength was quality-poor, flattered by delivery-time distortion, front-loading, and cost pressure rather than broad clean demand. The resulting weekly regime is slower demand, softer labor, renewed headline inflation, and a materially tighter ECB constraint.

B. EUR MACRO SIGNAL SCOREBOARD

Growth: -
Supporting evidence:
The week’s growth signal deteriorated across both hard data and surveys. Euro-area economic sentiment fell to 96.6 from 98.3, Spain retail sales slowed to 2.2% y/y from 4.0%, German retail sales fell 0.6% m/m against expectations for a rebound, French consumer spending dropped 1.4% m/m, and French industrial output fell 0.7% m/m. The PMI layer did not rescue the picture: Spain manufacturing fell back into contraction at 48.7, France stagnated at 50.0, and the euro-area 51.6 headline was explicitly quality-poor rather than a clean recovery signal.

Labor: -
Supporting evidence:
Labor is still far from a recessionary break, but the weekly direction turned weaker. Germany unemployment stayed at 6.3% and avoided a fresh shock, yet the file treats that stability as poor-quality inertia rather than strength. Italy’s unemployment rate rose to 5.3% from a revised 5.2%, and the euro-area unemployment rate rose to 6.2% from 6.1%, the first move up from the prior record low. The bucket therefore shifts from “softening but intact” toward “peak-and-turning.”

Inflation: -
Supporting evidence:
Headline inflation re-accelerated sharply enough to harden the regional policy problem. Germany HICP rose to 2.8% y/y from 2.0%, France HICP rose to 1.9% from 1.1%, and euro-area HICP rose to 2.5% from 1.9%, driven by an energy reversal, while core eased only slightly to 2.3%. Italy remained a softer node at 1.5% HICP y/y, and February PPI/import-price releases in France, Italy, and Germany still pointed to incomplete rather than generalized pipeline overheating. The week’s inflation message is therefore energy-led re-acceleration, not clean domestic core rebreak, but that is still enough to worsen the ECB bind materially.

Financial Conditions: ~
Supporting evidence:
The supplied file does not contain a direct market-financial-conditions release, so the week did not resolve this bucket cleanly. Indirectly, conditions worsened at the macro level: euro-area sentiment and confidence deteriorated, selling-price expectations rose, and the PMI sequence showed renewed cost pressure, delivery delays, and weaker confidence. But without direct credit, rates, lending, or market-stress evidence inside the tape, the better final read is worsening backdrop rather than a decisively broken financial-conditions signal.

Policy Constraint: -
Supporting evidence:
This was the clearest weekly conclusion. Germany’s March CPI/HICP re-acceleration and euro-area headline HICP back above target removed any easy dovish-growth reading of the slowdown, while softer core prevented a mechanically hawkish conclusion. The Commission survey package also showed weaker confidence alongside worse price expectations, and the PMI releases reinforced a supply-shock mix of higher costs and weaker demand. The ECB therefore ends the week with a tighter, more structural policy bind: growth is weakening, labor is no longer improving, but inflation is not easing in a way that would reopen a simple relief path.
