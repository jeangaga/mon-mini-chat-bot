SEK Macro Note — Low-Core Recovery, Hawkish Communication, Faded 2026 Hike Credibility

As of date: 2 July 2026
Data window: 27 Apr 2026 to 2 Jul 2026
Horizon: 3–6 months
Macro frame: 4-week impulse filter → medium-horizon SEK / Riksbank / EURSEK scenario update

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ conditional low-inflation recovery; domestic demand still soft
Labor: ~ slack buffer; not a hike trigger
Inflation: ↓ underlying inflation muted; headline rebound fuel / electricity-led
Financial cond: ~ external-rate differential tighter, domestic impulse still soft
Policy bias: Hold-first; hawkish communication, but 2026 hike credibility faded
Political risk: ~ external energy shock fading in price terms; Hormuz still not fully cleared

Executive Summary

Sweden remains a hold-first, low-underlying-inflation regime, but the Riksbank has shifted the communication layer hawkish to manage inflation expectations, SEK pass-through and wage-bargaining risk. The June MPR lifted the rate path and raised the probability of a 2026 hike, but the public market tape has not validated that as a high-conviction tightening scenario. The better regime description is not “Q4 hike now live as base-case tail”; it is hawkish signalling against a market still biased to fade 2026 tightening unless SEK, price plans or wages deteriorate.

The last 4 weeks changed the policy optics, not the domestic macro base. May CPIF rose to 1.5% and CPIF ex-energy to 0.5%, but the rebound was fuel / electricity-led and did not show broad underlying inflation. Growth improved at the margin through April GDP and production-value data, but the quality remains weak: Q1 GDP contracted, retail sales flattened, confidence stayed below normal, and the industrial rebound was value-based / export-supported rather than a clean domestic-demand upswing.

The binding constraint is now forward inflation management versus current low-core reality. The Riksbank’s forecast profile is internally asymmetric: near-term inflation is soft, while 2027–28 inflation was revised materially higher. That is a persistence / expectations-management bet, not a conventional overheating signal. The June minutes sharpen the point: Thedéen and Seim are the hawkish core, while Bunge, Hjelm and Jansson remain more cautious; Jansson and Hjelm could have accepted an unchanged path. That makes the June hawkish path a communication device with limited committee depth.

For SEK, the dominant driver remains external. EURSEK is still more sensitive to ECB–Riksbank spreads, global risk, energy premium and SEK pass-through than to domestic Swedish activity alone. SEK can repair if Sweden delivers low-inflation stabilization and energy risk continues to fade, but a Riksbank hike premium is not a clean SEK support unless EURSEK weakness itself becomes the policy trigger.

Growth / Activity

Growth is better described as a conditional low-inflation recovery than as outright weakness, but the domestic side is still not strong enough to justify a tightening story. Q1 GDP final confirmed a -0.2% q/q contraction, April retail sales were flat after downward revision, and consumer confidence remained below normal. Households remain the weak pocket.

The offset is that Sweden is not rolling over. April GDP indicator rose 0.5% m/m, private production improved, and industrial production value rebounded sharply. That reduces hard-landing risk and supports the idea that the weak Q1 base did not extend into a straight-line downturn. The problem is signal quality: the GDP indicator is preliminary, and the industrial rebound was value-based, price-flattered and export-led. Domestic orders and household demand have not yet confirmed a broad recovery.

The 3–6 month growth case is therefore stabilization with low inflation, not reflation. Housing expectations, mortgage-rule easing and lower measured inflation can cushion households, but the confidence / retail mix still argues for caution. A cleaner SEK growth story requires confirmation in real production volumes, retail, credit, housing transactions and household confidence. Without that, growth caps the Riksbank’s willingness to turn the June path into action.

Labor / Employment

Labor is a slack buffer. The latest usable labor anchor remains April unemployment at 8.7% NSA and 8.6% smoothed / seasonally adjusted. The level is elevated, but the direction was not deteriorating. There was no fresh labor-market shock in the latest impulse window.

That matters because the Riksbank’s hawkish communication is not being driven by current labor overheating. Slack gives the dovish side of the Board a reason to resist pre-emptive tightening. It also reduces the probability that energy and SEK pass-through automatically turn into wage-price persistence.

The forward labor channel is still important through collective bargaining. Thedéen’s June communication is partly aimed at shaping wage expectations before the bargaining round. That makes wage settlements less a current-data trigger and more a medium-horizon reaction-function variable. If wage demands absorb higher expected inflation or firms’ price plans harden, the June path tilt becomes more credible. If wage signals stay contained, the Riksbank’s hawkish message looks more like expectation management than an actual hiking path.

Inflation

Inflation remains the key reason Sweden is not a clean hawkish case. May CPIF rebounded to 1.5% and CPIF ex-energy to 0.5%, but the underlying profile is still very low. The rebound was driven by fuel and electricity reversal, not a broad services / wage / domestic-demand inflation impulse.

The Riksbank’s problem is that realized inflation and forecast inflation are pulling in opposite directions. Current CPIF ex-energy is far below target, and professional money-market expectations have reportedly softened. But firms’ price plans and the Business Survey point to some second-round cost pressure from the Iran / energy shock, while the Riksbank’s medium-term forecasts embed a sharp inflation re-acceleration in 2027–28. That creates a real but unproven inflation-persistence risk.

The inflation regime is therefore low current core with contested forward broadening. It is wrong to read May inflation as a hawkish confirmation, but it is also too easy to dismiss the Riksbank’s concern: the Bank is trying to prevent an external shock from entering price plans, SEK import prices and wage bargaining. The next decisive inflation test is not headline CPIF; it is whether CPIF ex-energy, services, firms’ price plans and wage settlements start validating the 2027 persistence story.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Riksbank decision was the 17 Jun 2026 MPR. The Executive Board held the policy rate at 1.75%, with the decision applying from 24 Jun. The rate path was lifted and the probability of a hike later in 2026 was raised. The policy signal was hawkish relative to March, but not because current inflation or activity were strong. The Riksbank cut near-term inflation assumptions while raising the 2027–28 inflation profile, creating a forward-persistence argument.

The reaction function is now best read as hold-first with hawkish communication optionality, not an active tightening cycle. The committee split matters: Thedéen and Seim are the hawkish anchor; Bunge, Hjelm and Jansson remain more cautious; Jansson and Hjelm could have accepted an unchanged rate path. That weakens the probability that the path is mechanically delivered unless incoming data force the dovish bloc to move.

The Riksbank appears to be waiting for four things: whether CPIF ex-energy broadens, whether firms’ price plans feed into services, whether wage bargaining absorbs higher inflation expectations, and whether SEK weakness becomes persistent enough to import inflation. The ECB is now a real external anchor after its June hike to 2.25%, but the Riksbank still needs Swedish inflation transmission to justify following it.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is hold at 1.75%, with no delivered 2026 hike unless SEK weakness, price plans, wages or core inflation force the Board to validate the June path. The policy path becomes more dovish if energy remains lower, CPIF ex-energy stays near 0.5%, professional expectations soften, and activity remains below forecast; in that case the hike tail is priced out further and a cut tail can re-emerge. The path becomes more hawkish if EURSEK breaks higher, ECB tightening widens the spread pressure, firms’ price plans broaden, wage bargaining hardens, or CPIF ex-energy moves materially toward target. The Riksbank is reacting most to expectations management and SEK pass-through, not to current domestic overheating.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The Swedish front end should trade hold-first with fadeable hike premium. The June path keeps a tightening tail alive, but market reaction and institutional views are not validating a strong 2026 hike story. The curve is still vulnerable to pricing out hikes if energy continues to normalize and CPIF ex-energy stays weak.

Rates repricing now depends less on the Riksbank’s published path and more on whether the data force the dovish bloc to accept it. A firmer CPIF ex-energy / services sequence, higher price plans and SEK weakness keep the front end supported. Lower oil, soft core inflation and falling professional expectations flatten the path. The clean rate-market tension is that the Riksbank wants to preserve hawkish expectations, while the current inflation and growth data argue for patience.

For FX, SEK is not a clean Riksbank-hike currency. EURSEK remains driven by ECB–Riksbank spread pressure, global risk appetite, energy premium and SEK pass-through. A hawkish Riksbank path can support SEK only if markets believe it constrains EURSEK upside; if the path is faded, SEK needs lower energy, stronger growth quality or better global risk to repair.

Financial conditions are mixed. Domestic conditions are not tight enough to force cuts: housing expectations have improved, mortgage-rule easing supports households, and low inflation improves real income optics. But conditions are not strong enough to validate hikes: confidence is weak, retail has flattened, and the industrial rebound is not a clean domestic-volume impulse. The ECB spread is the main external tightening channel, not Swedish demand.

Political / Policy Events

The external political shock has moved from acute inflation threat toward fading market premium but unresolved physical-risk tail. Brent has round-tripped sharply lower from the April peak and WTI moved below $70 by late June, which undercuts the energy-persistence leg of the Riksbank’s hawkish path. But Hormuz normalization is not fully confirmed in the stricter macro sense: market pricing has moved, while convoy-scale commercial-transit restoration and insurance normalization remain less certain.

Domestic political risk is not the core regime driver, but the September 2026 election is a soft constraint on aggressive Riksbank tightening. It does not prevent a hike if inflation persistence becomes clear, but it raises the bar for bold hawkish delivery in a weak-demand, low-core environment. The more relevant election risk is fiscal: a fiscally expansive outcome would complicate the inflation path without necessarily improving current activity quality.

Policy transmission still runs through VAT / fiscal effects, mortgage regulation, ECB spillover and SEK pass-through. Food VAT and temporary fiscal measures distort the inflation signal. Mortgage easing cushions households. ECB tightening increases relative-rate pressure on SEK. The Riksbank’s challenge is to keep inflation expectations anchored without tightening into a domestic economy that has not yet delivered broad recovery.

The central policy event. The Bank held at 1.75% but lifted the rate path and raised the probability of a later-2026 hike. The signal is hawkish communication, not an active tightening cycle unless incoming data validate the medium-term inflation forecast.

The key reaction-function clarification. Thedéen / Seim remain the hawkish core, while Bunge / Hjelm / Jansson are more cautious; Jansson and Hjelm could have accepted an unchanged path. This materially lowers confidence that the June path is mechanically deliverable.

3. Sweden CPI / CPIF / CPIF ex-energy — 11 Jun 2026
The hard-data anchor against the hawkish path. CPIF at 1.5% and CPIF ex-energy at 0.5% confirm low underlying inflation, while the monthly rebound was fuel / electricity-led rather than broad domestic inflation.

4. Riksbank Business Survey / price-plan signal — May / June 2026
The main hawkish counterweight. Firms’ price plans and cost pressure linked to the Iran / energy shock provide the Riksbank with a second-round-risk justification even while current core inflation remains muted.

Defines the growth tension. Q1 contraction and flat retail keep domestic demand soft, while April GDP and production reduce hard-landing risk. The rebound is stabilizing but not yet high-quality enough to support a tightening cycle.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The dominant market narrative broadly confirms the hold-first regime but is more skeptical of 2026 tightening than the Riksbank’s June path. The June MPR lifted the rate path and signalled a higher probability of a later-year hike, but the institutional baseline is still no delivered 2026 hike unless SEK, wages or price plans force the issue. Market pricing is treating the path as a hawkish communication tool, not as a high-conviction policy promise: the front end has room to price out hikes, SEK did not receive durable support from the decision, and EURSEK remains driven primarily by ECB–Riksbank spreads, global risk beta, energy premium and SEK pass-through. The rates / FX asymmetry is therefore: Swedish rates are capped by low core inflation and soft activity, while SEK remains vulnerable if ECB tightening or EURSEK weakness forces the Riksbank to defend credibility.

The real debate is the gap between current inflation and forward inflation management. The low-core camp has strong evidence: CPIF ex-energy is only 0.5%, May’s rebound was fuel / electricity-led, domestic demand is soft, professional expectations are not de-anchoring upward, and energy prices have fallen sharply since the acute Hormuz peak. The hawkish camp has a narrower but real mechanism: the Riksbank’s 2027–28 inflation forecast was revised materially higher, the Business Survey flags price-plan pressure from Iran-war costs, the ECB has already moved above the Riksbank, and wage bargaining gives Thedéen a reason to keep the communication channel hawkish. The committee split is central: Thedéen and Seim can keep the message alive, but Bunge, Hjelm and Jansson limit the probability of delivery unless the forward indicators harden.

The scenario breakers are now concentrated in four places. First, CPIF ex-energy / services / price plans must show whether broadening is real or only a forecast risk. Second, wage bargaining will decide whether the Riksbank’s hawkish communication has traction in the domestic nominal process. Third, EURSEK around the upper end of the recent range is the market trigger that could turn a low-core economy into an imported-inflation problem. Fourth, Hormuz / oil normalization is already weakening the energy-persistence case, but a confirmed physical reopening and sustained Brent below the stress range would further flatten the path; a renewed energy spike would revive the Riksbank’s insurance logic. The August Riksbank meeting, Q3 expectations data, ECB July / September decisions, and the September election are the key gates for whether the regime remains hold-first, shifts dovish, or hardens into credibility defence.

Bottom Line

Sweden is a low-core, weak-domestic-demand economy with a hawkish Riksbank communication overlay. The June path tilt matters, but the market is fading a delivered 2026 hike because current inflation, growth and energy-price dynamics do not yet validate it. The hawkish risk is not domestic overheating; it is SEK pass-through, ECB spread pressure, firms’ price plans and wage-bargaining expectations. SEK is still mainly an external beta / rate-differential / energy-premium currency, with domestic stabilization only a secondary support.

Base case: Riksbank holds at 1.75% through 2026; the 2026 hike tail survives as a credibility-management option, but delivery requires SEK weakness plus clear price-plan / wage / core-inflation broadening.

SEK Macro Note — Hold-First Riksbank, Dovish Data Lean, External-Risk Veto

As of date: 27 May 2026
Data window: 5 Jan 2026 to 22 May 2026, with final public-tape integration through late May
Horizon: 3–6 months
Macro frame: 4-week impulse filter → medium-horizon SEK / Riksbank / EURSEK scenario update

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ fragile stabilization, not clean reacceleration
Labor: ~ elevated slack, non-deteriorating
Inflation: ↓ materially soft; broader than VAT, but still partly distorted
Financial cond: ~ mortgage / fiscal cushion easing the shock, external risk still dominant
Policy bias: Hold-first, dovish data lean, hawkish tail via energy / SEK / expectations
Political risk: ~ external energy / Hormuz shock is regime-relevant; domestic fiscal policy cushions

Executive Summary

Sweden remains a hold-first macro regime, but the balance has shifted more dovish than a purely symmetric optionality framing. The Riksbank is still not validating cuts, because the external energy shock, SEK pass-through and expectations channel remain live. But the recent data mix is clearly below the March policy path: inflation has surprised down repeatedly, activity was weaker at the start of the year, and April CPIF was not only a VAT / food artefact. Services and electricity weakness mean underlying inflation is softer than a purely mechanical reading would imply.

The growth signal is mixed rather than recessionary. Q1 GDP contracted, consumer confidence remains soft, and industrial production weakened, but March GDP, retail sales, housing expectations, manufacturing PMI and private production preserve a stabilization floor. The household channel deserves more weight than before: Sweden’s high variable-rate mortgage exposure and the April mortgage-rule loosening make the rates / housing transmission fast, while discretionary fiscal measures cushion the energy shock and reduce the need for immediate monetary easing.

The binding constraint is now the gap between professional-market credibility and household / FX pass-through risk. Five-year CPIF expectations at 2.0% support Riksbank patience, but household inflation expectations are much higher and SEK weakness remains the route through which imported inflation can become policy-relevant. The base case is hold at 1.75% through the summer, with a dovish bias in the data but an external-risk veto on cuts. SEK is not trading as a pure domestic credibility currency; EURSEK remains mainly a rates-differential / equity-beta / energy-risk cross, with domestic Swedish stabilization acting as a secondary support.

Growth / Activity

Activity is mixed, with a fragile floor rather than a clean recovery. Q1 GDP contracted by 0.2% q/q and annual growth slowed to 1.6%, breaking the earlier smooth-recovery narrative. Consumer confidence remains below normal, and industrial production weakened despite strong manufacturing PMI readings. That combination prevents a strong-growth SEK narrative.

The offset is that Sweden is not rolling over. March GDP rose 1.9% m/m, retail sales rebounded 3.1% m/m and 6.2% y/y, private-sector production rose 1.1% m/m, housing expectations improved, and PMI stayed firmly expansionary. The quality of that signal is uneven: PMI strength is partly delivery-time / supply-chain contaminated, and the industrial-production decline argues against treating the PMI as pure demand acceleration.

The more important 3–6 month point is that Sweden has several stabilizers working at once. Lower inflation supports real incomes, fiscal measures cushion the energy shock, and housing transmission is fast because Swedish mortgages are highly rate-sensitive. Mortgage-rule easing adds a parallel domestic easing impulse. That does not create a boom; it reduces downside convexity. Sweden’s growth regime is therefore fragile stabilization with household / housing upside optionality, not broad reflation.

Labor / Employment

Labor is a slack buffer. The April unemployment rate at 8.7% NSA and 8.6% seasonally adjusted / smoothed confirms that slack remains elevated, while the direction is no longer deteriorating. Earlier headline unemployment jumps were less alarming once adjusted / smoothed data were considered.

This matters for the Riksbank because elevated slack lowers the probability that imported energy or SEK weakness automatically becomes a wage-price spiral. Sweden does not have the same domestic capacity problem as the more hawkish DM cases. But labor is not weak enough to force urgent cuts. The policy signal is permissive, not decisive: labor allows patience and keeps the inflation-risk tail conditional.

Wages remain the missing transmission variable. If wage settlements stay contained, the Riksbank can continue looking through temporary energy and VAT effects. If wage growth or services pay dynamics reaccelerate, the elevated household inflation-expectations channel becomes more relevant and the hawkish tail becomes more credible.

Inflation

Inflation is the clearest dovish impulse, and the softness is broader than a pure VAT story. April CPIF fell to 0.8% y/y and CPIF ex-energy fell to 0.0%, while the print was well below the Riksbank’s March forecast. Food / VAT mechanics are still central, but the downside also included electricity and services. That makes the April print more policy-relevant than a simple tax-distortion dismissal.

The sequence also matters. Sweden has produced repeated downside inflation surprises versus consensus and the Riksbank forecast. March CPIF ex-energy was already soft, and April extended the weakness. Stripping out the tax effect, underlying inflation still looks below target rather than close to 2%. That raises the probability that the next Riksbank move, if the energy shock fades, is a cut rather than a hike.

The offset is expectations and pass-through. Professional / money-market five-year CPIF expectations at 2.0% are the key credibility anchor, but household expectations are much higher and cannot be ignored. The Riksbank does not target household expectations mechanically, but a high household inflation-expectations backdrop matters if SEK weakens, energy stays elevated, or firms regain pricing power. Inflation is therefore low enough to kill near-term hawkishness, but not clean enough to activate cuts while the external shock is unresolved.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Riksbank meeting was on 7 May 2026. The Riksbank held the policy rate at 1.75%, a fifth consecutive hold. The decision acknowledged that both inflation and activity had come in weaker than expected, while preserving the warning that the external supply shock could still generate a broad and persistent inflation upturn.

The reaction function is not perfectly symmetric. The committee statement leaned more dovish than March because the data were weaker, but Thedéen’s pre-meeting framework kept the upside-inflation tail explicit. The practical policy mix is therefore: dovish data, hawkish external-risk veto. Current policy is restrictive enough to keep domestic inflation below target, but not being eased because the Bank wants the June MPR, May CPIF detail, expectations data, ECB path and Hormuz / energy trajectory before re-opening the cutting debate.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is hold at 1.75% through the summer, with a dovish data lean but no near-term cut unless the external shock fades. The path becomes more dovish if May/June CPIF confirms services and electricity softness beyond VAT, energy prices normalize, SEK stabilizes, unemployment weakens on adjusted data, and 5-year expectations remain anchored. The path becomes more hawkish only if elevated energy prices persist, SEK weakness feeds into import / services prices, household expectations bleed into wage and pricing behavior, or ECB tightening pressure forces a relative-policy response. The Riksbank is reacting most to inflation expectations, SEK pass-through and persistence risk, not to headline CPIF alone.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The Swedish front end should trade hold-first with residual cut optionality, not active hike pricing. The April inflation undershoot and weaker activity cap hawkish repricing. The external energy / SEK / expectations channel caps aggressive cut pricing. The near-term curve is therefore dominated by the June MPR and by whether May CPIF confirms that underlying softness is broader than tax effects.

Rates repricing depends on three gates: inflation quality, SEK pass-through and ECB spillover. A clean services / core undershoot with easing energy prices pulls the cut tail forward. Persistent energy pressure, a weaker krona or a more hawkish ECB pushes toward hold-longer, with only limited insurance-hike risk.

For FX, SEK-specific credibility helps, but it is not the main driver. EURSEK is still primarily a regional rates-differential, European risk and global-equity-beta cross. Sweden’s anchored professional expectations and soft inflation prevent domestic panic, but they do not automatically produce SEK strength. SEK support requires either a calmer external backdrop, better risk appetite, or confirmation that Sweden can run low inflation without weak growth. SEK vulnerability rises if EURSEK breaks higher on energy / risk-off while the Riksbank becomes worried about import-price pass-through.

Financial conditions are more nuanced than the earlier note implied. Housing sentiment is improving, mortgage transmission is fast because of variable-rate exposure, and mortgage-rule loosening adds a parallel easing impulse. Fiscal policy is also cushioning the shock through discretionary support and tax measures. Domestic conditions are therefore not tight enough to force cuts, but not loose enough to create overheating.

Political / Policy Events

Domestic politics is not the main regime driver through election or institutional risk. The relevant political / policy channels are fiscal cushioning, mortgage regulation and the external energy shock.

The fiscal stance is doing more work than a pure monetary-policy read would imply. Defence spending, tax reductions, fuel / electricity support and food VAT effects all soften the hit from the external shock. This helps explain why the Riksbank can wait: fiscal policy and tax mechanics are cushioning the growth and inflation shock simultaneously.

Mortgage policy also matters. The loosening of mortgage rules raises the loan-to-value ceiling and relaxes amortization constraints, reinforcing the housing channel just as lower inflation and rate expectations support households. That is a domestic easing impulse and a medium-horizon support to consumption / housing, even if it also preserves household-debt vulnerabilities.

The external political risk remains the master shock. Hormuz / Middle East disruption matters through energy prices, freight, risk appetite, SEK weakness and inflation expectations. The political-risk premium is therefore external and inflation-channel focused, not domestic-sovereign or central-bank-credibility driven.

March rebound extends into hard April / May data, housing sentiment turns into transactions / credit, and inflation remains below target without expectations damage.
Macro interpretation: Sweden becomes low-inflation stabilization rather than weak-growth disinflation.
Policy / market implication: Riksbank can hold without validating cuts; SEK gets cleaner support from growth-quality and credibility, especially if global risk is benign.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The dominant public narrative broadly confirms the hold-first regime: the Riksbank is expected to remain at 1.75% while it waits for the June forecast round, more inflation detail and the energy-shock path. The market is not pricing an imminent hike, and the main institutional baseline is still no near-term policy move. But the rates / FX implication is more conditional than a domestic-only SEK note would suggest. Swedish rates are held down by repeated inflation undershoots and weak early-year activity, while EURSEK remains dominated by ECB–Riksbank differentials, European risk appetite, equity beta and the Hormuz / energy premium. Sweden’s anchored professional expectations support the policy story, but they do not automatically make SEK a domestic-credibility long.

The real debate is the inflation interpretation. One camp treats the April collapse as partly mechanical and argues that the Riksbank should wait because food VAT, fuel prices, energy disruption and SEK pass-through make the signal noisy. The more dovish camp argues that the undershoot is broader: services and electricity were also soft, CPIF has missed forecasts repeatedly, and underlying inflation excluding tax effects is still below target. The hawkish tail exists, but it comes more from Thedéen’s supply-shock and expectations warning than from the latest inflation data. Institutional dispersion is wide at a 12-month horizon, from cut optionality if energy retreats to late-2026 / 2027 hike paths if inflation risk reappears. That makes the near-term call stable but the medium-term distribution unusually wide.

The scenario breakers are narrow. A Hormuz / energy normalization plus another weak CPIF detail would pull the cut debate forward and make the current hold look too conservative. A weaker SEK, persistent energy shock, higher household expectations and firm wages would shift the Riksbank from passive waiting toward credibility defence. A clean activity rebound through GDP, retail, housing and production would reduce dovish urgency and support SEK through growth quality rather than rate hikes. The key dates are May inflation-expectations data, the Q1 GDP breakdown, April GDP indicator, May CPI and the 17 June Riksbank MPR; those decide whether the regime remains hold-first, shifts toward cuts, or hardens into a pass-through-driven hold-longer stance.

Bottom Line

Sweden is still a hold-first macro regime, but the data lean more dovish than a symmetric optionality label implies. Inflation weakness is broader than VAT / food alone, while growth is mixed rather than broken. The Riksbank can wait because professional expectations are anchored and fiscal / mortgage channels cushion the shock, but household expectations, SEK pass-through and energy risk keep cuts from becoming the base case. SEK is not a pure domestic-stabilization trade; EURSEK remains driven mainly by rates differentials, risk beta and external shock premium.

Base case: Riksbank holds at 1.75% through summer; dovish data bias remains live, but cuts require energy normalization and another clean underlying-inflation undershoot.
