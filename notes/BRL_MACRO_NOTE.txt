## Executive Summary

Brazil has shifted from a simple inflation-capped easing story into a **stop-go, confirmation-first policy regime**. The easing cycle remains alive, but its remaining depth is shallow and no longer linear. Copom is still cutting because policy is restrictive and inflation has some marginal core relief, but the public reaction function is now anchored by above-ceiling inflation, de-anchored expectations, resilient activity, fiscal stimulus and no forward guidance.

The main change over the last four weeks is not only the data; it is the official and market interpretation of the data. May IPCA breached the tolerance ceiling at 4.72% y/y, June IPCA-15 later delivered better monthly/core/services details, and external accounts improved. But the Copom statement, minutes, RPM and press conference hardened the framework: the total easing magnitude is data-dependent, stimulus is an upside inflation risk, services inflation remains incompatible with convergence, and expectations are not anchored enough to validate a smooth path.

The prior “BRL supported by carry and external cushion” thesis still holds structurally, but it needs more two-sided framing. Brazil’s external financing is comfortable, FDI coverage is strong, and the Selic level still gives BRL carry credibility. The live market driver, however, is not only domestic credibility; it is also the US rate path. A hawkish Fed / narrower US-Brazil differential can pressure BRL even if the BCB behaves credibly. FX is therefore supported, but not insulated.

The binding constraint is **inflation expectations and fiscal-monetary credibility**, not external funding and not outright growth weakness. Activity is uneven, with retail softness and weak PMIs on one side, but the official tape still emphasizes resilient domestic activity and a strong labor market. Current policy assumption: one more 25bp cut to 14.00% remains plausible and close to consensus, but the cycle is now stop-go; the next move is conditional, not pre-committed. Base cross-asset implication: shallow residual easing, BRL carry still viable but more Fed-sensitive, DI duration capped by expectations and fiscal credibility.

## Growth / Activity

Brazil’s growth signal is uneven but not weak enough to dominate the policy path. Q1 GDP remained resilient, April IBC-Br rebounded, services volumes recovered, and April industrial output expanded. Those hard-data anchors keep the economy out of a recessionary-easing regime.

The quality of growth has nevertheless softened. The May PMI sequence reversed April’s inventory / front-loading bounce: manufacturing fell back below 50, services slowed close to stagnation, and the composite moved into contraction. New orders and export orders weakened, and employment growth in the PMI survey eased. The private-sector impulse is weaker than the backward-looking hard data suggest.

The consumer is the main soft pocket. April retail sales fell 1.5% m/m, y/y growth slowed sharply, and fuels / rate-sensitive categories were a drag. That shows the 14%+ Selic environment is transmitting into household demand. But this is not yet the dominant public narrative because official communication still highlights resilient activity and a strong labor market.

The correct 3–6 month framing is **resilient activity with a weakening margin**, not stagflation as a public-market base case. Growth is soft enough to preserve residual easing optionality, but still firm enough for the BCB to treat demand, labor, fiscal stimulus and services inflation as constraints on deeper cuts.

## Domestic Demand

Domestic demand is no longer a clean support pillar, but it has not weakened enough to force a dovish policy reweighting. Retail softness matters because it confirms rate transmission. PMI order weakness matters because it questions the durability of the Q1 / April hard-data strength. But fiscal stimulus, income-support measures, earmarked credit and a strong labor market keep the BCB from treating demand as sufficiently restrictive.

The demand channel is therefore part of the inflation problem, not simply an easing argument. The BCB is explicitly concerned that fiscal and credit impulses can weaken monetary-policy transmission. That matters because the same high Selic that is cooling retail may be partially offset by household-support and election-cycle measures.

Labor remains an indirect signal in the supplied data, but not a dovish one in the official tape. The market is not trading a labor-break story. The relevant policy question is whether domestic demand slows enough to reduce services inflation and expectations without requiring a credibility-damaging easing extension. That confirmation has not arrived.

## Inflation

Inflation remains the binding macro channel. The May IPCA print was the deterioration point: 0.58% m/m, 4.72% y/y, above the 4.5% upper tolerance band and stronger than expected. It confirmed that wholesale / food relief had not yet cleared the policy-relevant consumer gauge.

The June IPCA-15 improved the composition but did not clear the regime. Monthly inflation undershot consensus, food slowed, underlying services eased, labor-intensive services improved, and the BCB core average moved in the right direction. This keeps one more 25bp cut possible. But annual IPCA-15 still rose to 4.80%, above the ceiling, and the RPM / BCB communication kept the breach risk active despite the softer mid-month details.

The key issue is now expectations. Focus inflation expectations around 2026 / 2027 remain too high relative to target, and longer-horizon de-anchoring has become part of the policy debate. A single week of stabilization after a long run of upward revisions is helpful, but it is not re-anchoring. Copom needs repeated confirmation that expectations have stopped drifting, not only one softer inflation component.

What is no longer the main inflation problem is a pure wholesale-price shock. IGP-M and IGP-DI have cooled from the April spike, and food/grain supply is a partial offset. What remains unresolved is downstream services/core inflation, fiscal / credit stimulus, energy and weather risk. El Niño risk is a relevant supply-side caveat: food supply looks better now, but weather can still reverse the disinflation channel.

## Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

**Last Central Bank Meeting — Copom, 17 June 2026**
Decision: Cut Selic by 25bp to 14.25%
Previous rate: 14.50%
Stance: Hawkish easing; no guidance; shallow residual easing only.

The June meeting confirmed the easing cycle remains active, but only as a calibrated and data-dependent process. The move was in line with consensus and preserved one-more-cut optionality. The communication was the real policy signal: Copom refused to commit to the next move, stressed upside risks, kept inflation projections uncomfortable, and introduced fiscal / economic stimulus as an upside inflation risk that can weaken monetary transmission.

The post-meeting tape reinforced the same framework. The minutes formalized stop-go optionality, the RPM raised the probability of an upper-bound inflation breach, Galípolo refused to signal the next decision, and Picchetti emphasized that services inflation remains incompatible with convergence. This is not a committee trying to guide the market into a sequence. It is a committee forcing the curve to price each inflation, expectations, FX, fiscal and activity datapoint.

Policy is still restrictive. A 14.25% Selic keeps real rates high and supports BRL carry. But high real rates are not enough to justify a longer easing cycle while expectations sit above target and fiscal / credit impulse may be offsetting monetary restraint. The BCB is reacting to credibility conditions first, not to the level of nominal rates alone.

**CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION**

Base case over the next 3–6 months: Copom remains in a stop-go regime with shallow residual easing. One final 25bp cut to 14.00% is plausible and close to consensus, but not automatic; the cycle does not look like a smooth path toward materially lower rates. A more dovish path requires repeated confirmation from full IPCA, core/services, Focus expectations and BRL stability. A more hawkish path comes from renewed expectations drift, services/core reacceleration, fiscal / election stimulus, BRL weakness, Fed-driven differential compression or concrete evidence that monetary transmission is being blunted.

## Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end has moved from a clean easing trade toward shallow residual easing / plateau pricing. May IPCA forced out part of the expected year-end easing, and consensus now clusters around one more 25bp cut rather than a deep cycle. The June IPCA-15 composition gives receivers a tactical argument, but the BCB communication caps extrapolation.

Rates repricing now depends on the expectations gate. A full-June IPCA confirmation plus two or more Focus improvements would reopen a multi-cut debate. Without that, the curve should remain biased toward a long plateau. The long end is still more vulnerable because fiscal stimulus, earmarked credit, election spending and credibility risk keep term premium alive.

BRL is supported but more conditional than a domestic-only carry story implies. External accounts are strong: FDI coverage is comfortable, the current-account deficit is manageable, and trade still provides a nominal cushion. But late-June price action shows BRL is also trading the US rates channel. A hawkish Fed, higher US terminal pricing or slower US disinflation compresses the Brazil-US carry differential from the US side and can weaken BRL even if local fundamentals are stable.

The FX / rates asymmetry therefore exists, but it is not clean. BRL has better external cushioning than the DI curve, yet both are exposed to the same credibility and global-rate constraints. The constructive BRL case requires high carry, credible BCB restraint, stable fiscal expectations and no Fed-driven differential shock.

## Political / Policy Events

Politics matters through fiscal, credit and election-cycle transmission, not through an acute institutional shock. There was no single political event that reset the regime, but policy risk is now embedded in the reaction function.

The concrete channels matter. Income-tax exemptions, household-spending measures, earmarked credit, pre-election fiscal support and slower fiscal discipline can all blunt the normal transmission from high Selic to demand and inflation. That is why fiscal risk is not just a long-end premium story; it directly affects how restrictive policy needs to remain.

Copom composition remains an H2 risk, but it is secondary to election-cycle fiscal pressure. Any perceived dovish tilt in appointments would matter for credibility, but the immediate policy constraint is more tangible: whether government stimulus keeps demand and services inflation too firm for inflation expectations to re-anchor.

## ARC

**Data window: 9 March 2026 to 29 June 2026**

**March to mid-April — cautious easing begins.**
Brazil entered the easing phase with activity resilient enough to avoid a hard-landing frame and inflation soft enough at the margin to allow 25bp cuts. Copom never validated a smooth cycle: inflation projections, oil / geopolitical risk and expectations kept the stance cautious from the beginning.

**Late April to mid-May — inflation-capped easing.**
Retail, industrial output and trade supported the growth / BRL floor, while IPCA, IGP-M, IGP-DI and services-price rigidity made faster cuts hard to justify. The regime was already supported BRL versus fragile DI duration: high carry and external cushion, but no clean duration all-clear.

**Late May to early June — resilient demand, weak forward quality, sticky prices.**
External accounts improved, Q1 GDP was firm, and April hard data stayed positive, but PMIs rolled over and cost pressure stayed elevated. Public narrative still treated activity as resilient, not stagflationary, because the BCB and institutional forecasts emphasized strong labor, GDP resilience and fiscal support. The problem became too much inflation persistence for the degree of demand cooling visible in the forward data.

**Mid-to-late June — stop-go confirmation regime.**
Copom delivered another 25bp cut, May IPCA breached the tolerance ceiling, and BCB communication hardened. June IPCA-15 improved the core/services margin, external accounts remained supportive, and Focus later stabilized, but none of this produced a clean dovish reset. The setup is now shallow residual easing, high confirmation bar, and BRL carry increasingly exposed to the US-rate differential and election-cycle premium.

## Trigger Map

**Reopen easing debate / dovish reweighting**
Full IPCA confirms the IPCA-15 core/services improvement and Focus expectations decline for several weeks. Macro read: expectations gate starts to reopen. Market / policy implication: one final cut becomes likely and a shallow extension becomes tradable; front-end receivers gain, BRL can hold if Fed risk is quiet.

**Hold-longer / credibility constraint hardens**
Services/core reaccelerate, Focus resumes drifting higher, or fiscal / credit stimulus appears to blunt monetary transmission. Macro read: restrictive rates are not delivering enough disinflation. Market / policy implication: pause becomes extended plateau; DI selloff risk shifts from front end to term premium.

**US-rate / FX differential shock**
Fed repricing turns more hawkish or US data keeps the dollar bid. Macro read: BRL carry is compressed from the external side, not because Brazil’s balance of payments breaks. Market / policy implication: BRL weakens despite high Selic; Copom loses easing space through FX / expectations feedback.

**Election / fiscal risk premium widens**
Concrete pre-election spending, income-tax exemptions, earmarked credit growth or appointment concerns raise doubts about policy discipline. Macro read: neutral rate / credibility premium rises. Market / policy implication: long-end underperforms, BRL risk premium rises, easing cycle can stop even if activity softens.

## Debate / Public Tape Tension

Public market narrative broadly confirms the stop-go regime. The dominant institutional read is not that Brazil has entered a clean easing cycle; it is that Copom delivered a grudging 25bp cut while refusing a smooth path and raising the bar for further easing. Consensus still allows shallow residual easing — roughly one more 25bp cut is consistent with the latest Focus path — but the front end has already repriced away from a deeper cycle after the May IPCA breach and the BCB communication package. For rates, this validates plateau / shallow-residual-easing pricing rather than a duration all-clear. For FX, carry and external financing still support BRL, but the tape is trading the Brazil-US differential as much as domestic credibility; Fed repricing is now a live BRL driver.

The key tension is that the market narrative is less pause-specific and less stagflationary than the macro shorthand. Stop-go is well validated; “next-meeting pause” is a house-lean rather than a settled institutional consensus. Capital Economics and BBVA-type paths still leave cuts alive, while the stricter camp rests on expectations de-anchoring, fiscal impulse, services inflation and post-IPCA repricing rather than a uniformly declared cycle-over call. The public tape also frames demand as resilient more than stagflationary: the BCB cites strong labor and activity resilience, while institutional forecasts have upgraded growth on fiscal support. The deeper debate is therefore not weak growth versus inflation; it is whether resilient demand and fiscal stimulus keep expectations too high for the BCB to keep cutting.

The near-term scenario breakers are narrow and high-signal. Full June IPCA and the next Focus sequence decide whether the IPCA-15 core relief is a genuine disinflation turn or only a temporary stabilization. US CPI / Fed repricing decides whether BRL carry remains monetizable or gets compressed by the dollar leg. Fiscal and election-cycle measures decide whether the BCB’s concern about weakened monetary transmission becomes a binding policy constraint. Energy and weather remain two-sided: softer oil helps disinflation but can reduce Brazil’s commodity / fiscal cushion, while El Niño can reverse the food-relief channel. These are the gates that change the policy path, BRL/DI asymmetry and risk premium.

## Bottom Line

Brazil is still an easing story, but only in shallow residual form. The regime is now stop-go: one more 25bp cut is plausible, but the BCB has not validated a smooth path and the confirmation bar is high. Inflation expectations, services/core inflation and fiscal / stimulus transmission dominate the reaction function. BRL remains supported by carry and external financing, but the FX case is more exposed to Fed-differential compression and election risk than a pure domestic-carry frame implies. Base case: **one final cut possible, extended plateau likely thereafter; BRL carry remains viable but conditional, while DI duration needs repeated inflation-expectations confirmation before a cleaner rally can extend.**

Executive Summary

Brazil has moved from conditional easing into a more explicitly inflation-capped late-cycle easing regime. The policy direction remains easier, but the distribution is now much less benign: Copom can still cut because real rates are restrictive and activity is not overheating, but the path is increasingly hostage to inflation expectations, food / fuel / services pass-through, and fiscal credibility.

The main change over the last four weeks is the deterioration in inflation quality despite intermittent tactical relief. IPCA-15 undershot consensus, IPC-Fipe softened, and April IPCA marginally missed forecasts, but those dovish details are dominated by the broader structure: annual IPCA rose to 4.39%, close to the top of the target band, IGP-M and IGP-DI surged, PMI cost pressures intensified, and services prices remain sticky. The inflation problem is no longer only monthly volatility; it is the risk that upstream and relative-price shocks keep expectations from re-anchoring while Copom is already cutting.

What did not change is Brazil’s relative EM cushion. BRL is still supported by high carry, a strong trade balance, commodity export strength, and better external-account optics than more fragile EM peers. The domestic activity floor also remains alive: PMIs rebounded, industrial output beat, retail sales were strong, and imports rose alongside exports. But the final services-volume print weakens the clean growth-resilience narrative and introduces a more difficult calibration: sticky prices with less reliable activity breadth.

The binding constraint is therefore inflation credibility, not growth weakness. Activity softness can slow the pace of tightening in financial conditions, but it is not yet weak enough to force aggressive easing. Current policy assumption: Copom continues gradual easing only if inflation expectations stabilize and May CPI / services-core data soften. Faster cuts require a cleaner disinflation sequence; renewed fuel, services or expectations pressure pushes the regime toward slower cuts or pause. For BRL, the base case remains supported-but-conditional carry, not a clean disinflation rally.

Growth / Activity

Brazil’s growth impulse is no longer weak enough to dominate the reaction function, but it is not broad enough to call reacceleration. The first part of the window improved visibly: manufacturing PMI returned to expansion at 52.6, services PMI rose to 52.3, composite PMI reached 52.4, industrial output beat expectations, and retail sales surprised positively. That combination moved Brazil away from a recessionary-easing narrative.

The floor carriers are still services, retail, external demand and commodities. Retail was the cleanest domestic-demand support, with March sales up 0.5% m/m and 4.0% y/y, extending sequential momentum and showing that the household channel had not rolled over under high rates. Industrial output also improved, with a third monthly increase and positive sector breadth.

The weak pocket is the quality and breadth of activity. Manufacturing’s earlier recovery had cost-pressure and front-loading caveats. Industrial levels remain far from a structural boom. The 15 May services-volume fall of 1.2% m/m, broad across all five surveyed activities, is the clearest late-window texture change. It does not yet signal an outright services recession because the y/y trend remains positive, but it breaks the simple “resilient growth prevents dovishness” read.

The correct 3–6 month growth framing is therefore resilient but uneven stabilization. Brazil is not collapsing under restrictive rates, but the growth floor is less clean after the services-volume shock. That matters for rates because weak services volumes can support some duration bid, but sticky services prices still prevent a clean dovish policy translation.

Domestic Demand

Domestic demand is firm enough to keep Copom cautious, but uneven enough to prevent a hawkish growth upgrade. Retail resilience is the strongest pro-demand signal. The March retail beat, positive breadth and record-level framing show that consumption is still absorbing high rates better than a hard-landing scenario would imply.

The demand signal is not uniformly strong. Earlier retail y/y had slowed sharply, services activity undershot in February, and the latest services-volume decline points to fragility beneath the headline PMI rebound. The consumer is not breaking, but credit-sensitive and services-volume segments are no longer giving a clean acceleration message.

Labor is not independently observable in the current window. There was no hard PNAD / CAGED-type release in the supplied Brazil sequence. The labor read is therefore indirect: retail and services PMI resilience imply that income and employment conditions have not deteriorated enough to force a growth-dominant Copom pivot, while the services-volume contraction argues against upgrading labor-backed demand momentum. Net: domestic demand supports gradual easing, not faster easing.

Inflation

Inflation is the binding macro channel. The apparent dovish details inside the latest prints are not strong enough to change that conclusion.

The softer-than-consensus IPCA-15 and IPCA monthly outcomes bought tactical room, but the medium-horizon inflation quality deteriorated. IPCA-15 rose to 0.89% m/m and 4.37% y/y. Full April IPCA printed 0.67% m/m versus 0.69% expected, but the annual rate rose to 4.39%, only 11bp below the top of the target range. Food and health were major contributors, while transport relief looked partly timing-related rather than a durable offset.

The upstream channel worsened materially. IGP-M rose 2.73% m/m and IGP-DI rose 2.41% m/m, confirming that producer, wholesale and construction-linked pressures are rebuilding. PMI surveys showed sharp input-cost inflation and elevated selling-price inflation. This creates a margin / pass-through problem: consumer CPI has not fully mirrored the upstream shock yet, but the lag risk is now central to the policy debate.

The true binding inflation issue is therefore expectations and pass-through, not one CPI print. What is no longer the main problem is broad demand overheating. What remains unresolved is whether food, fuel, tariffs, freight and services costs remain relative-price shocks or become embedded in expectations and services-core inflation. Until that is resolved, Copom cannot credibly validate faster cuts.

The April meeting confirmed that Copom remains in an easing cycle, but not in a pre-committed normalization cycle. The 25bp cut was in line with consensus and consistent with the tactical relief from IPCA-15, but the surrounding data made a dovish interpretation difficult: IGP-M was accelerating, fiscal data were poor, geopolitical / inflation uncertainty remained high, and the committee kept the future path open.

Policy is still restrictive in level terms. A Selic at 14.50% continues to imply high real rates, especially if inflation expectations can be stabilized. But the meeting showed that the reaction function is credibility-first: Copom is willing to ease because policy is tight, but it is not willing to accelerate easing while inflation expectations, supply shocks and fiscal risk threaten convergence.

The central bank appears to be waiting for three things: confirmation that fuel and food shocks do not broaden into services/core inflation, stabilization in inflation expectations, and enough activity softness to justify continued easing without risking credibility. The market is likely assuming gradual cuts remain possible, but no longer treats faster easing as the default path.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

A more dovish path requires May IPCA below roughly the April run-rate, visible services-core cooling, stabilization in Focus expectations, BRL stability and no new fiscal slippage. A more hawkish path comes from renewed fuel or services pass-through, further IGP-to-IPCA transmission, expectations drift, BRL depreciation, or fiscal/election risk widening the credibility premium. Copom is reacting first to inflation convergence and expectations, second to credibility-sensitive FX / fiscal conditions, and only third to activity softness.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is trading conditional easing, not a clean cutting cycle. The April Selic cut confirms direction, but IPCA, IGP-M, IGP-DI and retail resilience reduce the case for acceleration. The next rates repricing will be driven by May IPCA, services-core momentum, Focus expectations and whether the latest services-volume weakness becomes persistent.

For rates, the curve has a split signal. Weak services volumes support receivers at the margin, especially if the activity slowdown broadens. But the long end remains vulnerable because fiscal slippage, producer-price acceleration and inflation expectations keep credibility risk alive. Faster BCB easing is difficult to price unless inflation data validate it first.

For BRL, the dominant channel is carry plus external cushion versus inflation credibility. BRL has support from high rates, a record April trade surplus, commodity exports, soybean / corn supply leadership and the absence of acute external funding stress. The trade balance reinforces Brazil’s relative EM advantage. But commodity strength is double-edged: it supports external accounts while also feeding pipeline inflation and potentially inflation expectations.

Financial conditions remain restrictive enough to slow parts of the economy, but not restrictive enough to create a simple recessionary easing argument. The better macro read is: BRL supported, DI duration fragile. The FX can hold up if Copom preserves credibility; duration needs actual disinflation confirmation.

Political / Policy Events

There was no single fresh domestic political shock in the last four weeks that independently reset the Brazil macro regime. Politics did not become the primary driver through a new election event, institutional break or central-bank independence shock.

But fiscal credibility is active in pricing. The March primary deficit miss and gross-debt concerns matter because Brazil is cutting into a still-fragile inflation-expectations environment. Fiscal slippage does not mechanically block 25bp cuts, but it raises the credibility hurdle for the easing cycle and keeps the long end sensitive to any deterioration in the fiscal path.

The policy-risk channel is therefore fiscal-monetary credibility, not immediate political crisis. Election risk remains a medium-horizon background risk for BRL and DI because markets are already sensitive to any sign that fiscal easing or pre-election spending weakens the disinflation story.

Executive Summary

Brazil has entered easing, but the market narrative is now explicitly about conditional easing rather than a clean cycle. The last four weeks confirmed the start of cuts, then made clear that Copom is no longer willing to pre-commit to pace. That is the real regime shift. Activity has stabilized enough to justify easing direction, but not weakened enough to force aggressive support. Inflation has improved enough in backward-looking annual terms to keep the easing story alive, but the forward constraint has worsened through expectations drift, oil shock pass-through, and the deliberate withdrawal of guidance at the March meeting.

What changed is not the direction of policy, but the shape of the distribution. Before the March Copom, the question was whether easing would begin and whether 25bp or 50bp was more likely. After the meeting, the question became broader: 50bp if oil retreats and expectations stabilize, 25bp if disinflation continues but remains messy, or even pause if fuel pass-through and expectations deterioration intensify. What did not change is the core macro asymmetry: growth softness alone still does not dominate the reaction function. Copom remains credibility-first.

The binding constraint is no longer simply “monthly inflation friction.” It is now the combination of still-improving backward-looking inflation, worsening forward inflation expectations, and a near-dated fuel pass-through that has not fully entered the CPI prints yet. The latest IPCA-15 was constructive in level and direction, but its composition was not clean enough to reopen a normal easing path. Externally, Brazil remains flexible rather than stressed, but that should be read as removal of acute vulnerability, not as an independent BRL-positive catalyst. For BRL and front-end rates, the most important variables are now expectations drift, oil transmission into April/May CPI, and whether fiscal/election noise begins to compound the credibility problem.

Growth / Activity

The growth regime has stabilized from the clearer February slowdown scare, but the stabilization remains narrow and rate-sensitive rather than broad-based. Retail, services, and IBC-Br all pushed back against the hard-landing narrative and confirmed that domestic demand is not breaking under restrictive policy. That matters because it reduces the growth urgency behind aggressive easing and gives Copom room to remain cautious.

But stabilization is not reacceleration. GDP stagnation in Q4, the earlier industrial recession signal, and weak manufacturing still define the medium-horizon floor of the economy. The floor carriers are services and household demand. The clearest weak pocket remains manufacturing and the more rate-sensitive industrial side. Even where industrial output rebounded, the composition leaned more toward capital goods than a broad consumer-demand revival.

So the correct 3–6 month framing is still floor / stabilization. Brazil is not in a clean downturn, but it is also not generating enough broad growth momentum to absorb a prolonged credibility shock without renewed weakening. The growth side argues for easing in direction, not for aggressive policy normalization.

Labor / Employment

Labor is not the decisive transmission channel in the current window, but the broad read remains resilient rather than breaking. There was no new hard labor-market print in the supplied release set, and the market narrative continues to treat the labor backdrop as supportive enough to prevent a growth-dominance turn in the reaction function.

That matters because a labor break would be the cleanest way to force Copom to weight activity more heavily. At present, that has not happened. The retail and services floor is consistent with labor resilience, while the industrial weakness argues against any strong labor re-tightening story. Net: labor is not forcing policy either way, which leaves inflation credibility in control.

Inflation

Inflation remains the key macro channel, but the constraint has become more explicitly forward-looking. Backward-looking inflation has improved enough to keep the easing story intact. February IPCA at 3.81% y/y was a major step lower, and March IPCA-15 slowed sharply from 0.84% to 0.44% m/m while the 12-month rate fell from 4.10% to 3.90%. Those are not worsening inflation prints in absolute terms.

But the public tape is right to treat this as more than a “slower-than-hoped disinflation” story. The sharper problem is expectations drift. Focus has been moving the wrong way, and that is exactly the kind of signal Copom cannot ignore when it has already entered easing under an oil shock. So the inflation problem is now three-layered: annual disinflation is still working, monthly inflation remains messy rather than clean, and expectations are drifting higher into a period when fuel pass-through has not yet fully hit consumer prices.

The composition of IPCA-15 matters here. Food and travel-type components did part of the work in the March surprise, which makes the print less alarming than a broad-based core reacceleration would have been. But that does not make it fully benign. The larger issue is that the oil shock’s transmission is not theoretical anymore. Diesel price increases have already occurred, and the main CPI impact is likely to appear with a lag in April and May. So the inflation regime is still directionally better than feared, but the near-term path is now mechanically harder rather than simply uncertain.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

Last Central Bank Meeting — Copom (18 Mar 2026)
Decision: Cut Selic 25bp to 14.75%
Previous rate: 15.00%
Stance: Easing began, but guidance was withdrawn and the path was reopened

The most important signal from the March meeting was not the size of the cut alone. It was the communication regime. Copom delivered a smaller-than-hoped move relative to a large part of the market, explicitly cited geopolitical conflict and oil risk, raised forward inflation projections, and removed any sense of sequencing commitment. That was the hawkish element. The market did not simply receive 25 instead of 50; it received a central bank telling it that the future path is now fully conditional.

Policy remains restrictive in level terms. The first cut did not materially change that. The main transmission channel from the meeting was therefore credibility and pricing, not mechanical rate relief. Effective conditions loosened less than the policy-rate change alone would suggest because the market lost confidence in the pace of follow-through.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months: easing direction remains intact, but the path is non-linear and may include slower sequencing or even pause rather than a clean run of sequential 25bp cuts. Consensus still leans toward additional easing through year-end, but the distribution has widened materially. A more dovish path requires oil relief, expectations stabilization, and CPI prints showing that the fuel shock is not contaminating the broader convergence path. A more hawkish path comes from continued expectations drift, visible fuel pass-through into April/May CPI, BRL weakness, or fiscal/political noise that reopens the credibility channel. Copom is reacting first to the convergence path and expectations, second to activity, and only third to the desire to normalize rates. The April meeting is genuinely open.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is no longer trading a pre-programmed easing cycle. It is trading the resolution of the oil shock and the inflation-expectations drift. That is the key change. The market still carries a year-end easing bias, but it is now operating with a far wider distribution around the near-term path than before the March Copom. The important outlier in public institutional commentary is not a slightly slower easing pace; it is the possibility of a full mid-year pause if inflation expectations and fuel pass-through worsen.

For rates, the main repricing channels are now: April/May CPI, weekly expectations drift, and whether Copom’s guidance-free stance is validated by subsequent data. Growth data matter, but mainly insofar as they threaten the services and household-demand floor. The bar for growth alone to overpower the inflation-credibility constraint remains high.

For BRL, the tape is best read through carry plus credibility, not through the external account alone. The external side matters because it removes acute vulnerability and keeps Brazil out of a funding-stress regime. But it is not the main active driver. The more important near-term BRL channel is the DI curve reaction to Copom and to incoming inflation data. If the market sees the BCB preserving credibility, carry support holds. If expectations drift and fiscal/election concerns compound the inflation problem, BRL weakness can quickly re-open the pass-through channel and narrow easing room further.

Financial conditions therefore remain restrictive in the macro sense, with the added point that the March cut delivered less relief than the headline suggests because the credibility channel tightened relative to pre-meeting expectations.

Political / Policy Events

There was no new domestic political or fiscal break in the last four weeks that independently reset the macro regime. That point still stands. But it is too weak to treat fiscal and election risk as dormant. They are already being priced as live background vulnerabilities in BRL and the local curve.

That distinction matters. The political channel is not active because of a single fresh event in the latest month; it is active because the market is already discounting the possibility that pre-election fiscal loosening could derail the disinflation-easing mix later in 2026. So the correct framing is: no new political shock in the last four weeks, but fiscal credibility and October election risk remain active medium-horizon pricing channels right now, not merely structural background.

This was the print that validated easing direction. The annual rate fell sharply to 3.81% y/y, which gave Copom the cover to start cutting. Without this print, the March opening move would have been much harder to defend.

This clarified the nuance of the inflation regime. The print beat consensus, but the monthly and annual direction still improved. 5. Current Account / FDI (Feb) — 27 Mar 2026

Trigger Map

More dovish / easier policy / cuts pulled forward
Oil shock relief or clear retreat in Brent, stabilization or decline in Focus inflation expectations, and an April CPI print showing low monthly momentum with services deceleration despite fuel noise. That is the combination that would reopen 50bp-style normalization.

Services and household demand remain resilient, industry stops worsening, and the inflation/fuel shock proves temporary rather than persistent. That would produce a more benign soft-landing-with-cuts narrative, but only if credibility remains intact.

Bottom Line

Brazil is still an easing story, but no longer a linear one. The core regime call remains correct: easing has started, but under a hawkish, guidance-free, credibility-managed framework. The inflation story is still directionally better, yet the forward path is more difficult because expectations are drifting and fuel pass-through is still ahead rather than behind. External accounts remove acute vulnerability, but they do not solve the core constraint. Fiscal and election risk also remain live pricing channels even without a new domestic shock. Base case: easing direction stays intact over 3–6 months, but the near-term path is now genuinely open between slower cuts and pause, with April/May inflation and expectations deciding whether Copom can normalize pace or must stay defensive.
