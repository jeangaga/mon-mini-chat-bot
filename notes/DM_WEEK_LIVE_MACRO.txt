United Kingdom — House Price Rightmove YY (Mar)
Release Date: 16 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 01:01 UK

Reuters Data

House Price Rightmove YY: -0.2% | Prior: 0.0%
House Price Rightmove MM: 0.8% | Prior: 0.0%

Professional Market Commentary

Official Rightmove framing was “steady rather than strong”: March delivered a normal spring asking-price lift, but supply remained at an 11-year high for this point in the year, average time-to-sale was the longest for this stage since 2013, and the average two-year fixed mortgage rate had risen to 4.51% from 4.24% the prior week. Readable housing commentary broadly kept that same emphasis: the market had not broken, but it remained highly price-sensitive and supply-heavy rather than genuinely strong.

Comment — Economist Layer

The y/y move to -0.2% is a mild deterioration from February’s flat annual print, but the more important signal is the internal mix. A +0.8% m/m rise in March is a typical spring seasonal bounce, not renewed pricing power. The problem is that this seasonal lift is occurring alongside excess supply, slower time-to-sale, and renewed mortgage-rate pressure. That combination says sellers are recovering seasonality, not regaining control of pricing. In macro terms, the Rightmove series is behaving like a housing market with too much stock and only limited ability to absorb tighter financing conditions.

State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had UK housing as subdued rather than collapsing, with Nationwide and Halifax still showing modest resilience while construction PMI remained clearly weak. March Rightmove fits that sequence: the monthly bounce returned, but the annual rate slipped negative, so the housing bucket moved from stall to mild deterioration rather than from stall to recovery.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a BoE-moving print on its own, but it keeps the housing channel on the “still rate-sensitive” side of the UK story. If the mortgage repricing seen in Rightmove’s tracker persists, housing remains a drag on any clean consumer re-acceleration narrative. Reaction-function relevance is therefore second order: limited direct policy value by itself, but mildly consistent with gradualism rather than any aggressive extrapolation from better near-term activity prints elsewhere.

Canada — Housing Starts, Annualized (Feb)
Release Date: 16 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 13:15 ET

Reuters Data

House Starts, Annualized: 250.9k | Prior: 238.0k | Revised: 240.1k | Poll: 252.5k | Surprise: -1.6k

Professional Market Commentary

CMHC framed February as a modest monthly rebound with the six-month trend still essentially flat rather than improving decisively. Official guidance stayed cautious: CMHC said business uncertainty and construction costs should weigh on starts in the near to medium term, and the agency’s broader housing outlook remained one of softer construction rather than renewed expansion.

Comment — Economist Layer

The monthly rebound matters less than its quality. SAAR recovered from January’s drop, but the print still missed consensus and the prior month was revised up, which trims the strength of the bounce. More importantly, the six-month trend remained basically flat, so the signal is stabilization at a softer level, not inflection. Under the hood, the national number is also uneven: strength in some multi-unit markets sits alongside persistent weakness in more challenged regions. This is not a clean national construction recovery.

State transition: continuation.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had Canada housing starts as weak and explicitly rate-sensitive, with the house view that restrictive borrowing conditions were still biting the construction cycle. February partially repairs that weakness, but only to the extent of a payback bounce; it does not overturn the softer housing regime already established in the file.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the BoC this remains a soft-demand / residential-investment signal, not a reason to reprice hikes. Combined with weak labour data and softer inflation, housing still fits a hold-now, cuts-not-hikes medium-term discussion more than any renewed tightening bias.

CPI Trim: 2.3% | Prior: 2.4% | Poll: 2.4% | Surprise: -0.10%

Professional Market Commentary

Official and institutional commentary treated the report as a clean but backward-looking disinflation print. StatCan highlighted the GST/HST base effect; the seasonally adjusted monthly pulse was much softer than the raw monthly headline; and the dominant economist framing was that February gives the BoC a benign pre-shock baseline, while March becomes the harder test once the final tax-base effect and oil pass-through overlap.

Comment — Economist Layer

This was not just a headline base-effect story. Headline y/y fell to 1.8% partly because the February 2025 tax normalization dropped out, but the seasonally adjusted monthly CPI pace was only 0.1%, CPI excluding indirect taxes slowed to 1.9%, and the BoC’s preferred core measures moved lower together. That is a materially cleaner underlying inflation picture than the raw headline alone implies. Sticky pockets remain, but the right read is: base effects helped the headline, and underlying inflation also genuinely cooled before the energy shock hit.

State transition: improvement.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had Canadian inflation already on a gradual disinflation path, with January core measures softer and the broader Canada macro sequence leaning dovish alongside stalling GDP and labour-market cracks. February extends and broadens that trend: headline moved below 2%, and the preferred core measures all stepped lower together. Relative to the prior sequence, this is a cleaner pre-shock improvement in the inflation baseline.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the 18 March BoC decision, this supports hold with a dovish lean rather than any hike debate. Policymakers can more plausibly look through a temporary oil-led headline rebound because the pre-shock core backdrop has softened and labour slack has increased. The next real verification test is March CPI: that print will combine the final tax base effect with the first fuller oil-shock pass-through.

Australia — RBA Cash Rate (Mar)
Release Date: 17 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 04:30

Reuters Data

RBA Cash Rate: 4.10% | Prior: 3.85% | Poll: 4.10% | Surprise: 0.000%

Professional Market Commentary

Official RBA communication made clear that the hike was not just an oil-shock reaction. The Board raised the cash rate 25bp to 4.10%, warned of a material risk that inflation stays above target for longer, and recorded a very tight 5–4 split. Governor Bullock’s message was explicit: higher fuel prices add to inflation, but they were not the reason for the decision; inflation was already too high because demand was outstripping supply.

Comment — Economist Layer

The key signal is not the 25bp move itself, which was priced; it is the combination of second consecutive tightening, explicit domestic-demand rationale, and a narrow 5–4 vote. That mix says Australia remains the DM outlier still leaning into inflation control, but the split also says the cycle is closer to its limit than a unanimous hawkish board would imply. The official statement is compositionally important: business investment outperformed, consumption underperformed, labour underutilisation stayed low, and housing had been strong over the prior year. This is a capacity-pressure hike, not an externally forced panic move.

State transition: escalation.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file already had Australia as the only clear DM hiker, with February’s move to 3.85% framed as the start of a live tightening cycle. March confirms that trajectory, but with an important change in texture: February was recorded as unanimous in the prior file, whereas March is explicitly 5–4. So the regime is still hawkish continuation, but with visibly rising internal resistance.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This strengthens the Australia-versus-rest-of-DM divergence trade. Versus peers that are holding or debating cuts, the RBA is still tightening on domestic inflation grounds. But the 5–4 split matters for terminal-rate mapping: May is live, not locked. The hawkish signal is real; confidence around a long sequence of follow-on hikes should be lower than the headline action alone suggests.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

It does not justify a meaningful downgrade of the BoJ’s “resilient services/private consumption” baseline on its own.

Professional Market Commentary

Official MoF data confirm the headline surprise: exports rose 4.2% y/y, imports rose 10.2%, and the balance swung to a JPY 57.3bn surplus. But the composition is much less clean than the surplus headline suggests. MoF data and Reuters coverage both indicate that exports to China and the US weakened materially even as the aggregate balance printed much better than expected.

The real signal is export deceleration, not the trade-balance beat. Exports slowed from 16.8% to 4.2%; that still beat a low consensus, but it is a material loss of momentum. At the same time, the bilateral mix worsened in Japan’s two critical markets, China and the US. Imports rebounding 10.2% is the constructive domestic-demand side of the report, but it also means the surplus is not telling you that external demand suddenly re-accelerated.

State transition: false dawn.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had January trade as export-strong and import-weak, with the deficit narrower than feared. February reverses that mix: export momentum cooled sharply, imports rebounded strongly, and the balance improved mostly because the prior deficit was extremely large. In sequence terms, the external sector has become less clean, not more.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For BoJ mapping, this is mixed rather than decisively positive. Stronger imports help the domestic-demand story, but weaker exports to China and the US increase uncertainty around the external pillar of growth. The headline surplus should not be read as a reason to accelerate hawkish BoJ repricing.

Canada — BoC Rate Decision (Mar)
Release Date: 18 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 14:45 ET

Reuters Data

BoC Rate Decision: 2.25% | Prior: 2.25% | Poll: 2.25% | Surprise: 0.000%

Professional Market Commentary

The hold was fully expected, but the statement and press conference were richer than the action. The BoC held at 2.25%, said the economy remains in excess supply, flagged the labour market as soft, and noted that CPI excluding taxes and core measures are all now close to 2%. At the same time, it explicitly described the Middle East war as a new uncertainty layer and said it would look through the immediate inflation impact, while not allowing high energy prices to broaden into persistent inflation.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a genuine policy fork, not a simple “hold and done” message. The domestic baseline is soft: growth has weakened, unemployment has risen, and core inflation has come down materially. But the oil shock has created an upside inflation risk just as growth is weakening. That is textbook stagflation-style policy tension. The important point is that the BoC did not validate a renewed hiking bias; it kept the burden of proof on persistence in inflation spillovers, not on the first-round energy move.

State transition: no material change in action, regime fork in forward path.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had January’s BoC hold framed as a tactical pause inside an easing-biased regime. Since then, the live sequence has added soft growth, weak labour, softer housing starts, and a cleaner February CPI baseline. March’s hold therefore continues the pause, but with more two-sided uncertainty because the oil shock now interrupts what had become a progressively more dovish domestic data path.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

The BoC is still much closer to a future cut debate than to a hike debate, but timing has become less clean. Near term, the Bank has room to wait because core inflation is close to target and the economy has slack. The decisive verification test is March CPI: if oil lifts headline but core spillovers stay contained, the cut path reopens later in 2026.

Professional Market Commentary

Official and Reuters reporting point in the same direction: GDP rose only 0.2% q/q in Q4, below both market and RBNZ expectations, while expenditure GDP rose just 0.1%. The composition matters: rental, hiring and real estate services were supportive, but construction was a clear drag, and the broader read from economists was that the release was softer than expected and left more spare capacity in the economy than previously assumed.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is technically positive growth but weak-quality growth. The production measure rose 0.2%, yet the expenditure measure rose only 0.1%, which is the more worrying line. Official reporting and Reuters-based summaries also show gross fixed capital formation weakening and household demand remaining soft, so domestic demand stayed subdued. The small positive headline was therefore carried by a narrow mix rather than by broad internal demand. That is exactly why the GDP result matters for the RBNZ: it implies more spare capacity than the Bank had likely assumed entering the oil shock.

State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had already shown a mixed NZ picture: better dairy and terms of trade, but only moderate retail-volume growth and still-soft household demand. Q4 GDP resolves that tension in the softer direction. Export-price support helped, but the broader domestic recovery remained too narrow to deliver a convincing growth handoff into year-end.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is modestly dovish for the RBNZ, even with oil complicating the near-term inflation path. A weaker output backdrop gives the Bank more room to absorb first-round energy inflation without needing to tighten. In FX terms, the combination of weak GDP, a wider current-account deficit, and Australia’s tighter policy path keeps the relative-pressure story on NZD versus AUD intact.
