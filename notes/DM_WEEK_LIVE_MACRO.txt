Importance: **

Reuters Data

Trade Balance: 698.0M | Prior: -257.0M | Revised: -365.0M
Annual Trade Balance: -3.19B | Prior: -3.00B | Revised: -3.10B

Professional Market Commentary

Official Stats NZ framing emphasized stronger March exports and imports, with exports up 7.3% y/y to $7.9bn and imports up 9.6% y/y to $7.25bn. The visible composition in public coverage centered on precious metals, jewellery and coins, plus fruit, rather than a broad-based manufacturing or generalized external-demand boom. No Tier 1 institutional debate detected.

Comment — Economist Layer

The headline monthly swing is strong, but the hierarchy matters. March moves from a revised February deficit to a decent surplus, yet the rolling annual balance still deteriorates and imports are growing faster than exports on the y/y comparison. That makes this more of a monthly external-relief print than a regime-changing trade improvement. Composition also argues for caution: the export lift is being helped by narrow categories and seasonal fruit strength, so the signal is less about a generalized export-cycle upswing than about specific price and product effects. State transition: no material change.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior DM file had New Zealand already splitting between a more resilient external/price side and much weaker domestic services demand, with PSI falling to 46.1 and household/card-spending signals still soft. This trade surplus fits that same split rather than overturning it. The external bucket looks somewhat better at the margin, but the broader NZ macro story still depends much more on inflation and domestic demand than on one strong monthly trade print.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Helpful for NZD at the margin, but secondary for the reaction function. For rates, this does not materially change the RBNZ story unless later data show the external pulse feeding into broader income and demand support. The more important strategic implication is relative: a firmer trade print cushions downside growth risk a little, but CPI remains the real policy hinge this week.

United Kingdom — House Price Rightmove MM / YY (Apr)
Release Date: 20 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 01:01
Importance: **

Reuters Data

House Price Rightmove MM: 0.8% | Prior: 0.8%
House Price Rightmove YY: -0.9% | Prior: -0.2%

Professional Market Commentary

Rightmove’s own April release framed the market as surprisingly resilient rather than strong. Asking prices rose 0.8% m/m to £373,971, but that increase was still below the long-run April average of 1.2%; the release also emphasized higher mortgage rates since the Iran shock and unusually intense seller competition.

Comment — Economist Layer

The important split is between level and direction. A positive monthly print shows spring seasonality is still working, but the annual rate remains negative and the official Rightmove narrative explicitly says sellers are operating against a tougher financing backdrop and heavier supply. That means the signal is not “housing recovery,” but “housing still absorbing higher mortgage rates better than feared on the month while pricing power remains weak on the year.” In macro terms, this is a modest stabilization print inside an already fragile housing transmission channel. State transition: stabilization.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior DM file had UK housing moving into clear deterioration, with Halifax and RICS both showing that higher mortgage rates were biting demand and March Rightmove already reading as steady seasonality rather than genuine strength. April does not break that sequence. It softens the near-term downside slightly on the monthly move, but the negative annual rate confirms that housing is still a restrictive-rate transmission channel rather than a recovery leader.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Still second-order for the BoE, but directionally useful. This release is not strong enough to repair the UK growth story, yet it is also not weak enough to create a fresh growth scare by itself. The strategic read remains that UK housing is subdued and rate-sensitive, which keeps it supportive of gradualism on policy rather than any renewed hawkish extrapolation from isolated resilience prints.

CPI Inflation YY: 2.4% | Prior: 1.8% | Poll: 2.6% | Surprise: -0.20%

CPI BoC Core MM: 0.2% | Prior: 0.4%
CPI Median: 2.3% | Prior: 2.3% | Poll: 2.3% | Surprise: 0.00%
CPI Trim: 2.2% | Prior: 2.3% | Poll: 2.3% | Surprise: -0.10%

Professional Market Commentary

The dominant institutional read was “headline hotter, core better.” Statistics Canada made clear that the acceleration in headline inflation came from energy, especially gasoline, while CPI excluding gasoline slowed to 2.2% y/y from 2.4%. Public institutional commentary, including BMO and market coverage cited by Reuters, converged on the same point: the record gasoline shock mattered, but the underlying report was milder than feared.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a good example of why composition dominates headline. Energy rose 13.1% m/m in March, gasoline surged 21.2% m/m, and that alone explains most of the jump in headline CPI. Underneath that, the disinflation structure remained intact: ex-gasoline CPI slowed, restaurant inflation cooled sharply because of GST/HST base effects, and several components were still declining or soft. The core hierarchy is therefore reassuring rather than alarming: median held at 2.3%, trim eased to 2.2%, and BoC core m/m halved to 0.2%. This is not “inflation solved,” but it is clearly not a broadening domestic-core re-acceleration either. State transition: stabilization.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior DM file had Canada entering this week with growth already softer through construction and housing, while manufacturing and wholesale rebounds were too backward-looking to resolve the BoC trade-off. This CPI release fits that same regime. Growth was already fragile, and now inflation is being pushed around by energy rather than by a convincing re-heating in domestic core. That keeps Canada in a weak-growth, hard-hold mix rather than shifting it into a clean stagflation escalation.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This materially strengthens the BoC look-through case for April 29. Headline is uncomfortable, but the policy-relevant core signal is still compatible with patience, especially given the softer construction backdrop already in the file. For markets, the front end should stay anchored to hold rather than pivot toward a genuine hike narrative unless April shows broader pass-through beyond fuel.

CPI Index Number: 1,339.0 | Prior: 1,327.0 | Poll: 1,337.8 | Surprise: 1.20

Professional Market Commentary

The professional read was clearly hawkish. Official Stats NZ reporting confirmed annual CPI stayed at 3.1%, above the top of the RBNZ’s 1–3% target band, while Westpac highlighted 1.1% q/q non-tradables inflation and persistent firmness outside a few energy items. House commentary before and after the release had already been leaning toward a higher-Q2 inflation profile, and the Q1 outcome confirmed that the inflation problem is not just imported fuel noise.

Comment — Economist Layer

The hierarchy inside this release is what matters. Headline y/y staying at 3.1% might look stable, but the quarterly acceleration to 0.9% and firm 3.5% annual non-tradables inflation tell us domestic persistence remains too high. Westpac’s read is useful here: electricity and other administered prices are still lifting the floor, yet non-tradables excluding housing and utilities also stayed firm, which argues against dismissing this as a pure petrol story. That is reinforced by rents being notably soft rather than hot, meaning the inflation persistence is broader than just the usual housing channel. State transition: escalation.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior DM file had New Zealand still caught between weak domestic activity and a policy backdrop that was already becoming more inflation-constrained. This print resolves that tension on the inflation side. Domestic demand may still be soft, but Q1 CPI shows that inflation is not gliding back into the band cleanly enough for the RBNZ to relax.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is the cleanest hawkish release in the file so far. It does not force an immediate OCR path mechanically, because growth remains weak and Q2 still matters, but it keeps the RBNZ firmly in tightening-contingency mode and raises the bar for any dovish interpretation of softer activity data. NZD should remain relatively supported on rates differentials unless later data sharply undermine the domestic-demand base.

HMRC Payrolls Change: -11k | Prior: 20k | Revised: -6k

Professional Market Commentary

The institutional narrative did not take the unemployment drop at face value. ONS showed unemployment at 4.9% but inactivity rising to 21.0%, vacancies falling to 711k, and payroll employment down 11k in March; Reuters and analyst round-ups quickly converged on the same interpretation that the headline improvement was composition-driven rather than evidence of renewed labour-market tightness.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a classic misleading-headline labour report. The 4.9% unemployment rate is the eye-catching print, but the internals stay soft: employment growth undershot, payrolls were negative for a second month, vacancies fell to the lowest since early 2021, and private-sector regular pay growth was just 3.2%, the weakest since 2020. The composition matters even more because ONS is explicitly telling users to read the LFS alongside other indicators. Once that is done, the signal is clear: labour is still loosening gradually, and the apparent fall in unemployment is being flattered by higher inactivity rather than broad hiring strength. State transition: false dawn.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior DM file had UK labour as stabilising rather than breaking, with March’s release showing firmer quantity data and slower wage growth. Today’s update weakens that earlier stabilization. Payrolls have rolled back into contraction, vacancies keep falling, and the cleaner wage signal is softer, so the UK labour bucket now looks more like gradual loosening again than durable stabilization.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

BoE-relevant, but not in a simple dovish direction. This labour mix reduces the odds that wages alone force a fresh tightening impulse, yet it also arrives into an energy-shock backdrop that keeps headline inflation risk alive. The policy mapping is therefore still “patient hold,” not “cut soon”: softer labour internals help on the domestic side, but not enough to offset imported inflation risk.
