MONDAY 13 APR 2026

New Zealand — Business PSI (Mar)
Release Date: 13 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 00:30
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Business PSI: 46.1 | Prior: 49.2

Professional Market Commentary

BusinessNZ and BNZ framed March as a clear services-sector deterioration. The official release showed PSI at 46.0, all five sub-indexes below 50, and a broad hit to discretionary consumer-facing activity, while BNZ argued the combined PMI/PSI signal now points to a materially weaker near-term growth backdrop.

Comment — Economist Layer

The key signal is breadth, not just the sub-50 headline. Activity/sales fell to 44.6, new orders to 45.7, employment to 46.4, and negative respondent comments rose to 69.1%, which means the March drop reads as a synchronized services contraction rather than a narrow confidence wobble.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
New Zealand entered the new week with manufacturing still above 50 and the RBNZ freshly in hawkish-hold mode. March PSI cuts directly against that earlier resilience by showing domestic services are weakening much faster than the manufacturing side suggested. The NZ macro mix therefore shifts toward a sharper internal split rather than a clean re-acceleration.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is NZD-negative on domestic macro and raises the bar for any near-term shift toward more overt RBNZ hawkishness. It does not force easing talk by itself, but it makes upcoming card-spending and retail data more important because the Bank now faces visibly weaker services demand against an inflation-risk backdrop.

Canada — Building Permits MM (Feb)
Release Date: 13 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 14:30
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Building Permits MM: -8.4% | Prior: 4.8% | Revised: 3.5% | Poll: -2.1% | Surprise: -6.30%

Professional Market Commentary

StatCan’s release made the headline weakness clear: total permits fell by C$1.1 billion to C$12.1 billion, led by a 24.0% drop in non-residential permits, while residential permits rose modestly. The main mitigating narrative is that part of the institutional decline reflected payback after an unusually large January institutional permit in Ontario, but the official release still describes a materially weaker intentions print.

Comment — Economist Layer

The composition softens the headline only partially. Residential multi-unit activity still provided some support, but the growth signal remains weak because the non-residential collapse was large, January was revised lower, and on a constant-dollar basis permits fell 8.6% m/m and 11.5% y/y.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
Canada entered the week with activity already softening while the BoC still faced an awkward wage and inflation constraint. February permits deepen the weak-activity side of that trade-off without resolving the price-side problem. Canada therefore remains in a hard-hold rather than easy-cut macro bucket.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is CAD-negative on domestic growth at the margin, but it is not enough on its own to force a more dovish BoC. The strategist significance is cumulative: if manufacturing sales, wholesale trade and housing starts also disappoint, the growth side of the BoC dilemma will become much harder to ignore.

Japan — Reuters Tankan N-Man Idx (Apr)
Release Date: 15 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 01:00
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Reuters Tankan N-Man Idx: 31 | Prior: 25

Professional Market Commentary

Reuters framed the April survey as a split Japan story. Manufacturing sentiment slumped sharply on oil and supply-chain concerns tied to the Middle East conflict, but non-manufacturing sentiment improved to 31 from 25, supported by construction, real estate and information-services demand; at the same time, the forward outlook still deteriorated, with non-manufacturers seen at 14 in July.

Comment — Economist Layer

For this chat’s release, the important point is that the non-manufacturing side did not yet roll over with the rest of the shock narrative. A rise to 31 suggests Japanese domestic services and investment-linked service sectors were still holding up better than feared in early April, which offsets the weak household-demand story to some extent.

The offset is incomplete because the same Reuters survey shows forward expectations weakening materially. So the release is better read as near-term resilience with worsening future vulnerability, not as a clean all-clear on Japan services.

State transition: stabilization

Context from prior regional live file:
Japan’s prior sequence had become increasingly uneven, with household demand disappointing even as parts of the business sector remained firmer. April Reuters Tankan non-manufacturing strengthens the resilient-services side of that split. Japan therefore stays mixed rather than sliding into a one-way domestic-demand downturn.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This modestly helps the BOJ’s confidence that service-side demand has not cracked outright, but it is not enough to erase caution because the outlook index still weakens sharply under the Iran-war shock. For rates and JPY, it is mildly supportive versus a pure-growth-scare narrative, while keeping the April BOJ debate conditional rather than one-sided.

Japan — Machinery Orders MM / YY (Feb)
Release Date: 15 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 01:50
Importance: ****

Reuters Data

Machinery Orders MM: 13.6% | Prior: -5.5% | Poll: -1.1%
Machinery Orders YY: 24.7% | Prior: 13.7% | Poll: 8.5%

Professional Market Commentary

The official Cabinet Office release confirmed a sharp rebound in core private-sector machinery orders in February, and accessible market coverage characterized the result as a major beat versus expectations on both monthly and annual measures. In the current Japan debate, that matters because machinery orders are treated as a leading capex signal and therefore as one of the clearest hard-data checks on whether the domestic economy is absorbing the shock.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is the strongest upside activity surprise in the file so far. A 13.6% m/m rebound after January’s 5.5% fall tells us the capex side of Japan’s economy was much firmer in February than consensus expected, which directly offsets the weak household-demand story and argues against reading Japan as a simple domestic slowdown case.

The usual volatility caveat still applies to machinery orders, so this should not be over-elevated into a full regime turn on one print. But in weekly sequence it matters because capex had been one of the remaining supports to the BOJ normalization case, and this release strengthens that support materially rather than weakening it.

State transition: reversal

Context from prior regional live file:
Japan entered the week with household and consumer-sensitive indicators soft, but with parts of the corporate side still more resilient. February machinery orders now push that corporate-capex side back into clear leadership. Japan therefore remains a split economy in which investment is still cushioning softer consumption.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is the cleanest pro-BOJ data point of the week so far because it reduces concern that business investment is already rolling over. It does not force an immediate hike call under current geopolitical uncertainty, but it supports the medium-term normalization narrative and is mildly JPY-supportive at the margin.
