MONDAY 23 MAR 2026

Norway — Credit Indicator YY (Feb)
Release Date: 23 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 08:00

Reuters Data

Credit Indicator YY: 4.6% | Prior: 4.4% | Revised: 4.5%

Professional Market Commentary

Official SSB data showed domestic credit growth edging up to 4.6% y/y in February from 4.5% in January. The internal mix was firmer for corporates than households: household credit stayed at 4.7%, non-financial corporates accelerated to 4.1% from 3.5%, and municipal-government growth slowed to 4.9% from 6.5%. No Tier 1 institutional debate detected.

Comment — Economist Layer

The signal is mild acceleration, not a regime break. The hierarchy matters: household credit did not re-accelerate, but corporate borrowing did, which makes this more informative about business-side resilience than about renewed household leverage excess. In a DM week that is starting to show softer survey momentum elsewhere, Norway is not yet showing the same deterioration through the credit channel.
State transition: mild acceleration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file did not contain a Norway credit block, but its broader regional baseline already argued that DM should not be treated as one common cycle. February credit adds one more piece of evidence in that direction: Norway enters the week with a firmer domestic-financial backdrop than the softening survey signals seen elsewhere in DM.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Ahead of the 26 March Norges Bank decision, this does not force a hawkish repricing on its own, but it does argue against reading Norway through the same soft-growth lens as the UK or New Zealand. The main relevance is relative: Norges still faces a firmer domestic-credit backdrop than several DM peers.

Australia — S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash / S&P Global Svs PMI Flash / S&P Global Comp PMI Flash (Mar)
Release Date: 23 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 23:00

Reuters Data

S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash: 50.1 | Prior: 51.0
S&P Global Svs PMI Flash: 46.6 | Prior: 52.8
S&P Global Comp PMI Flash: 47.0 | Prior: 52.4

Professional Market Commentary

Official and market commentary framed this as a genuine loss of momentum: the flash composite fell to 47.0 from 52.4, marking the first contraction in 18 months, with weakness driven by services rather than manufacturing. Coverage also highlighted falling new orders, weaker confidence, and still-firm price pressures rather than a clean disinflationary slowdown.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is the first serious challenge to the clean “Australia as hawkish outlier” narrative established earlier in the DM sequence. The hierarchy matters: manufacturing stayed marginally above 50, but services collapsed below 50 and pulled the composite into contraction, which means the demand-sensitive domestic side of the economy weakened sharply immediately after the RBA hike. At the same time, the inflation texture did not cleanly improve, so the right read is not simple dovish reversal; it is growth deterioration colliding with still-firm pricing pressure.
State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had Australia as the clearest DM hawk, but it had already inserted a caution flag: the leading index softened after the 5–4 RBA hike, implying that the growth side of the story was becoming more fragile. The flash PMI now strengthens that warning materially. The hawkish regime is not invalidated, but its activity underpinnings are clearly weaker than they looked on 17–18 March.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the RBA, the mapping shifts from “hawkish continuation” toward “hawkish with rising growth constraint.” May remains live, but not linearly so. Australia can still have the tightest reaction function in DM while also facing a higher probability that softer activity starts to cap the hiking path.

TUESDAY 24 MAR 2026

Japan — CPI, Overall Nationwide / CPI, Core Nationwide YY (Feb)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 00:30

Reuters Data

CPI, Overall Nationwide: 1.3% | Prior: 1.5%
CPI, Core Nationwide YY: 1.6% | Prior: 2.0% | Poll: 1.7% | Surprise: -0.10%

Professional Market Commentary

Official and Reuters framing converged on the same point: reported inflation softened materially, but the weakness was heavily policy-distorted rather than purely demand-driven. The Statistics Bureau reported headline CPI at 1.3%, core CPI at 1.6%, and the index excluding both fresh food and energy at 2.5%, while Reuters highlighted the role of subsidies and the BOJ’s plan to introduce a cleaner measure that strips out one-off policy effects.

Comment — Economist Layer

The key hierarchy is headline versus underlying. The soft headline is real in reported terms, but it is not the best measure of domestic inflation persistence. The analytically important signal is that subsidy-driven relief pulled the reported gauges down while the broader underlying measure stayed above 2%. So this is not clean disinflation; it is temporary mechanical suppression overlaying only gradual underlying cooling.
State transition: temporary deterioration in reported inflation, continuation in underlying inflation firmness.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file already had Japan as mixed rather than cleanly strong: services sentiment had stabilised, domestic demand had not collapsed, but the external trade mix had deteriorated. February CPI fits that sequence. It does not overturn the underlying inflation story, but it does make BOJ communication harder because the headline now looks softer than the underlying trend.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This complicates April timing, but it does not kill the tightening case. The BOJ can still lean on underlying measures and on the expected expiry of subsidy effects, yet the bar for a simple headline-driven hawkish read is now higher. The next verification point is whether April inflation rebounds as policy distortions fade.

Japan — S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash SA / S&P Global SVC PMI Flash SA / S&P Global Comp Op Flash SA (Mar)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 01:30

Reuters Data

S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash SA: 51.4 | Prior: 53.0
S&P Global SVC PMI Flash SA: 52.8 | Prior: 53.8
S&P Global Comp Op Flash SA: 52.5 | Prior: 53.9

Professional Market Commentary

Official S&P Global coverage described the release as softer expansion rather than reversal: all three PMIs stayed above 50, but activity lost momentum from February’s stronger readings. The broader S&P research note also highlighted a sharper rise in input costs, with the rate of input-price inflation reaching the highest since April 2025.

Comment — Economist Layer

The correct read is softer momentum with a cost squeeze. All three PMIs remain expansionary, so direction has not turned negative; however, the pace clearly rolled over from February and the cost side became more policy-relevant than the headline level. That creates a mild stagflationary texture: demand is still expanding, but less quickly, while firms face renewed margin pressure because costs are rising faster than the growth pulse.
State transition: deterioration in pace, not direction.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had already moved Japan away from a clean re-acceleration story: Reuters Tankan services were only stable, and the trade report showed a less convincing external mix. The March flash PMI deepens that moderation signal and links it more explicitly to cost pressure.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the BOJ this is directionally ambiguous but tactically important. Growth moderation argues for caution, while firmer cost pressure argues against a straightforward dovish read of the CPI miss. Net result: it keeps the normalisation case alive, but timing remains conditional rather than cleanly accelerated.

United Kingdom — Flash Composite PMI / Flash Manufacturing PMI / Flash Services PMI (Mar)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 10:30

Reuters Data

Flash Composite PMI: 51.0 | Prior: 53.7 | Poll: 52.9 | Surprise: -1.90
Flash Manufacturing PMI: 51.4 | Prior: 51.7 | Poll: 50.1 | Surprise: 1.30
Flash Services PMI: 51.2 | Prior: 53.9 | Poll: 53.0 | Surprise: -1.80

Professional Market Commentary

Institutional commentary treated this as a stagflationary shock print. S&P Global’s official release showed the composite at a six-month low, and Reuters highlighted that manufacturers faced the sharpest rise in input costs since 1992 while overall business growth slowed markedly. The dominant narrative was not simple slowdown, but growth stalling while inflation pressure re-accelerates.

Comment — Economist Layer

The internal hierarchy matters. Manufacturing beat consensus, but services missed badly and services is the more important domestic-demand signal for the UK. The composite staying above 50 prevents a recession call from this release alone, yet the pace fell close to stall speed just as cost pressure jumped. That is why the right interpretation is sharp deterioration in pace with a genuine stagflationary twist, not merely a bad services month.
State transition: sharp deterioration in pace with stagflationary re-pricing.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had only mild UK weakness through the housing channel: the March Rightmove block showed a still-functioning but rate-sensitive market, not a broad domestic-demand break. The flash PMI materially worsens that picture. UK activity moved from “soft but resilient” toward “near-stall with renewed inflation pressure” in a single high-information release.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This materially complicates the BoE path. A clean easing narrative is harder because cost pressure has re-intensified, but a hawkish response also risks leaning into a visible loss of growth momentum. The release therefore compresses policy optionality rather than resolving it in either direction.

United Kingdom — CBI Distributive Trades (Mar)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 12:00

Reuters Data

CBI Distributive Trades: -52 | Prior: -43

Professional Market Commentary

Official CBI and Reuters coverage both framed this as a very weak retail signal: sales volumes fell at the quickest pace since April 2020, and retailers expected April to remain deeply negative at -49. The official release also showed weak online and total distribution sales, while Reuters noted the survey ran from 25 February to 13 March and therefore only partly captured the full conflict shock window.

Comment — Economist Layer

The important point is not the COVID-era comparison by itself, which is rhetorically powerful but analytically imperfect. The deeper signal is that consumer-facing activity was already weak and remained weak even before the full lagged impact of higher fuel costs and uncertainty is visible. In other words, this reads less like a one-off shock and more like an already fragile consumer sector taking another hit.
State transition: acceleration of deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM file had UK housing as rate-sensitive but not broken. The combination of today’s CBI release and the flash services PMI now pulls the broader UK domestic-demand bucket into a clearly softer regime than the file had previously shown.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a direct BoE trigger by itself, but it reinforces the PMI message that domestic demand is fragile. Into UK CPI and retail sales, it raises the probability that the BoE’s inflation problem is arriving alongside a meaningful consumer-growth problem.
