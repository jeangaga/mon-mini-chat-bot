Executive Summary

The UK regime is best framed as extended-hold soft-growth with unresolved persistence risk. The prior passive-hold stagflationary setup remains, but the last four weeks have shifted the balance toward weaker activity and a less hawkish BoE centre. Growth deterioration is real in the forward tape, but not yet a completed hard-data downturn. Inflation risk has also softened at the headline and pipeline level, but services CPI, wages and expectations still block a clean easing call.

The key change is the BoE’s vote-versus-substance divergence. The 18 Jun MPC held Bank Rate at 3.75%, with the vote moving from 8–1 to 7–2 as Greene joined Pill in voting for a hike. Optically, that is hawkish. Substantively, the majority’s reaction function drifted toward extended hold: the Bank lowered the short-term inflation path, energy prices fell, headline CPI undershot the prior fear, PPI momentum cooled, labour demand softened, and Bailey’s trade-off language gave the majority cover to tolerate temporarily above-target inflation while waiting for persistence evidence.

Growth has weakened, but the note should not overstate hard-data confirmation. April GDP fell 0.1% m/m and services contracted 0.2%, while housing stayed weak and June PMI / CBI releases deteriorated across services, goods orders and distribution. But 3M/3M GDP was still +0.7%, the fifth consecutive three-month rise, and manufacturing had genuine pockets of strength. The correct read is survey-led broadening slowdown risk, not a confirmed downturn.

Inflation is no longer an immediate headline-CPI panic. May CPI held at 2.8% versus fears of a move above 3%, PPI monthly input momentum cooled, and the BoE lowered its expected Q4 CPI peak toward a little over 3.25% from the April 3.6% area. Services CPI at 3.7% matters, but it was largely anticipated by the public tape rather than a new hawkish shock. The binding issue is whether services, wages and expectations remain sticky after the energy impulse fades.

GBP is now a weaker-quality carry currency. Gilts can rally on lower energy, softer CPI/PPI and weak activity, but sterling has not participated cleanly because growth quality, fiscal credibility, term premium and domestic political risk are now part of the price. GBP/EUR can still receive relative support if the BoE looks stickier than the ECB, but the broader sterling asymmetry is less favorable: rate support is increasingly offset by weak growth and fiscal / political risk premium.

Growth / Activity

The UK growth signal has deteriorated, but the hard-data base is not yet recessionary. April GDP fell 0.1% m/m, led by a 0.2% fall in services, confirming that the shock was no longer limited to surveys or housing. The services contraction matters because it is the UK’s core domestic engine and the channel most relevant to labour slack, wage pressure and BoE reaction-function risk.

The caveat is important. The three-month GDP trend stayed positive at +0.7%, the fifth consecutive three-month rise, and manufacturing output beat expectations. Some manufacturing strength is contaminated by stockpiling and front-loaded orders, but it should not be dismissed entirely: the public tape treated part of the output strength as genuine subsector resilience, including pharmaceuticals. This keeps the regime in slowdown-risk territory rather than outright downturn.

The forward surveys are weaker and more consistent. Composite PMI remained below 50 for a second month, services fell to 48.7, and new business weakened. CBI orders dropped to -45, the weakest since 2020, confirming that the manufacturing-output headline was not a clean demand signal. CBI distributive trades fell to -54, adding retail / distribution confirmation to the services and goods-order weakness.

Housing remains a rate-sensitive drag rather than a stabilizing offset. Halifax posted a second monthly decline, RICS stayed deeply negative, and Rightmove asking prices fell sharply for June. Lower mortgage quotes help at the margin, but affordability and buyer confidence remain weak. The housing channel reinforces the downside cost of extended restrictive policy.

Retail is the main counter-signal, but not a regime changer. May retail sales beat strongly, including ex-fuel, yet the composition was weather, promotions, online strength and base-effect heavy. GfK confidence stayed weak, major-purchase intentions remained depressed, and CBI retail later deteriorated. The better read is household smoothing through a shock, not a clean consumer reacceleration.

Domestic Demand / Labor

Labour is moving in the direction the BoE majority needs, but not decisively enough to unlock cuts. Headline labour data were mixed: unemployment improved to 4.9% versus a 5.0% poll and employment rose more than expected. But the better-quality labour-demand signals softened: claimant count rose, payrolls were only marginally positive after downward revision, vacancies fell to 707k, and youth unemployment stayed high.

The labour regime is low-hire / weaker-demand, not layoff collapse. Vacancies are the cleanest signal that firms are less willing to hire. Payrolls have not broken further, but they are not recovering convincingly. This weakens the case for pre-emptive hikes because second-round inflation risk is harder to validate when labour demand is cooling.

Wages remain the obstacle. Total pay and regular pay beat expectations, and that gives Greene / Pill enough evidence to argue that inflation persistence cannot be dismissed. The public tape is more nuanced than a simple wage-scare narrative: private-sector wage growth is closer to target-consistent levels, and public-sector / bonus effects complicate the aggregate. Still, the BoE cannot cut on labour slack alone until regular pay and services CPI soften together.

The labour channel is therefore the bridge between weak growth and policy optionality. If vacancies keep falling, payrolls are revised lower and regular pay cools, the majority can reopen an eventual easing path. If wage data stay firm into the next pay round, the minority hike tail remains live even with weak activity.

Inflation

Inflation has shifted from headline panic to persistence test. May CPI stayed at 2.8% y/y versus expectations for a move to 3.0%, monthly CPI was softer than expected, core CPI undershot, and RPI was also soft. This gave the BoE majority cover to wait and reduced the urgency of the hawkish reaction function.

The BoE’s own inflation path was marked down. The June guidance pointed to CPI a little under 3% in Q3 and a little over 3.25% in Q4, below the April projection around 3.6%. That recalibration matters more than the vote split alone. The institutional inflation path is no longer validating an immediate pre-emptive hike unless services, wages or energy re-accelerate.

PPI supports the same marginal story. Input prices remain elevated on a year-on-year basis, but the monthly impulse slowed sharply, output-price momentum cooled, and core output eased. That points to pipeline cresting rather than fresh upstream acceleration. It does not eliminate pass-through risk, but it lowers the probability that the energy shock automatically embeds in goods and services pricing.

Services inflation is still the gate, but the 3.7% print should not be treated as a shock. It was expected by several institutional forecasters after April’s distorted services softness. Its importance is not the surprise value; it is the level and persistence risk. A sustained services rate above the mid-3s, combined with firm wages, keeps the BoE credibility constraint alive. A fade back below that area would materially weaken the hawkish case.

The binding inflation issue is therefore narrower and more conditional. Headline CPI and PPI no longer justify an immediate tightening narrative by themselves. The remaining constraint is whether services inflation, wages and expectations remain sticky after the energy path normalizes.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Bank of England policy meeting was 18 Jun 2026. The MPC held Bank Rate at 3.75% by a 7–2 vote. Greene and Pill voted for a 25bp hike to 4.00%; the other seven members voted to hold. June was a non-MPR meeting, so the signal came through the minutes and individual rationales rather than a new full forecast round.

The meeting was hawkish by vote but dovish-leaning in the centre. The dissenters argued that second-round effects and expectations required a risk-management hike. The majority had more evidence to wait: lower energy prices, softer headline CPI versus the April path, cooler PPI momentum, emerging labour slack, weak demand and a lower short-term inflation profile. Bailey’s framing accepted temporarily above-target inflation as part of the return-to-target trade-off, and the minutes attributed much of the upward sterling yield-curve slope to risk premia rather than expected rates.

Policy is restrictive and the BoE is buying time. The majority is not validating cuts yet, but it is also not endorsing the hawkish flank. Taylor’s extended-hold logic fits the broader centre: policy is already above neutral, growth is softening, and the Bank can wait for persistence evidence. The 30 Jul MPR is the next major test because it forces a full forecast update and a new vote split after the energy decline, May CPI/PPI relief and late-June activity deterioration.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is Bank Rate held at 3.75%, with cuts deferred and hikes only a minority / conditional tail. A more dovish path requires durable lower energy, services CPI easing below the current 3.7% area, regular pay moderation, weaker vacancies / payrolls and stable expectations. A more hawkish path requires services CPI to stay sticky or rise, wage upside to persist, expectations to firm, energy to rebound, or Mann / another holder to move toward Greene and Pill. The BoE is reacting most to services-wage-expectations persistence, but the majority is increasingly constrained by soft growth and already-restrictive financial conditions.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The UK rates market is trading extended hold with residual hike risk, not a clean tightening cycle. Money-market expectations have moderated sharply from the March peak, when multiple hikes were priced, toward a much smaller residual hike premium. The remaining upward slope looks more like risk premium than pure expected-rate pricing, consistent with the BoE’s own framing.

Rates can rally on softer data, but the rally is conditional. Cooler headline CPI, lower PPI impulse, weak PMIs and softer labour demand support front-end relief. But services CPI, wage upside and a two-vote hike dissent stop the curve from pricing a straightforward easing path. The market is close to the BoE centre, but still vulnerable to services / wage surprises.

Long-end gilts are a separate macro channel. The 30-year gilt rally after energy de-escalation and softer data showed that term-premium stress can reverse quickly. But the decomposition is mixed: global term-premium forces explain a large part of the move, while UK-specific fiscal credibility, QT supply, pension-fund demand shifts and the 30-year / 5-year spread remain domestic risk-premium variables. This makes UK financial conditions sensitive to both global duration and local fiscal confidence.

GBP is not tracking gilt relief cleanly. That bond-FX divergence is the key market signal. Lower yields can help activity, but if they come from weak growth and lower policy credibility rather than improved fundamentals, sterling does not necessarily benefit. GBP is therefore a lower-quality carry asset: rate support exists, but it is increasingly offset by growth weakness, fiscal risk and political uncertainty.

GBP/EUR remains the cleanest relative-policy expression if the BoE is stickier than the ECB. But the public tape has shifted toward questioning sterling carry durability. Several institutional views now lean toward EUR/GBP upside over a 6–12 month horizon because UK growth is weaker and the BoE is less hawkish than residual market pricing implies. For broad GBP, the key risk is that markets move from “sticky BoE support” to “weak-growth / fiscal-risk premium.”

Political / Policy Events

Political and fiscal risk are now more regime-relevant than in the prior note. The channel is not a fresh fiscal impulse; it is fiscal credibility, gilt term premium, competitiveness, confidence and sterling risk premium. The autumn Budget cycle, OBR headroom and the Chancellor’s credibility are now part of the gilt / GBP tape.

The political channel is also showing up in activity commentary. Domestic political uncertainty is cited alongside costs and weak confidence in services weakness. CBI’s competitiveness framing adds another layer: the UK growth problem is not just cyclical demand weakness, but also trade friction, supply-chain integration and EU-UK reset optionality. That matters for the medium-horizon GBP risk premium.

The long-end fiscal channel is not purely UK-specific; global term premium matters. But the UK has a local amplification mechanism through fiscal headroom, QT supply, pension demand and sensitivity to political leadership. If fiscal credibility holds and energy fades, GBP can repair some risk premium. If the Budget cycle reopens fiscal-stress concerns, sterling can weaken even if gilts rally on growth.

The public tape broadly confirms the extended-hold regime: the BoE is not cutting, but the active-hike argument has weakened materially. The real debate is not whether the UK is weak or strong; it is how much weight to put on soft survey deterioration versus still-positive hard-data momentum, and how much services CPI persistence still matters after the BoE marked down its inflation path. Executive Summary

The UK regime is now passive-hold stagflationary deterioration: growth momentum has weakened enough to reduce the probability of near-term BoE tightening, but inflation dynamics remain too unresolved to reopen a clean easing path. The last four weeks changed the balance of the policy trade-off. The 30 April BoE meeting and MPR formalised a hawkish-hold framework around the energy shock, with Bank Rate held at 3.75%, Pill dissenting for a hike, and the inflation projection lifted. The subsequent labour / CPI / PMI / retail sequence then shifted the live regime away from active hike acceleration and toward passive hold: market rates have already tightened financial conditions, while the MPC waits to see whether the shock becomes embedded in wages, services prices and inflation expectations.

The demand signal has deteriorated, but it should not be overstated as a fully confirmed consumer collapse. Labour weakness is more convincing than retail weakness: payrolls fell sharply, vacancies dropped to a five-year low, unemployment rose, and regular pay slowed, though LFS and payroll estimates remain revision-prone. Services PMI contraction and weak CBI orders are high-signal forward indicators, while April retail sales were partly distorted by fuel-stockpile reversal. The correct growth read is therefore services-led contraction risk with consumer weakness emerging, not yet a completed broad hard-data downturn.

The inflation constraint is more concrete than a simple “CPI relief” label suggests. April CPI at 2.8% looks like the trough, not the new run-rate. Core and services CPI surprised lower, but base effects are expected to reverse, the BoE’s own path points to CPI rising through Q2–Q4, and PPI / construction / services-cost pressure keeps the second-round channel live. The binding constraint is still imported-cost persistence and its possible transmission into wages, services pricing and corporate margins.

GBP has lost the clean rate-divergence story. Sterling can still receive intermittent support from BoE stickiness versus the Fed / ECB, especially in GBP/EUR, but that support is now lower quality because political risk premium, fiscal / term-premium stress, gilt volatility and weaker domestic growth are competing drivers. Growth / Activity

The UK growth regime has moved from false-dawn resilience into a weaker, more asymmetric setup. Q1 GDP at +0.6% q/q, March GDP at +0.3% m/m, services resilience, manufacturing output strength, stronger mortgage approvals and firm Nationwide prices all showed that the economy entered the shock with more momentum than feared. But the quality of that strength was poor. A meaningful part of the activity support was backward-looking, pre-shock, or flattered by stockpiling, frontloaded orders, remortgaging and delivery-time distortions.

The forward sequence is materially weaker. CBI Distributive Trades collapsed, construction PMI fell to 39.7, RICS deteriorated to -34, flash composite PMI fell below 50, services PMI dropped to 47.9, and CBI orders weakened to -41. Manufacturing headline resilience is not a clean offset because it is being flattered by precautionary stockbuilding and supply-chain hedging. Construction is the clearest sectoral weak pocket: weak demand, high borrowing costs, material shortages and fuel surcharges are hitting simultaneously.

The consumer signal is weaker, but still less complete than the PMI signal. April retail sales fell 1.3% m/m, but the headline was heavily affected by motor-fuel payback after March stockpiling; ex-fuel sales fell only 0.4%. The GfK major-purchase index and discretionary categories point in the same direction, but the hard-consumption evidence is not yet enough to call a fully confirmed consumer break. The right classification is services-led deterioration with consumer fragility emerging.

The macro implication is still negative. The UK no longer has a clean services floor, housing is rate-sensitive again, construction is deteriorating, and manufacturing strength is low quality. But the next hard-data sequence matters: if May retail, services output and payroll revisions confirm the survey break, the growth channel moves from downside risk to full domestic-demand deterioration.

Labor / Employment

Labor is the strongest domestic argument against near-term BoE tightening. HMRC payrolls fell by 100k in April, the prior payroll reading was revised down, unemployment rose to 5.0%, vacancies fell to a five-year low, and regular ex-bonus earnings slowed to 3.4%. That combination materially weakens the wage-price-spiral risk that would be needed to justify an aggressive pre-emptive policy response.

The caveat is data quality. LFS employment rose strongly, unemployment estimates remain affected by survey reliability concerns, and the latest payroll figure is provisional and revision-prone. The correct read is therefore not “labour has broken,” but labour demand is clearly losing momentum across the cleaner payroll / vacancy / regular-pay indicators. That is enough to raise the hurdle for hikes, but not enough to force cuts.

Wage pressure is no longer the main hawkish impulse. Total pay was lifted by bonuses, but regular pay is the cleaner underlying MPC metric and it softened. Real wage growth is being squeezed by the renewed inflation shock, limiting the 2022-style wage-embedding analogy. For the BoE, this is the key circuit breaker: if the labour market continues to loosen, second-round inflation risk becomes harder to validate.

For the next 3–6 months, labour is the decisive domestic transmission channel. Further payroll declines, weaker vacancies and regular-pay moderation would reopen late-2026 easing optionality. A wage rebound, or evidence that energy costs are feeding into 2027 wage bargaining, would re-activate the Pill / Mann hawkish tail.

Inflation

The inflation regime is temporary CPI relief with expected re-acceleration risk. April CPI, core CPI and services CPI all surprised lower, with headline CPI at 2.8%, core at 2.5% and services at 3.2%. That reduces immediate hike pressure and gives the MPC time to wait. But April looks more like a trough than a new disinflation run-rate.

The forward inflation path is still uncomfortable. The BoE’s April framework points to CPI rising through Q2 and Q3 and rising somewhat further in Q4 on energy and food effects. Private institutional framing is similarly focused on base-effect reversal and energy pass-through. The April print therefore does not solve the reaction-function problem; it delays immediate action while preserving the need to test persistence.

Pipeline pressure remains the key unresolved channel. PPI input prices rose 7.7% y/y and output prices 4.0% y/y, while construction and services PMIs flagged acute cost pressure. CBI selling-price expectations also point to firms attempting pass-through despite weak demand. The immediate risk is margin squeeze; the policy risk is that margin squeeze becomes pricing behaviour.

What is no longer the main issue is broad demand-led overheating. The UK inflation problem is now cost-push persistence and second-round transmission: energy, refined petroleum, freight, construction materials, services input costs, corporate pricing power and wage bargaining. The BoE can tolerate first-round energy effects if temporary. It cannot tolerate them if they become embedded.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Bank of England policy meeting was on 30 April 2026. The MPC left Bank Rate unchanged at 3.75% by an 8–1 vote, with Huw Pill dissenting for a 25bp hike to 4.00%. The April Monetary Policy Report lifted the near-term CPI profile and set out a three-scenario energy-shock framework, with the policy path depending on shock duration, inflation persistence and second-round transmission.

The meeting was an active hawkish hold, not a dovish pause. Pill’s dissent made the hike tail visible, while Mann’s later focus on wage embedding and corporate pricing power kept the persistence channel alive. The BoE’s Scenario B logic matters: even without the worst energy outcome, inflation can stay above target for longer and require a higher rate path than previously priced. The Committee has institutionalised hawkish optionality.

The post-meeting data shifted the near-term execution function toward passive hold. Labour weakness, lower services CPI, services PMI contraction and retail softness give the MPC time to wait, while higher market rates have already tightened financial conditions. Bailey’s framework is not a dovish pivot; it is a time-buying mechanism. The Bank is still waiting for evidence on whether the energy shock embeds into wages, services pricing and expectations.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is Bank Rate held at 3.75%, cuts deferred, hikes conditional. A more dovish path requires energy and shipping risk to fade, PPI pressure to stop broadening, services CPI to remain contained after base effects reverse, payroll weakness to be confirmed, and regular pay to keep slowing. A more hawkish path requires CPI re-acceleration to exceed the expected base-effect rebound, PPI pass-through into services, wage re-acceleration, or higher inflation expectations. The BoE is reacting most to second-round transmission risk, while labour slack and market-rate tightening now limit the case for immediate hikes.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The UK front end has moved from aggressive hike validation risk toward trimmed-but-live hawkish pricing. The market has reduced the probability of a third hike and now prices a less aggressive path, but roughly two hikes remaining in the curve means the hawkish tail is not extinguished. The rates market is therefore no longer a one-way inflation trade; it is balancing labour / PMI weakness against expected CPI re-acceleration and pipeline pass-through.

Financial conditions are tightening through more than the BoE reaction function. Housing and construction remain direct mortgage-rate channels, but gilt term premium and fiscal / political risk have become separate macro variables. The long-end gilt move is not just about Bank Rate expectations; it also reflects fiscal credibility, political uncertainty and risk-premium repricing. This matters because higher long yields tighten financial conditions even if the MPC does nothing.

For FX, GBP has lost the clean policy-divergence narrative. Sterling can still be supported versus lower-yielding or more clearly easing currencies if the BoE remains sticky, making GBP/EUR especially relevant. But GBP/USD and broad sterling are increasingly exposed to UK-specific risk premium: weak services, deteriorating labour quality, fiscal / gilt volatility, and domestic political uncertainty. Rate support is still operative, but it is degraded.

The key FX asymmetry is now conditional. GBP can stabilize if energy risk fades, labour weakness stays orderly, and the BoE remains on hold while the ECB / Fed look more dovish. GBP weakens if markets stop paying for BoE stickiness and start pricing the UK as a stagflation / political-risk currency with impaired growth and unstable term premium.

Political / Policy Events

Political risk is now regime-relevant. It is not yet the dominant macro driver, but it is no longer background noise. Domestic political uncertainty is appearing in the growth, gilt and FX tape through weaker confidence, delayed spending / hiring / investment decisions, and risk-premium pressure around fiscal credibility.

The macro channel is not classic fiscal impulse. It is political uncertainty + fiscal credibility + gilt term premium + GBP risk premium. Labour regional setbacks, leadership speculation around Starmer, and market sensitivity to fiscal headroom are now interacting with the energy shock and BoE constraint. That creates a separate tightening channel: risk premium can rise even if the BoE does not hike.

The policy mix is therefore more fragile than a pure external-energy story would imply. Energy risk still drives the inflation shock, but domestic politics can amplify the financial-conditions channel by weakening confidence, lifting term premium, and reducing the quality of sterling carry. A stabilization in political leadership would remove one GBP-negative channel; further political deterioration would widen the sovereign / fiscal risk premium even if macro data soften.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The dominant market narrative broadly confirms the passive-hold regime: the BoE is no longer being priced as a straightforward near-term hiking story, but the curve has not moved back to an easing cycle. The key tension is calibration. The demand deterioration is real in the survey and labour flow, but the hard consumer break is less settled because April retail was fuel-distorted and ex-fuel weakness was milder than the headline. Conversely, the inflation risk is more concrete than a “soft CPI” framing implies: April is widely treated as the trough, with CPI expected to re-accelerate as base effects unwind and energy / food pass-through arrives. The institutional split is therefore not “hike or cut now”; it is whether the BoE’s Scenario B / Pill-Mann persistence channel survives weaker labour and services data. Political risk is the other non-trivial dispute: it is clearly visible in GBP and gilts, but its durability and macro magnitude are less firmly pinned down than the energy and inflation channels.

Executive Summary

The UK has moved from conditional easing and gradual disinflation into a clearer stagflation bind, but the right policy characterization is suspended-cut hold with hawkish optionality rather than a clean hiking turn. The last four weeks changed the regime in two decisive ways. First, the 19 March BoE meeting reset the reaction function: the Committee moved to a unanimous 9–0 hold, elevated second-round inflation risk from energy persistence, and suspended the live cut debate. Second, the subsequent PMI, CPI, national-accounts and manufacturing sequence showed that the economy entered the shock from a weak domestic base rather than a recovery phase: growth was already close to stall speed, housing remained rate-sensitive, and inflation had not cooled enough to make the shock easy to absorb.

What changed is the balance of risks around the policy path. Late-February still allowed a plausible gradual-cut scenario if wages and services kept easing. By early April, that had become a delayed-cut, hold-longer regime in which the BoE is waiting to see whether the energy shock remains external and temporary or contaminates wages, services and price-setting behavior. What did not change is that the UK still lacks a genuine growth engine. Housing is fragile, construction remains weak, goods activity is soft, and services have slowed enough to threaten the only meaningful floor in the economy.

The binding constraint is now the interaction between imported inflation and weak domestic momentum. This is not yet a broad domestic re-acceleration story: core and services are sticky, not explosively re-heating, and wage disinflation has resumed. But the inflation side also did not clear enough before the shock, leaving the BoE unable to lean dovish into a worsening terms-of-trade backdrop. Base case: Bank Rate stays at 3.75% through 30 April and likely beyond, with cuts delayed rather than cancelled. GBP can retain some support from relative-rate repricing, but that support remains low quality because it rests more on policy delay and positioning than on strong domestic fundamentals.

Growth / Activity

The growth regime is now best described as stagnation, not collapse. Earlier in the year, the UK still had enough services momentum and household resilience to support a soft-landing repricing, but that impulse faded. By mid-March, monthly GDP was flat, services showed no growth, industrial activity remained soft, and housing surveys deteriorated sharply as higher oil prices fed back into mortgage-rate expectations. The late-March and early-April sequence then confirmed that the UK entered the shock with very little domestic cushion. Q4 GDP was only 0.1% q/q, real income dynamics were soft, and external terms of trade worsened rather than improved.

The key distinction is between level and impulse. February credit, mortgage lending and approvals, plus March Nationwide, showed that housing and household-finance transmission were still functioning going into the shock. But those were backward-looking level signals, not fresh forward momentum. The current impulse is weaker: flash services PMI slowed sharply, new orders fell below 50, CBI retail/distributive signals remained poor, GfK softened, and the March manufacturing detail deteriorated materially beneath the still-above-50 headline.

Sectorally, services had been carrying the floor, but that floor is now less secure. Housing remains the clearest rate-sensitive weak pocket, construction is still contractionary, and manufacturing has shifted into a more stagflationary mix in which delivery delays and cost pressure flatter the headline more than underlying demand does. The UK is therefore not in outright downturn yet, but it is operating with very limited shock absorption. That is the right macro backdrop for delayed easing, weaker growth forecasts, and a more persistent policy bind.

Labor / Employment

Labor remains the main medium-term mitigant to the inflation scare. The key sequence was January/February weakening followed by a firmer March labor print. Earlier data showed claimant count rising, HMRC payrolls softening, employment missing expectations, and wage growth easing to 4.2%, which had started to reopen a gradual-cut story. The 19 March labor release then prevented a clean dovish transition: employment beat strongly, unemployment held at 5.2% rather than rising, payrolls improved, and regular pay growth slowed further to 3.8%.

That mix matters because it is neither inflationary enough to force a hike nor weak enough to force a cut. Quantity held up better than feared, wage pressure continued to cool, and the result was exactly the sort of labor backdrop that lets the BoE pause and gather evidence. Since then, there has been no fresh hard labor release to overturn that baseline. The survey layer has weakened materially, but the hard data still say soft-but-stable rather than broken.

For the next 3–6 months, labor is the clearest scenario discriminator. If unemployment drifts higher, payrolls stay soft, and wage growth continues cooling from the 3.8% area, the UK can still return to a delayed easing path once the energy shock fades. If wage disinflation stalls and labor remains tighter than expected while energy stays elevated, the second-round narrative becomes materially more credible. Right now, labor is soft enough to cap a clean hiking story and important enough to keep the medium-horizon cut story alive.

Inflation

The inflation problem is now better described as sticky domestic disinflation colliding with a new imported-cost shock. Earlier in the year, the question was whether services inflation and wages would cool enough to validate gradual cuts. The answer never became clean enough. January CPI brought headline down to 3.0%, but services stayed at 4.4%. February CPI held headline at 3.0%, but core rose to 3.2% and services only eased marginally to 4.3%. That is not domestic re-acceleration, but it is also not the kind of disinflation that gives the BoE room to look through a renewed energy shock.

What is no longer the main problem is broad goods inflation. Pipeline measures had been moderating and producer output prices softened. What has become the main problem is inflation quality. The March flash and final manufacturing sequence made that explicit: input costs saw their biggest month-on-month jump since 1992, supplier delivery delays worsened sharply, and output slipped into contraction territory. That mix points to disruption and cost pressure rather than healthy demand.

The unresolved question is whether the shock remains concentrated in energy, food and imported inputs or starts contaminating domestic price and wage setting. That is why the inflation story is more dangerous than the raw core/services levels alone suggest. The UK is not yet back in a broad domestically driven inflation regime, but it has re-entered a setup in which disinflation can stall at exactly the wrong moment.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Bank of England meeting was on 19 March 2026. The MPC left Bank Rate unchanged at 3.75% and voted 9–0 to hold, compared with the prior cut-biased split. The official message was materially more hawkish than markets had been positioned for. The Committee explicitly flagged greater risk of domestic inflation pressure via second-round wage and price-setting effects if higher energy prices persist, and stated that a larger or more prolonged shock could require a more restrictive stance.

But the meeting should not be read as a clean directional pivot to hiking. The better characterization is a hawkish hold with explicit conditional tightening risk and real internal emphasis on waiting for more evidence. The Committee effectively said two things at once: the shock is serious enough to suspend the cut debate, but the scale and persistence of the shock are still uncertain enough that strong directional inference is premature. Policy is restrictive enough to keep housing and rate-sensitive sectors fragile, but not restrictive enough to dominate the inflation shock immediately.

Base case over the next 3–6 months is prolonged hold at 3.75%, beginning with a high bar for change at the 30 April meeting and a delayed-cut path rather than a near-term hiking cycle. A more dovish path would require energy prices to fall back, wage disinflation to remain intact, labor slack to build further, and services inflation to resume a clearer descent. A more hawkish path would require energy to stay elevated long enough for services/core inflation and wage-price setting to re-firm, not merely for headline inflation to pop. The BoE is reacting most to the interaction between imported-energy persistence and domestic second-round transmission, with labor data acting as the key medium-term circuit breaker.
