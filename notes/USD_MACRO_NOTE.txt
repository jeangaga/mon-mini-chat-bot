Executive Summary

The U.S. regime is best described as policy-constrained stagflationary bifurcation. The last 4 weeks did not produce a recessionary break: claims stayed close to 200k, continuing claims remained contained, retail control held, industrial production rebounded, business inventories were sales-supported, and parts of manufacturing remained expansionary. But the quality of that resilience deteriorated. Services are soft, housing is rate-capped, consumer confidence has capitulated, real-income pressure is visible, and manufacturing strength is partly defensive stockbuilding / supply-chain hedging rather than clean final-demand acceleration.

The major change is the reaction function. The debate has moved beyond delayed cuts. The April FOMC, the minutes, Waller’s 22 May pivot, and market pricing now put the Fed in a hold-or-hike optionality regime. This does not make hikes the base case in a mechanical sense, but it makes the hike tail real rather than rhetorical. Cuts now require either clear disinflation back on track or visible labor-market deterioration. Weak sentiment alone is not enough.

The inflation signal has broadened through CPI, PPI, import prices, PMIs, regional surveys and inflation expectations. But the correct formulation is not “broad core inflation reacceleration already proven.” Realized core strength is still partly contaminated by one-offs and mechanical effects, while energy, tariffs, shipping and supply-chain channels remain the original shock source. The more important policy problem is that pipeline inflation and Michigan 5-year expectations are now high enough to threaten credibility before hard labor data have weakened.

What did not change is labor. The labor market remains low-fire rather than recessionary. Hiring appetite, survey employment, labor flows and mobility have weakened, but claims and continuing claims do not yet validate a second-mandate pivot. That is the floor under higher-for-longer policy.

Rates and FX are now more two-sided than a simple “USD supported” frame. The front end is supported by cuts being priced out and a live hike tail, but the dollar’s safe-haven premium is already contested. USD can stay supported through yield rigidity, Fed constraint and risk premium, but the directional call is no longer one-way: a de-escalation in energy / Hormuz risk, a Warsh-led dovish communication surprise, or a clean labor break would make the dollar vulnerable quickly.

Growth / Activity

Growth is not collapsing, but the composition is weakening. The strongest supports remain backward-looking or nominal: Q1 GDP rose 2.0%, retail control stayed positive, IP rebounded, core capex held up, inventories supported activity, and Empire State orders were strong. Those releases block a hard-landing call.

The forward stack is worse. S&P services barely expanded in May after earlier contraction, the composite PMI did not accelerate, ISM services had already shown weaker business activity and employment, and consumer sentiment deteriorated to crisis-like levels. That matters because services and households are more important for the 3–6 month scenario than inventory-led manufacturing strength.

Housing remains the clearest rate-sensitive weak pocket. NAHB and pending sales showed a demand floor, but mortgage rates re-tightened to 6.56%, single-family permits and starts weakened, and aggregate construction resilience was carried by multi-family. Housing is therefore stabilizing only conditionally: demand exists, but the financing ceiling is binding.

Manufacturing is a false friend. PMI, ISM, Empire State and parts of IP look constructive, but the internal signal is supply-protection rather than clean expansion. Stockbuilding, longer delivery times, price anticipation and supply-chain hedging are carrying part of the strength. That delays recession timing, but worsens the policy-quality signal because it keeps activity alive while prices accelerate.

Labor / Employment

Labor remains the main stabilizer. Initial claims stayed near 200k through the latest window, the four-week average fell toward 202.5k, and continuing claims stayed below 1.8M. That is not a labor-break configuration. The firing channel is still benign.

The deterioration is in labor quality and labor flows. JOLTS showed weaker openings, hires and quits; PMI employment softened; Philly employment weakened; manufacturing employment deteriorated in both S&P and ISM; and small-business / survey indicators point to hiring caution. This is a low-hire / low-fire labor market losing breadth before it loses jobs.

Labor costs do not give the Fed enough disinflation comfort. ECI rose 0.9% q/q in Q1, wages remained firm, and benefits accelerated. This is not a wage-price spiral, but it is also not a clean labor-cost normalization regime.

The practical implication is asymmetric. Labor is not weak enough to force cuts now, but the low-hire structure creates downside convexity later. If final demand weakens further, the transition from hiring freeze to layoffs could be quick. Until claims or unemployment confirm that transition, the Fed remains constrained.

Inflation

Inflation is the dominant regime variable. CPI, PPI, import prices, PCE, GDP price components, trimmed-mean PCE, ISM prices, PMI prices, Empire State prices and Michigan expectations all moved in the wrong direction during the rolling window. The inflation issue is no longer only gasoline.

The binding issue is credibility risk through expectations and pipeline pass-through. Energy, shipping, tariffs and imported-cost pressure remain the shock source, but the pass-through channels are broader: producer margins, trade services, nonfuel imports, survey selling prices, services categories and household inflation expectations. Michigan 5-year expectations at 3.9% are the key policy variable because they force the Fed to treat consumer weakness as a constraint rather than a dovish signal.

The realized-core story is less clean than the headline regime label. Core CPI at 0.4% m/m was hot, but some of the strength appears mechanically distorted and partly one-off. That means the note should not frame the regime as confirmed broad-core reacceleration. The stronger conclusion is that headline, pipeline and expectations inflation have become sufficiently threatening that the Fed cannot wait passively for benign core interpretation.

What is no longer the main problem is pure overheating. Capacity utilization remains below long-run norms, housing is constrained, services are soft, and real consumption quality is weakening. The inflation problem is cost-push plus pass-through risk, not a clean excess-demand boom. The key unresolved question is whether the shock embeds into core and expectations or fades with energy de-escalation.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest Fed policy meeting was 28–29 Apr 2026. The Fed held the target range at 3.50%–3.75%. The mechanical hold was expected, but the meeting was a regime event because of committee fragmentation: four dissents, three hawkish objections to easing-bias language, and visible resistance to preserving a clean “next move = cut” signal.

The April minutes released on 20 May reinforced that this was an institutional shift, not just one speaker. A majority saw policy firming as potentially appropriate if inflation remained persistently above 2%, while many preferred removing the easing-bias language. Waller’s 22 May speech then made the reaction function operational: hikes cannot be ruled out if inflation does not abate, and cuts require either inflation improvement or significant labor deterioration.

Warsh is now a policy wildcard, not a simple hawkish input. He was confirmed on 13 May, Powell’s chair term ended on 15 May, and Warsh’s first FOMC as Chair is the 16–17 Jun meeting. He does not mechanically swing the committee because he replaces the dovish-dissent seat while Powell retains a vote. The near-term implication is institutional uncertainty, not immediate hawkish reinforcement. Political pressure for lower rates sits against a committee whose latest data and communication bias are more restrictive.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is extended hold at 3.50%–3.75%, with cuts deferred and a live hike tail. A more dovish path requires inflation expectations to re-anchor, energy / Hormuz pressure to fade, and CPI / PCE to show limited core pass-through, ideally alongside clearer labor softening. A more hawkish path requires another firm core inflation block, persistent nonfuel import / producer pressure, Michigan 5-year expectations near or above 4%, and claims remaining close to the low-200k regime. The Fed is reacting most to expectations, core pass-through and labor-break evidence, not to weak sentiment in isolation.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is trading policy rigidity: cuts priced out, a live hike tail priced in, and a June FOMC that can either validate or challenge the hold-or-hike regime. The next major repricing trigger is the inflation block, especially CPI / PCE and whether Michigan expectations retrace. Labor is the second trigger, but it needs claims or unemployment confirmation.

The long end now has an independent channel. The note cannot reduce rates to Fed reaction function plus energy. A 30-year yield around multi-decade highs introduces fiscal, supply and term-premium risk into the rates map. That matters for USD and risk assets because long-end pressure can tighten financial conditions even if the Fed only holds.

Financial conditions remain restrictive where they transmit most directly. Mortgage rates in the mid-6s keep housing affordability binding, and the latest move to 6.56% immediately challenged the early-week housing stabilization signals. Housing is therefore a real-economy tightening channel, but not a clean easing trigger because shelter / housing inflation does not adjust quickly enough.

USD is already a live debate. Yield rigidity, fewer Fed cuts and hike-tail pricing support the dollar, but the safe-haven premium is no longer secure. The dollar’s direction depends on whether front-end support and term-premium demand dominate, or whether geopolitical de-escalation, a Warsh dovish surprise and growth deterioration pull the market back toward cuts. USD support is therefore conditional now, not only later.

Political / Policy Events

Yes, political / policy developments are regime-relevant.

The first channel is geopolitical. Iran / Hormuz / energy / shipping risk is the master inflation variable because it drives fuel, logistics, insurance, import prices and inflation expectations. A de-escalation would be the fastest route to a dovish reweighting; persistence would harden the credibility constraint.

The second channel is trade / tariffs / import pass-through. The import-price and PPI stack shows that tradable-goods and margin channels matter. Tariff and supply-chain cost pressure are now part of the inflation map, not a side issue.

The third channel is institutional and fiscal. Warsh’s transition adds policy-communication uncertainty, while fiscal / Treasury supply and term-premium pressure add an independent long-end risk. These channels matter because they can tighten financial conditions without a new Fed hike and can influence USD independently of near-term inflation data.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The dominant public tape broadly confirms the regime call: the Fed is in higher-for-longer hold with two-sided optionality, not a simple delayed-cut cycle. The institutional reaction-function shift is visible in the April minutes, the dissent structure, and Waller’s 22 May pivot, while market pricing has moved toward no cuts in 2026 and a meaningful probability of a December hike. Rates are therefore trading a credibility constraint: labor is too stable to force cuts, inflation expectations are too high to relax, and the front end has to price policy rigidity. The FX implication is more conditional than outright bullish USD: yield support and fewer cuts help the dollar, but the quality of that support depends on whether the risk premium persists.

The real debate is inflation persistence versus shock transience. The pipeline and expectations story is strong: PPI, import prices, survey prices and Michigan 5-year expectations validate the hawkish-hold regime. But the realized-core evidence is less conclusive than the headline narrative suggests, with part of the core CPI strength plausibly distorted by one-offs and mechanical effects. The distribution of views is therefore not “stagflation or no stagflation”; it is whether the current shock becomes embedded enough to require policy defense, or fades before labor and real spending break. The USD debate sits on the same fault line: one camp emphasizes front-end rigidity and relative policy support, while another sees the safe-haven premium already unwinding and a softer-dollar path if energy risk fades and cuts re-enter the curve.

The scenario breakers are concentrated and near-term. The fastest dovish break is geopolitical: Hormuz / energy de-escalation that pulls headline inflation expectations lower before core pass-through hardens. The second is labor: claims sustainably above the low-200k regime and continuing claims moving clearly higher would reopen the cut path even if inflation is not fully benign. The third is the June FOMC under Warsh: a communication stance that validates hold-or-hike optionality would harden the current regime, while an unexpectedly cut-friendly tone would be dollar-negative and would challenge the front-end pricing shift. The fourth is the long end: if fiscal / term-premium pressure keeps pushing yields higher independently of the Fed, financial conditions can tighten enough to damage growth without requiring an actual policy hike.

Bottom Line

The U.S. is in policy-constrained stagflationary bifurcation: resilient hard data and low claims are preventing a dovish pivot, while pipeline inflation and expectations are forcing the Fed to defend credibility. The modal policy path is extended hold, with cuts pushed out and a real hike tail. The main correction is that realized core persistence is not fully proven, and USD support is already contested rather than safely directional. The key swing variables are energy / Hormuz, Michigan expectations, core PCE pass-through, claims, Warsh’s June communication, and the long-end term-premium channel. Base case: Fed holds longer, no near-term easing, hike tail alive, USD two-sided with near-term yield support but rising vulnerability to de-escalation or labor deterioration.

Executive Summary

The last 4 weeks did not produce a full hard-landing turn, but they did materially worsen the growth / inflation trade-off. Forward-looking services data softened, labor-market quality weakened underneath the headlines, housing tightened again through the mortgage-rate channel, and inflation stopped behaving like a one-way disinflation process.

What changed is not that the U.S. suddenly entered full stagflation in a 1970s sense. The more accurate shift is narrower and more operational: the economy now faces softer forward growth and firmer cost pressure at the same time, which makes the Fed’s restrictive hold more durable and pushes the cuts path further out. Backward-looking Q1 support is still visible through payroll optics, retail sales, and manufacturing expansion, but that support increasingly looks lagged, narrow, or flattered by one-offs rather than like the start of a new reacceleration leg.

Layoffs still have not accelerated into a classic recession signal, the labor market is weaker in quality more than in quantity, housing remains the clearest rate-sensitive weak pocket, and the Fed has not formally abandoned later easing. The current policy regime is therefore not “cuts off the table forever,” but “hawkish hold with conditional easing still alive.”

The binding constraint is that growth is not yet weak enough to force a dovish pivot, while inflation has become less cooperative. That inflation problem is also now two-layered. One layer is exogenous and conflict-linked: oil, shipping, tariffs, metals, and supply-cost repricing. The other is domestic and more persistent: sticky services, incomplete labor-cost normalization, and still-firm pricing behavior in the larger domestic economy. That is why the front end cannot easily price early easing, and why USD support remains tied to delayed Fed easing and risk premium rather than to a clean domestic upside regime.

Growth / Activity

Growth has shifted from “mixed but floor-holding” toward “resilient in the rear-view mirror, weaker in the forward stack.” The stronger side of the file is still present: industrial production and manufacturing stabilized earlier in the window, trade arithmetic improved, retail sales rebounded, and payroll headlines prevented a full recession narrative. That matters more than a backward-looking retail bounce because services remain the larger macro bucket and the more relevant read on whether domestic demand is carrying into Q2.

Housing remains the clearest weak pocket and has become more dangerous again. Earlier stabilization signals were false dawns tied to a temporary affordability window. Permits stayed weak, new home sales collapsed, home-price momentum had already slowed, and mortgage rates rose again into quarter-end. That leaves Q2 with a more fragile financing-sensitive base. Labor / Employment

Labor remains the most contested part of the macro picture. The practical macro conclusion is that labor is no longer strong enough to reassure on medium-horizon growth, but not yet weak enough to dominate the Fed reaction function. That is why labor contributes to the note’s softer-forward-growth call without yet justifying an outright dovish regime shift.

Inflation

The inflation regime has worsened, but the distinction between exogenous and endogenous inflation matters more now than in the earlier version of the note. Over the last 4 weeks, PPI, import prices, survey pricing, and ISM Prices Paid all pointed to renewed pipeline pressure. That is a real deterioration and enough to keep the inflation guardrail active.

But the public tape is also correct to separate the recent cost shock from fully embedded domestic inflation persistence. A meaningful share of the latest re-heating still runs through energy, supply disruption, and geopolitical pass-through rather than a clean endogenous domestic overheating story. That matters because an exogenous shock can tighten the near-term Fed constraint without necessarily creating a durable multi-quarter domestic inflation spiral.

The domestic inflation problem, however, is not solved either. Services inflation is still sticky enough, labor-cost normalization is incomplete, and pricing behavior in the larger domestic economy remains firmer than a clean disinflation narrative would require. So the right inflation frame is neither “shock-only and ignorable” nor “fully embedded domestic re-acceleration.” It is a layered inflation problem: exogenous cost shock on top of an already incomplete domestic disinflation process.
