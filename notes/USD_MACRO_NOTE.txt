Executive Summary

The U.S. has moved out of the cleaner soft-landing-with-friction frame and into an inflation-constrained hold regime with mild stagflationary characteristics. The last 4 weeks did not produce a full hard-landing turn, but they did materially worsen the growth / inflation trade-off. Forward-looking services data softened, labor-market quality weakened underneath the headlines, housing tightened again through the mortgage-rate channel, and inflation stopped behaving like a one-way disinflation process.

What changed is not that the U.S. suddenly entered full stagflation in a 1970s sense. The more accurate shift is narrower and more operational: the economy now faces softer forward growth and firmer cost pressure at the same time, which makes the Fed’s restrictive hold more durable and pushes the cuts path further out. Backward-looking Q1 support is still visible through payroll optics, retail sales, and manufacturing expansion, but that support increasingly looks lagged, narrow, or flattered by one-offs rather than like the start of a new reacceleration leg.

What did not change is equally important. Layoffs still have not accelerated into a classic recession signal, the labor market is weaker in quality more than in quantity, housing remains the clearest rate-sensitive weak pocket, and the Fed has not formally abandoned later easing. The current policy regime is therefore not “cuts off the table forever,” but “hawkish hold with conditional easing still alive.”

The binding constraint is that growth is not yet weak enough to force a dovish pivot, while inflation has become less cooperative. That inflation problem is also now two-layered. One layer is exogenous and conflict-linked: oil, shipping, tariffs, metals, and supply-cost repricing. The other is domestic and more persistent: sticky services, incomplete labor-cost normalization, and still-firm pricing behavior in the larger domestic economy. That is why the front end cannot easily price early easing, and why USD support remains tied to delayed Fed easing and risk premium rather than to a clean domestic upside regime.

Growth / Activity

Growth has shifted from “mixed but floor-holding” toward “resilient in the rear-view mirror, weaker in the forward stack.” The stronger side of the file is still present: industrial production and manufacturing stabilized earlier in the window, trade arithmetic improved, retail sales rebounded, and payroll headlines prevented a full recession narrative. That is enough to block an outright hard-landing call in the present tense.

But the quality of the growth signal deteriorated meaningfully as the window progressed. The key development was the service-economy downshift. The March flash PMI had already softened the services impulse; regional services data then weakened sharply; and the final S&P Global services PMI fell into contraction. That matters more than a backward-looking retail bounce because services remain the larger macro bucket and the more relevant read on whether domestic demand is carrying into Q2.

Housing remains the clearest weak pocket and has become more dangerous again. Earlier stabilization signals were false dawns tied to a temporary affordability window. Permits stayed weak, new home sales collapsed, home-price momentum had already slowed, and mortgage rates rose again into quarter-end. That leaves Q2 with a more fragile financing-sensitive base. The correct growth classification is therefore below-trend activity with narrowing support: enough residual Q1 resilience to block a recession call now, but not enough forward strength to sustain a renewed soft-landing upgrade.

Labor / Employment

Labor remains the most contested part of the macro picture. The public tape is still led by the March payroll headline and lower unemployment rate, which keeps the dominant market narrative closer to labor resilience than labor break. That matters because a clean labor deterioration would be the easiest trigger for Fed repricing, and the latest headline did not deliver it.

But underneath the headline, labor quality has weakened. JOLTS signaled softer flows, weaker mobility, and lower hiring appetite; ADP remained narrow in composition; participation fell; U6 rose; and broader labor quality measures stayed weak. That does not amount to a classic recession-cleansing labor market, because layoffs are still not accelerating in a decisive way. But it does argue that the labor market is softening through weaker absorption and weaker breadth rather than through a firing wave.

The practical macro conclusion is that labor is no longer strong enough to reassure on medium-horizon growth, but not yet weak enough to dominate the Fed reaction function. That is why labor contributes to the note’s softer-forward-growth call without yet justifying an outright dovish regime shift.

Inflation

The inflation regime has worsened, but the distinction between exogenous and endogenous inflation matters more now than in the earlier version of the note. Over the last 4 weeks, PPI, import prices, survey pricing, and ISM Prices Paid all pointed to renewed pipeline pressure. That is a real deterioration and enough to keep the inflation guardrail active.

But the public tape is also correct to separate the recent cost shock from fully embedded domestic inflation persistence. A meaningful share of the latest re-heating still runs through energy, supply disruption, and geopolitical pass-through rather than a clean endogenous domestic overheating story. That matters because an exogenous shock can tighten the near-term Fed constraint without necessarily creating a durable multi-quarter domestic inflation spiral.

The domestic inflation problem, however, is not solved either. Services inflation is still sticky enough, labor-cost normalization is incomplete, and pricing behavior in the larger domestic economy remains firmer than a clean disinflation narrative would require. So the right inflation frame is neither “shock-only and ignorable” nor “fully embedded domestic re-acceleration.” It is a layered inflation problem: exogenous cost shock on top of an already incomplete domestic disinflation process.

That distinction matters. The meeting did not say the Fed had shifted to a renewed tightening bias. It said the Fed could not ease into a geopolitically driven cost shock while the domestic economy was still resilient enough to avoid a growth emergency. Policy is still restrictive rather than neutral, and the reaction function remains conditional: the Fed is waiting to see whether the inflation re-heating passes through into core and whether labor / growth deterioration becomes broad enough to offset it.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

The Fed is reacting most to whether the recent cost shock embeds into measured core and whether forward weakness becomes visible enough in labor and spending to alter the balance.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The dominant rates narrative is still restrictive hold under asymmetric uncertainty: the Fed cannot cut easily, but the market also does not yet have grounds to price a fresh hiking cycle. What matters most for repricing from here is whether the next CPI / PCE block validates the import-price / PPI / ISM cost shock and whether labor deterioration broadens beyond quality weakness into more visible slack creation.

Financial conditions are restrictive and have tightened again through the housing channel. Mortgage rates rising back up after housing had already lost momentum is one of the clearest real-economy transmission mechanisms in the file. The geopolitical risk premium also matters now through yields, oil, and uncertainty, which means financial conditions are being tightened not only by domestic policy but by the external shock overlay.

For USD, the correct framing is conditional and two-layered. Near term, the dollar is supported by safe-haven demand, delayed Fed easing, and relative U.S. resilience. But that support is not the same as a clean structural bullish USD story. Underneath it, the medium-term structural bear case can re-emerge if the conflict premium fades, rate differentials compress later in the year, and the U.S. growth slowdown becomes more visible. So USD strength belongs to the “Fed constrained + risk premium” branch, not to a durable domestic reacceleration branch.

So politics matters now in two ways: first through the external cost shock and risk premium; second through institutional uncertainty around the Fed leadership transition. It still does not operate as the main domestic growth driver, but it is no longer negligible.

Financial conditions have not tightened enough to force a broad downturn. The transmission of restrictive policy continues to run mainly through housing and other interest-sensitive segments, not through a generalized credit event.

Labor

Labor is softening more clearly, but it is still not breaking in a recessionary way.

The labor signal has shifted from late-cycle cooling toward more visible softening. Payrolls at -92k and unemployment at 4.4% matter. But the more important structural point is that hiring and breadth are weakening faster than layoffs are exploding. That is still a softer deterioration path than a classic recession break.

Claims help keep that distinction intact. Claims have moved up from prior trough levels, but they remain contained enough that the latest payroll weakness cannot yet be read as a full labor-market break. The better read is ongoing deterioration through slower hiring, weaker breadth, and gradually rising slack.

That is enough to keep later easing in play. It is not yet enough to force immediate Fed action.

Inflation

Inflation remains mixed, sticky, and incomplete, not regime-breaking.

The key point is unchanged: the binding inflation issue is not goods/imports. It is services prices, wage persistence, labor-cost dynamics, and survey price components. That is why softer labor data alone are still insufficient to force early easing.

The recent mix is awkward rather than benign. Services activity has strengthened materially, which keeps demand alive in the stickiest part of the economy. Manufacturing prices jumped to 70.5, which revives the input-cost tail. That does not overturn the broader disinflation trend, but it does keep the Fed from treating current inflation as fully cooperative.

Productivity and ULC matter here. Productivity has been strong enough to absorb part of the wage pressure, which reduces the risk of a sustained wage-price spiral. But the balance is still not soft enough to declare labor-cost inflation solved. That is the right way to frame the inflation backdrop: better, but not clean.

Fed / Rates / USD

The Fed reaction function remains broadly stable.

The current mix still argues for hold / plateau first:

services are strong enough to block imminent recession pricing,

labor is softening enough to preserve easing optionality,

inflation is improving, but not cleanly enough to validate aggressive cuts.

Cuts therefore still require two conditions:

broader labor cooling;

cleaner progress in services/core inflation.

For rates, the front end is still trading labor plus inflation, not manufacturing by itself. Manufacturing stabilization only matters if it broadens into stronger growth or feeds into inflation persistence.

For USD, the setup remains conditional. The dollar is supported when the market focuses on delayed easing and relative U.S. resilience. It weakens when labor downside becomes the dominant signal and pulls the cuts path forward.

Bottom Line

The U.S. is still not recessionary, but not cleanly re-accelerating. Services and domestic demand hold the floor; housing remains weak; manufacturing is improving but not yet convincingly. Labor has moved from low-hire / low-fire toward clearer softening, but not confirmed collapse. Inflation is still easing, but not cleanly enough in services/cost channels to force early Fed easing.

Base case: plateau first, cuts later if labor and inflation cooperate.
