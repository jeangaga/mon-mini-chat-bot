USD Macro Note — PM Version
As of: 10 Mar 2026 (Paris)
Horizon: rolling 4-week impulse into 3–6 month scenario

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ Mixed / services-led floor
Labor: ↓ Softening
Inflation: ~ Disinflation continues, but sticky in services/cost channels
Financial conditions: ~ Not restrictive enough to force downturn; housing still biting
Policy bias: Hold / plateau first → easing optionality later

Executive Summary

The U.S. remains in a soft-landing-with-friction regime: not recessionary, but not cleanly re-accelerating. Services and domestic demand still hold the floor; housing remains the clearest weak pocket; manufacturing has improved, but not with enough quality to justify a full upside regime shift.

The main change in the 4-week impulse is labor. The regime has moved from low-hire / low-fire toward clearer softening. ADP was soft, payrolls printed -92k, unemployment rose to 4.4%, and hiring breadth has narrowed. That is enough to strengthen easing optionality, but not enough yet to call a full labor break, especially with temporary distortions still in the mix and claims not yet signaling a classic layoffs shock.

Inflation is still moving in the right direction, but not cleanly enough to unlock early easing. The binding channel remains services, wages, ULC, and survey price components. Goods/import inflation is not the main policy problem. Stronger services activity and a rise in manufacturing prices keep the inflation tail alive even as labor softens.

Core policy conclusion is unchanged: plateau first, cuts later only if labor cooling broadens and inflation progress becomes cleaner. Front-end pricing is still driven much more by labor and inflation than by manufacturing alone. USD support holds when delayed easing dominates, but fades when labor downside becomes the main macro signal.

Growth

Growth is holding above recessionary territory, but breadth is still uneven.

The strongest part of the economy remains services and private domestic demand. ISM Services at 56.1 is consistent with a real activity floor, not a recession signal. Retail and GDP-related demand signals still fit the same message: demand bending, not breaking. Headline growth slowed, but private demand has held up better than the top-line GDP optics suggest.

Manufacturing is no longer the clean drag it was, but it is not yet a trusted growth engine. ISM Manufacturing at 52.4 and a firmer Philly Fed support a short-cycle stabilization story. But the quality of that rebound is still mixed. Seasonal effects, reorder activity, tariff-front-running, and survey volatility all cap conviction. Goods are improving; they are not yet leading a new cycle.

Housing remains the clearest weak pocket. NAHB at 36, softer home sales, and slowing home-price momentum all say the same thing: policy transmission is still working most visibly through affordability and financing-sensitive sectors.

Financial conditions have not tightened enough to force a broad downturn. The transmission of restrictive policy continues to run mainly through housing and other interest-sensitive segments, not through a generalized credit event.

Labor

Labor is softening more clearly, but it is still not breaking in a recessionary way.

The labor signal has shifted from late-cycle cooling toward more visible softening. Payrolls at -92k and unemployment at 4.4% matter. But the more important structural point is that hiring and breadth are weakening faster than layoffs are exploding. That is still a softer deterioration path than a classic recession break.

Claims help keep that distinction intact. Claims have moved up from prior trough levels, but they remain contained enough that the latest payroll weakness cannot yet be read as a full labor-market break. The better read is ongoing deterioration through slower hiring, weaker breadth, and gradually rising slack.

That is enough to keep later easing in play. It is not yet enough to force immediate Fed action.

Inflation

Inflation remains mixed, sticky, and incomplete, not regime-breaking.

The key point is unchanged: the binding inflation issue is not goods/imports. It is services prices, wage persistence, labor-cost dynamics, and survey price components. That is why softer labor data alone are still insufficient to force early easing.

The recent mix is awkward rather than benign. Services activity has strengthened materially, which keeps demand alive in the stickiest part of the economy. Manufacturing prices jumped to 70.5, which revives the input-cost tail. That does not overturn the broader disinflation trend, but it does keep the Fed from treating current inflation as fully cooperative.

Productivity and ULC matter here. Productivity has been strong enough to absorb part of the wage pressure, which reduces the risk of a sustained wage-price spiral. But the balance is still not soft enough to declare labor-cost inflation solved. That is the right way to frame the inflation backdrop: better, but not clean.

Fed / Rates / USD

The Fed reaction function remains broadly stable.

The current mix still argues for hold / plateau first:

services are strong enough to block imminent recession pricing,

labor is softening enough to preserve easing optionality,

inflation is improving, but not cleanly enough to validate aggressive cuts.

Cuts therefore still require two conditions:

broader labor cooling;

cleaner progress in services/core inflation.

For rates, the front end is still trading labor plus inflation, not manufacturing by itself. Manufacturing stabilization only matters if it broadens into stronger growth or feeds into inflation persistence.

For USD, the setup remains conditional. The dollar is supported when the market focuses on delayed easing and relative U.S. resilience. It weakens when labor downside becomes the dominant signal and pulls the cuts path forward.

Trigger Map

More dovish / cuts pulled forward

another weak labor print,

broader hiring weakness,

unemployment drifting higher again,

cleaner services/core disinflation.

Hold-longer / mildly hawkish reweighting

services stay firm,

wage and cost measures stop improving,

manufacturing price pressure persists,

labor softness remains narrow or distorted.

More constructive growth scenario

manufacturing stabilization broadens,

housing stops deteriorating,

private domestic demand stays firm without renewed inflation pressure.

Bottom Line

The U.S. is still not recessionary, but not cleanly re-accelerating. Services and domestic demand hold the floor; housing remains weak; manufacturing is improving but not yet convincingly. Labor has moved from low-hire / low-fire toward clearer softening, but not confirmed collapse. Inflation is still easing, but not cleanly enough in services/cost channels to force early Fed easing.

Base case: plateau first, cuts later if labor and inflation cooperate.
