CHF Macro Note — Zero-Rate Hold, Ambiguous FX Conditionality, Hormuz False-Dawn Risk

As of: 2 July 2026
Data window: 23 May 2026 to 22 June 2026; public-tape extension through 2 July for Hormuz / FSR
Horizon: 3–6 months
Central bank: Swiss National Bank
FX relevance: CHF, mainly EUR/CHF and broad safe-haven CHF crosses

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ Stable but not domestic-demand-led
Labor: ~ Low-signal; not a policy driver
Inflation: ~ Headline firmer, core near floor
Financial cond: ~ CHF channel dominant; intervention evidence inconclusive
Policy bias: Hold at 0%; FX intervention if necessary; hike-tail live but unvalidated
Political risk: ~ Hormuz unresolved / false-dawn risk; UBS-FSR tail reduced

Executive Summary

Switzerland remains a low-inflation, zero-rate, FX-managed safe-haven file. The June MPA confirmed the base case: SNB hold at 0.00%, medium-term inflation still low at 0.6% / 0.6% / 0.7% for 2026–2028, and no validation of a near-term rate-hike regime. The policy lever remains CHF, not domestic rates.

The important 4-week change is not a macro breakout; it is the distribution around the hold. Negative-rate risk has faded because CPI is no longer at the deflation edge and activity is resilient enough, but the market still carries a live normalization / hike-tail if energy and CHF dynamics turn. The “if necessary” language on intervention is real, but it should be treated as deliberate ambiguity rather than a clean dovish-on-FX pivot: the SNB did not validate an insurance-only interpretation.

The binding constraint is CHF-driven financial conditions at the zero-rate floor. Stronger Q1 activity and better PMI reduce easing urgency, but the GDP composition / domestic-demand channel is not strong enough to create a recovery-led inflation story. Inflation is still narrow and oil-led, with core near the floor. That leaves the SNB reacting to the conditional inflation forecast, EUR/CHF, energy duration and safe-haven pressure rather than current headline CPI alone.

The main scenario variable is Hormuz, not domestic politics. The in-window oil retreat and partial reopening softened the franc bid, but the shock is not falsified: commercial reopening remains fragile, toll / clearance risk returned, mine risk persists, and insurance normalization is not clean. UBS / financial stability is less of a 3–6 month policy constraint after the July FSR signalled banking resilience, though the UBS capital debate remains a political overhang.

Growth / Activity

Swiss growth is more resilient than the “low-inflation” label alone implies, but it is not a broad domestic-demand recovery. KOF printed 98.0, exactly in line and still below the 100 medium-term average. That is stabilization below trend, not a cyclical upswing.

Q1 GDP resilience and May PMI improvement remove immediate easing pressure. They show that Switzerland is not in an outright downturn and that industry / services have held better than early-year fragility suggested. The caveat is composition: the public tape points to weak domestic final demand beneath the stronger headline GDP read. That reinforces the prior retail message: the Swiss recovery is not consumer-led.

Manufacturing remains a useful stabilization signal, but not a clean acceleration signal. PMI improvement and better orders / production reduce recession risk, while elevated delivery times and supply-chain effects keep the signal quality contested. The correct growth state is resilient stabilization with weak domestic breadth.

Labor / Employment

Labor remains low-signal for CHF. There was no standalone labor-market release in the 23 May–22 June window that changed the policy map. The SNB noted unemployment was up somewhat, but not enough to become binding.

The key distinction is between registered unemployment stability and broader labor softness measures. The policy-relevant point is unchanged: there is no labor overheating channel and no labor-market collapse channel. Labor does not force the SNB in either direction.

For the reaction function, labor is secondary to CHF, inflation forecasts, external demand and energy shock duration. It matters only if it begins transmitting into lower domestic demand and lower medium-term inflation forecasts.

Inflation

Inflation is still too low to validate a conventional hawkish SNB story. May CPI rose to 0.6% y/y, but the move was mainly oil / energy-related and core was only 0.3%. The SNB can look through that configuration because the medium-term inflation path is still close to the low end of price stability.

The June forecast is the anchor. Inflation at 0.6% in 2026, 0.6% in 2027 and 0.7% in 2028 is not a tightening profile. It confirms that energy has lifted the near-term path without generating broad domestic persistence.

The unresolved issue is whether energy remains benign reflation or becomes a stagflationary constraint. For Switzerland, higher oil can initially help lift too-low inflation, but a renewed Hormuz shock that lifts headline and core together would change the distribution. Conversely, a durable oil reversal and renewed CHF appreciation would reopen the disinflation / easing tail.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest SNB Monetary Policy Assessment was on 18 June 2026. The SNB held the policy rate at 0.00%, with no rate surprise. Sight deposits continued to be remunerated at the policy rate up to the threshold, with the discount above the threshold unchanged at 0.25pp.

The meeting confirmed a zero-rate hold with low medium-term inflation pressure. The SNB did not follow the ECB into rate action, and it did not validate hawkish market pricing. It also did not reopen immediate negative-rate risk. The policy stance remains expansionary in rate terms, but conditional through the FX channel.

The intervention language is the nuance. “If necessary” was added to the increased willingness to intervene against rapid / excessive CHF appreciation. That is not a full retreat from intervention. It is a deliberately conditional stance: rate differentials, EUR support and partial geopolitical relief had already weakened CHF, so the SNB could avoid pressing the intervention signal while preserving the option to act quickly if CHF appreciation resumes.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case for the next 3–6 months is hold at 0.00%, with FX intervention conditional on renewed rapid / excessive CHF appreciation. A more dovish path requires CHF strength, CPI undershoot, a lower medium-term conditional inflation forecast and renewed activity softness; intervention comes before negative rates, and negative rates remain a crisis tail. A more hawkish path requires CHF staying soft, activity remaining resilient, energy pressure persisting, and core / medium-term inflation moving higher enough to validate the market’s normalization tail. The SNB is reacting most to CHF-driven financial conditions and the conditional inflation forecast, not to headline CPI alone.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The Swiss front end is anchored around the 0% hold, but the distribution is not one-sided. Consensus holds the policy rate flat through 2026 and likely into 2027, while pre-MPA pricing still carried close to one hike by year-end. The June MPA did not validate that pricing, but it also confirmed that negative-rate risk has faded. The curve is therefore trading a hold base case with a live but data-dependent normalization tail.

For CHF, the main driver is not Swiss domestic rates. It is the interaction between ECB-SNB spreads, EUR/CHF, global risk and SNB intervention risk. ECB tightening / euro-area rate support reduces CHF appreciation pressure and lets the SNB stay passive. Risk-off / Hormuz re-escalation does the opposite by strengthening CHF and tightening Swiss monetary conditions through the exchange rate.

Reserve data do not prove active intervention. May FX reserves fell by roughly CHF 5bn, which does not fit an aggressive reserve-accumulation story, but valuation, gold / SDR and exchange-rate effects prevent a clean transaction read. The clean intervention audit is quarterly FX-transaction data, not monthly reserves alone; sight deposits are useful but imperfect proxies.

Financial conditions are not restrictive through the domestic rate channel. They operate through CHF, imported inflation, external demand and risk premia. EUR/CHF around 0.90–0.92 remains the intervention-sensitive zone: above it, conditional language can dominate; toward 0.90 with momentum, the SNB reaction function becomes much more concrete. The US currency-manipulation constraint adds an operational ceiling to large-scale intervention: sustained intervention around CHF 40bn over 12 months would reopen political optics risk.

Political / Policy Events

No domestic Swiss political event reset the 3–6 month macro regime. The relevant policy risk is external: Hormuz durability, energy prices, safe-haven CHF demand, ECB spillovers and US optics around SNB intervention.

Hormuz is the central political / geopolitical macro variable. The partial reopening and oil retreat reduced immediate franc pressure in-window, but the reopening is not a clean falsification event. Convoy-scale commercial transit, normalized war-risk insurance and durable non-reimposition of toll / clearance barriers are not yet established. That makes the geopolitical impulse a false-dawn risk rather than a resolved de-escalation.

The July Financial Stability Report reduces a potential blind spot. The SNB’s message that Swiss banks are resilient and UBS is already well capitalised against tougher requirements lowers the probability that financial-stability caution constrains monetary policy in the near term. The UBS capital bill remains a political overhang, but it is not the dominant CHF driver over the next 3–6 months.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The public tape broadly confirms the CHF regime: zero-rate hold, low medium-term inflation and FX as the active monetary-conditions channel. Consensus is still not a conventional SNB tightening cycle; the June MPA held at 0%, kept inflation forecasts low through 2028 and left growth forecasts moderate. EUR/CHF is therefore less a Swiss domestic-data trade than an ECB-spread / global-risk / SNB-reaction-function trade. The rates implication is a flat 0% base case, with negative rates pushed into a crisis tail and the front end only able to reprice materially if the inflation forecast rather than headline oil alone moves higher.

The real disagreement is around conditionality and the hike-tail. “If necessary” matters, but it is not a clean dovish-on-FX pivot because the SNB deliberately avoided saying whether intervention readiness had fallen. The market read the wording and franc weakness as marginal relief, while the central bank preserved ambiguity. At the same time, the note cannot dismiss normalization as only a distant 2027 issue: pre-MPA pricing carried close to one hike by year-end, reflecting the view that the SNB can move early if energy and CHF dynamics stop suppressing inflation. Against that, low core inflation, weak domestic-demand breadth and a still-low forecast remain the strongest arguments for fading actual near-term hikes.

The unresolved scenario breakers are narrow but decisive. Hormuz is not falsified: partial reopening and lower oil helped in-window price action, but toll / clearance uncertainty, mine risk and incomplete insurance normalization keep the shock open. A durable reopening would weaken oil, reduce the inflation tail and leave CHF more exposed to rate differentials; a re-closure would lift energy and safe-haven CHF together, forcing the SNB to choose between tolerating stronger CHF and leaning harder through intervention. The clean intervention audit also remains unresolved until quarterly FX-transaction data; monthly reserves and sight deposits are useful but valuation-contaminated. The July FSR lowers financial-stability risk as a near-term policy constraint, so the main regime gates remain Hormuz, EUR/CHF, June / July CPI and the September MPA forecast path.

Bottom Line

Switzerland is a low-inflation stabilization case with a 0% hold base case and an explicitly conditional FX-management regime. The June MPA did not validate hikes, but it also left a live normalization tail because negative-rate risk has faded and market pricing remains sensitive to energy / CHF dynamics. Growth is resilient enough to avoid easing pressure, but not domestic-demand-led enough to create a recovery-inflation story. CHF remains conditionally supported in risk-off, but rapid appreciation faces intervention risk, especially toward the 0.90–0.92 EUR/CHF zone. Hormuz is the key open macro-political variable and should be treated as unresolved, not cleanly de-escalated.

Base case: SNB holds at 0.00% over the next 3–6 months, keeps FX intervention as conditional insurance, and lets CHF trade through ECB spreads, global risk and whether Hormuz / EUR/CHF force a policy-response reweighting.

CHF Macro Note — Low-Inflation Stabilization, FX-Managed Safe Haven

As of: 27 May 2026
Horizon: 3–6 months
Central bank: Swiss National Bank
FX relevance: CHF, mainly EUR/CHF and broad safe-haven CHF crosses

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ / ↑ conditional stabilization, but PMI quality contested
Labor: ~ stable, not a policy driver
Inflation: ~ headline up, core still very low
Financial cond: ~ CHF / EUR-CHF channel dominant, rates channel secondary
Policy bias: Hold at 0% + active FX intervention preference
Political risk: ~ external geopolitical / energy shock relevant; US intervention-friction tail present

Executive Summary

Switzerland remains a low-inflation stabilization case, but the public tape makes the cross-asset asymmetry more two-sided than a simple dovish / negative-rate framework. The base case is still SNB hold at 0%, with FX intervention preferred to negative rates if CHF strength becomes excessive. April CPI at 0.6% y/y validates that hold: headline inflation is no longer at the deflation edge, but core inflation remains low enough to let the SNB downplay the move.

The last 4-week impulse improved the activity picture, but not cleanly. KOF rebounded and manufacturing PMI rose further into expansion, yet the PMI signal is heavily contested because supplier delivery times and supply-chain disruption may be inflating the headline. Retail sales remain the weak domestic-demand check: the headline was fuel-masked, ex-service-station sales were flat, and durable-goods categories were soft. The growth regime is therefore stabilization from weak levels, not a self-sustaining domestic recovery.

The rates / FX story is no longer only “hold versus negative rates.” The market tape carries a live hold versus hike-tail debate because energy / Hormuz persistence has pushed some tightening premium into the Swiss curve, even as several strategists still reject the idea of SNB hikes. CHF remains policy-managed, but the marginal EUR/CHF driver is increasingly ECB pricing with the SNB pinned at 0%. Operationally, EUR/CHF around 0.90 is the key intervention threshold; below that zone, the FX-management regime becomes much less theoretical.

Growth / Activity

The growth signal has improved, but from a weak base and with lower signal quality than the PMI headline implies. The March/early-April configuration was internally conflicted: KOF collapsed below 100 and warned of broad forward deterioration, while manufacturing PMI rebounded into expansion. The latest window partially repaired that tension: KOF rebounded to 97.9 and manufacturing PMI rose to 54.5.

The problem is that the activity improvement is not clean. PMI internals in the WEEK tape showed better orders, production and purchasing volumes, but the public narrative gives more weight to delivery-time distortion and supply-chain disruption. That makes the PMI a stabilization signal, not a clean growth acceleration signal. It reduces recession risk; it does not prove that the Swiss manufacturing cycle has normalized.

Domestic demand remains the weak pocket. Retail sales were positive at the headline level but poor in composition, with fuel doing too much of the work and durable categories weak. Switzerland has moved away from outright industrial weakness, but the recovery is not broad enough to create a domestic-demand-led macro story.

Labor / Employment

Labor is stable and low-signal. Adjusted unemployment held at 3.0% in both March and April. That does not indicate overheating, but it also does not show a domestic labor-market break that would force a dovish SNB shift.

The earlier move from 2.9% to 3.0% was a mild softening signal, but it has not developed into deterioration. There is no visible layoff cycle, no wage-pressure problem, and no labor channel that dominates the reaction function.

For the SNB, labor remains a background stabilizer. It keeps the macro backdrop from becoming recessionary, but policy is still driven by CPI, CHF, external shocks and the conditional inflation forecast.

Inflation

Inflation remains low, contained and policy-friendly. The sequence moved from near-zero inflation early in the year to 0.3% y/y in March and 0.6% y/y in April. That is a meaningful move away from the deflation edge, but not a domestic persistence problem.

The key detail is the split between headline and core. Headline CPI rose on energy / import / housing-adjacent channels, while core inflation stayed extremely low. That is exactly the configuration that lets the SNB hold at 0% and downplay the April pickup: imported inflation is visible, but domestic inflation pressure is not broad enough to justify hawkish policy.

The binding inflation channel is therefore energy / import / FX pass-through, not wages, services persistence or domestic demand overheating. The unresolved question is whether a prolonged energy shock pushes headline and core inflation higher together. If core stays subdued, the SNB can stay patient. If energy persistence bleeds into the medium-term forecast, the market’s hike-tail pricing becomes harder to dismiss.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest SNB Monetary Policy Assessment was on 19 March 2026. The SNB left the policy rate unchanged at 0% and kept the conditional inflation forecast inside price stability over the forecast horizon, assuming the policy rate remains at 0%. The operative signal was not the unchanged rate alone; it was the FX-management hierarchy around CHF strength and safe-haven pressure.

The subsequent communication sharpened that reaction function. Negative rates remain available, but the bar is high. The preferred first-line tool against excessive CHF appreciation is FX intervention, and the public tape has moved beyond pure “readiness” language: the SNB has been willing to buy foreign currency and keep that tool active if needed. This makes CHF a policy-managed safe haven, not a pure risk-off currency.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case for the next 3–6 months is hold at 0%, with active FX intervention preference and negative rates as a high-bar tail rather than the central path. A more dovish path requires CHF appreciation, CPI undershoot, a lower conditional inflation forecast and renewed downside pressure on activity. A more hawkish path requires persistent energy / Hormuz pressure, headline inflation moving above the current benign range, and core inflation no longer validating the SNB’s downplay. The SNB is reacting most to CHF strength, medium-term inflation forecasts, imported inflation risk and external shock duration, not to domestic overheating.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The Swiss front end is trading a two-tailed distribution around a hold base case. The economist / strategist consensus is still that the SNB does not need to move soon, but market pricing has recently embedded a tightening premium as the energy shock raised the probability of a hawkish tail. The older negative-rate tail has not disappeared entirely, but it is no longer the only risk around the 0% base case.

That changes the macro asymmetry. The rates market is not simply asking whether the SNB returns to negative rates; it is asking whether a persistent energy shock forces the SNB to tolerate stronger CHF and potentially validate hike risk. Against that, low core inflation and weak domestic demand argue that the hurdle for actual hikes remains high.

For FX, EUR/CHF is increasingly a function of ECB pricing and European rate spreads because the SNB is pinned at 0%. CHF safe-haven demand still matters, but it is being actively managed through verbal and balance-sheet intervention. EUR/CHF around 0.90 is the operational threshold: above it, the SNB can stay mostly communicative; below it, intervention risk becomes the dominant CHF constraint.

Financial conditions are not restrictive through the domestic rate channel. They operate through CHF, external demand and imported inflation. That puts EUR/CHF, ECB policy, energy shock duration, sight deposits, reserves and SNB language ahead of most domestic Swiss releases in the cross-asset hierarchy.

Political / Policy Events

There were no domestic political developments in the last 4-week window that reset the Swiss macro regime. The relevant policy risk is external: Middle East / Hormuz duration, energy prices, ECB policy spillover, CHF safe-haven demand and the political tolerance for SNB intervention.

US currency-friction is a live constraint on the scale and optics of SNB intervention. It does not eliminate the SNB’s willingness to act, but it matters if intervention becomes large, persistent and visible. The SNB’s preferred tool is FX management, but heavy intervention can reopen international political pressure.

A secondary domestic-policy tail is the population-cap referendum debate. It is not a central macro driver yet, but it fits a broader restrictive-supply narrative that could matter at the margin for housing, labor supply and longer-run CHF structural support. For now, it remains a background risk rather than a 3–6 month scenario driver.
