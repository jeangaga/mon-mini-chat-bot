A. NOK WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

Norway’s merged 9 Mar–3 Apr window moved from inflation stabilisation to outright policy escalation. The 10 March CPI print initially reduced the immediate hawkish tail: headline CPI fell to 2.7% y/y from 3.6%, and core CPI-ATE eased to 3.0% from 3.4%. But the print was not a clean dovish reset because monthly core accelerated to 0.7%, leaving Norges Bank with still-too-high underlying inflation and no evidence that sequential pressure had collapsed.

The decisive regime shift came on 26 March. Norges Bank held the policy rate at 4.0%, but the hold itself was secondary: the rate path flipped from a cuts framework to a hikes framework, citing higher inflation, stronger wage growth, higher ULC and greater uncertainty. Credit was still firm, registered unemployment remained stable, and the softer offsets — LFS unemployment up to 4.9% and retail sales ex-auto down 1.1% — were not enough to invalidate the hawkish pivot. No Norway releases were present in the supplied 30 Mar–3 Apr file, so the final week added no new NOK signal.

A. NORWAY WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

Norway’s merged window delivered a clear sequence: first a major inflation shock, then a mixed post-shock activity check that reduced but did not eliminate eventual easing optionality. The 10 February CPI print was the defining event. Headline CPI rose to 3.6% y/y versus 3.1% consensus, and core CPI-ATE reached 3.4% versus 3.0% consensus. The surprise was large, broad enough to matter, and institutionally interpreted as persistent rather than purely seasonal because housing/utilities, transport, restaurants/accommodation, rent mechanics and reduced seasonal discounting all contributed. The immediate reaction-function implication was a later and shallower Norges Bank easing path.

The activity data that followed did not add a fresh hawkish impulse, but it also did not overturn the CPI shock. Mainland GDP held at +0.4% q/q, confirming steady rather than weak domestic activity. Credit growth eased only marginally, labour softened on the LFS measure but registered unemployment stayed stable, and retail sales ex-auto rebounded. The dovish elements were concentrated in housing and industry: house-price momentum slowed from 4.2% to 3.0% y/y, and manufacturing output fell for a second consecutive month. The resulting regime is therefore “January CPI shock pushes cuts out; activity mixed and mildly softer afterward; housing and manufacturing beginning to transmit restrictive policy; easing optionality reduced but not closed.”
