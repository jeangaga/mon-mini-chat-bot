A. UNITED KINGDOM WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS
The UK macro sequence across this window resolved into a decisive hawkish-hold regime shift, driven by the 19 March MPC decision but prepared by the labour and housing data that preceded it. The week opened with Rightmove confirming that the housing market remained highly rate-sensitive: a normal spring seasonal bounce in asking prices occurred alongside an 11-year supply high, the longest average time-to-sale since 2013, and a renewed rise in the two-year fixed mortgage rate to 4.51%. That framing — market not broken, but not absorbing tighter financing conditions well — set the transmission-channel context for everything that followed.
The decisive turn came on 19 March. The labour release gave the MPC the cover it needed: employment beat strongly, unemployment held at 5.2% rather than rising to the 5.3% consensus, and regular-pay growth slowed further to 3.8%. Quantity firmer than feared, wage pressure softer — the ideal pre-decision mix for a hawkish hold without a hike. The MPC then delivered exactly that: a 9–0 unanimous hold at 3.75%, against February's 5–4 split with four members voting to cut. All four prior cut voters moved back to hold. The official minutes explicitly flagged the increased risk of domestic inflationary pressures through second-round wage and price-setting effects if higher energy prices persist, and stated that a larger or more protracted shock would require a more restrictive stance. The Friday CBI orders print (-27 from -28) then acted as the necessary brake on any over-hawkish extrapolation: manufacturing output was still falling faster in the three months to March, and the goods economy remained deeply sub-normal, arguing firmly for hold rather than a live tightening cycle. The resulting regime is higher-for-longer hold with a conditional hike threat, not an active hiking cycle.
