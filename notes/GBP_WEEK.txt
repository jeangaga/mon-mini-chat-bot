A. GBP WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

GBP over the supplied window was a clear growth-slack confirmation tape, but not a clean BoE dovish-pivot tape. The sequence started with housing weakness through Rightmove, then May CPI cooled versus the going-in narrative while services inflation re-accelerated to 3.7%; PPI showed the upstream monthly impulse cresting, and labour softened underneath despite headline wage hooks. The 18 Jun MPC then crystallised the policy bind: hold at 3.75%, but a wider 7–2 hawkish dissent as Greene joined Pill, while the majority retained an active-hold / wait frame because CPI/PPI, energy and labour demand were less hawkish than the vote split alone implied.

The central-bank tape is the key overlay: the 7–2 vote looked hawkish, but the majority’s reaction function drifted dovishly. The Bank marked down its short-term inflation path after the US–Iran/oil collapse and the downside May CPI surprise; Lombardelli rejected pre-emptive tightening; Taylor later endorsed an extended hold rather than an immediate cut, and emphasized that policy is already above neutral. The second week then turned the growth signal more decisive: GfK stayed weak, the retail-sales beat looked weather/promotions/base-flattered, the flash PMI confirmed a second month of private-sector contraction led by services, CBI orders hit the weakest level since 2020, and CBI distributive trades confirmed retail/distribution weakness. Final regime: GBP has deteriorating domestic-growth quality and stronger slack evidence, but BoE cuts remain gated by services CPI, wages and expectations credibility.

Main contradiction: Growth and financial-condition data were firmer than feared — GDP, services output, manufacturing, credit, retail and PMIs all beat — while labour surveys/payrolls still softened and services/wage inflation remained too high for the BoE to move quickly.

The BoE reaction function required labour softening to feed into wages and services CPI; until that happened, stronger activity and leaky financial conditions argued for patience.

Implication: The near-term BoE path is gradualism. Cuts remain conditional rather than imminent. The next resolution point is whether wage growth moves down from 4.7% and services CPI moves below 4.5%, while PMI/export-order strength either persists or fades.
