A. GBP WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

GBP moved from false-dawn resilience into confirmed stagflationary deterioration, while the BoE moved from a formal hawkish-hold pivot into a passive-hold framework that lets market tightening buy time. The early period still showed active housing finance, firmer Nationwide prices, manufacturing frontloading, a BoE 8–1 hold with Pill dissenting for a hike, and a genuinely strong Q1 GDP / March output print, but those supports were increasingly low-quality: credit and orders looked pulled forward, manufacturing was supply-chain / stockpiling distorted, and the goods-trade deficit and housing surveys already pointed to external and rate-sensitive fragility. The final week resolved the activity direction lower: payrolls fell sharply, unemployment rose to 5.0%, services PMI contracted, CBI orders weakened, retail sales fell hard, and GfK major-purchase intentions deteriorated.

The policy regime is not a clean dovish pivot. CPI, core CPI and services CPI delivered tactical relief, and Bailey’s May TSC framing shifted the near-term read away from an active hike path toward “market rates buying time.” But the BoE’s April MPR had already embedded a materially higher CPI path, Pill’s hike dissent and Mann’s wage-embedding risk preserve the hawkish tail, and PPI / services-cost / construction-cost pressure keep the second-round channel unresolved. Final GBP regime: services-led and consumer-confirmed deterioration, with weaker labour and retail demand raising the hurdle for BoE tightening, but not enough to make GBP a clean easing candidate while the BoE’s state-contingent Hormuz / energy framework remains live.

B. GBP MACRO SIGNAL SCOREBOARD

Growth: -
Supporting evidence:
The period began with stronger optics — Q1 GDP +0.6% q/q, March GDP +0.3% m/m, services +0.3% m/m, manufacturing output +1.2% m/m, Nationwide prices up 0.4% m/m and mortgage approvals at 63.5k — but the forward sequence deteriorated sharply. CBI Distributive Trades collapsed to -68, construction PMI fell to 39.7, flash composite PMI fell to 48.5, services PMI to 47.9, CBI orders weakened to -41 and April retail sales fell -1.3% m/m, with ex-fuel sales also down. The cumulative signal is a false-dawn hard-data peak giving way to survey and realized-demand deterioration.

Labor: -
Supporting evidence:
Early manufacturing PMI showed employment rising for the first time in 18 months, but the later labour block dominated: HMRC payrolls fell -100k, the ILO unemployment rate rose to 5.0%, vacancies fell to a five-year low and regular ex-bonus earnings slowed to 3.4%. The LFS employment beat was explicitly discounted against cleaner administrative and vacancy evidence. Labour therefore shifted from residual resilience to deterioration, lowering the quality of the GBP growth story.

Inflation: ~
Supporting evidence:
Consumer inflation delivered tactical relief: CPI y/y fell to 2.8%, core CPI to 2.5%, services CPI y/y to 3.2%, RPI softened and services CPI surprised below poll. But the archive does not validate a clean disinflation regime: PPI input prices rose 7.7% y/y and output PPI 4.0% y/y, construction and services PMIs flagged sharp cost pressure, manufacturing PMI input costs were near a four-year high, CBI selling-price expectations rose, and the BoE’s April MPR had already lifted the Q3 CPI projection materially versus February. Inflation is softer at the consumer margin but still unresolved through upstream and second-round channels.

Financial Conditions: -
Supporting evidence:
Credit and housing finance initially looked resilient, with mortgage lending at £6.2bn, approvals above poll and consumer credit still above its recent six-month average. But the signal increasingly became rate-sensitive stress rather than easy conditions: RICS fell to -34, Halifax annual growth slowed, Rightmove remained an asking-price rather than transaction signal, construction collapsed under borrowing-cost and input-cost pressure, and retail / major-purchase data showed household caution. The central-bank tape reinforces this: the May 5 long-end gilt blowout and Bailey’s later “market rates buying time” framework show that market tightening was doing part of the BoE’s work before yields later retreated on softer data and Hormuz de-escalation.

Policy Constraint: ~
Supporting evidence:
The BoE held at 3.75% on 30 April with an 8–1 vote and Pill dissenting for a 25bp hike, while the April MPR formalised a three-scenario energy-shock framework and lifted the near-term CPI path. That was the hawkish-hold pivot. By 20–22 May, the labour, CPI, PMI and retail sequence had weakened materially, Bailey framed higher market rates as buying time, Dhingra said hikes may be avoidable under moderate second-round effects, and economist consensus shifted toward no hike this year. But Mann’s wage-embedding risk, Pill’s dissent, Bailey’s gas-infrastructure warning and the hot PPI pipeline keep the policy constraint two-sided rather than dovish. The live BoE stance is passive hold / cuts deferred, with hike risk conditional on renewed Hormuz escalation or second-round pass-through.

State transition: continuation — hawkish optionality rising

Context from prior regional live file: The UK prior sequence had already shifted into a stagflationary-demand tension, with weaker PMIs, housing softness and collapsing retail survey evidence alongside worsening cost optics.
