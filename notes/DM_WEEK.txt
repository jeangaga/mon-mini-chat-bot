A. DM WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

DM macro did not converge to a single regime this week; it split along policy and demand lines. Australia remained the clear inflation/capacity outlier with a second consecutive hike, but the leading index and labour-quality mix softened; the UK shifted decisively into hawkish-hold territory as labour stayed firmer than feared and the MPC suspended the live cut debate; and Japan delivered a tactical hold that preserved medium-term normalization pressure. By contrast, Canada and New Zealand continued to accumulate softer domestic-demand evidence, with Canada printing a cleaner pre-shock disinflation baseline and New Zealand showing weak-quality growth plus still-fragile external repair. Sweden and Switzerland stayed in cautious-hold mode rather than regime change, supported by anchored expectations and FX/energy management.

The weekly macro message is therefore not broad DM reflation or broad DM slowdown, but widening relative-value dispersion under a common oil-shock overlay. Growth signals were mixed to softer outside Japan’s capex pocket and parts of UK labour; labour loosened only gradually where released; inflation pressure was not uniformly re-accelerating, but policy sensitivity to energy persistence rose sharply. The resulting regime was tighter policy constraint in Australia/UK/Japan, softer-growth optionality in Canada/New Zealand, and watchful holds in Sweden/Switzerland.

Growth: ~

Switzerland stayed at 0% but increased its willingness to intervene in FX, showing that financial tightening risk was being managed through the currency channel rather than eased away. The weekly signal was still one of restrictive or tightening financial conditions, not relaxation.

Policy Constraint: -
Supporting evidence:
The RBA delivered a second consecutive hike with a 5–4 split, the BoE shifted from a 5–4 cut-biased split to a 9–0 hold, and the BoJ held but with an explicit 1.0% dissenter and no retreat from normalization. The BoC held with a softer domestic baseline, but the oil shock complicated the forward cut path; Sweden and Switzerland also stayed on hold under energy/geopolitical uncertainty. Across the region, central banks remained constrained by inflation persistence or shock-management risk, so policy flexibility narrowed rather than broadened.

That combination says sellers are recovering seasonality, not regaining control of pricing. In macro terms, the Rightmove series is behaving like a housing market with too much stock and only limited ability to absorb tighter financing conditions.

State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior DM live file had UK housing as subdued rather than collapsing, with Nationwide and Halifax still showing modest resilience while construction PMI remained clearly weak. March Rightmove fits that sequence: the monthly bounce returned, but the annual rate slipped negative, so the housing bucket moved from stall to mild deterioration rather than from stall to recovery.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a BoE-moving print on its own, but it keeps the housing channel on the “still rate-sensitive” side of the UK story. If the mortgage repricing seen in Rightmove’s tracker persists, housing remains a drag on any clean consumer re-acceleration narrative. Reaction-function relevance is therefore second order: limited direct policy value by itself, but mildly consistent with gradualism rather than any aggressive extrapolation from better near-term activity prints elsewhere.

A. DM WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

DM growth weakened over the week, but with clear cross-country dispersion rather than a uniform downturn. Canada and the UK delivered the strongest real-economy softness, with trade, wholesale, manufacturing sales, labour, GDP, services, and housing-sensitive channels all pointing to weak pre-energy-shock domestic demand. Sweden’s January hard-data batch also broke the prior recovery hope, even as inflation stayed cleanly below target and labour remained soft rather than collapsing. Against that, Japan remained the relative firm spot: Q4 GDP and capex were revised up credibly, external balances improved modestly, and pipeline inflation softened, though household demand still failed to validate a clean consumption handoff. Norway sat between those poles, with inflation cooling but not enough to reopen an easy easing path, while New Zealand manufacturing stayed solid without changing the broader mixed demand picture.

The final weekly regime is therefore differentiated prolonged hold, not DM convergence. Sweden looks like the cleanest disinflation case, Norway remains inflation-constrained, the BoE faces a stagflation-style bind, the BoC is increasingly constrained by weakening demand, and the BoJ retains gradual normalization optionality but not urgency. The shared oil/inflation overlay matters, but the more important point is that DM central banks are entering it from very different domestic-growth starting points.

BOTTOM LINE — WEEK SYNTHESIS (what’s new / what mattered)

UK: “soft-landing” probability rose sharply — and the BoE cutting narrative got less urgent.
The week started with mixed labour/wages (not weak enough to force cuts), then CPI delivered “no re-acceleration in core/services,” but the key narrative shock was Friday’s UK flash PMIs + retail sales: that combo is exactly what makes the BoE’s reaction function asymmetric (they can ease only if growth cooperates and inflation keeps falling). This week delivered growth cooperation. Net: cuts become later / more conditional rather than “imminent”; GBP support becomes more durable because it’s backed by activity momentum, not just positioning.

Japan: BoJ stayed on rails — the economy still sends mixed signals, so the tightening path stays ultra-gradual.
Hard data (machinery orders, trade balance) argued for caution; surveys (PMIs) argued for resilience. CPI core in line and the policy decision unchanged crystallise the baseline: BoJ is not accelerating the hiking cycle; it’s waiting for sustained, high-confidence confirmation. Net: JPY remains more a function of global rates and risk than of domestic policy surprise.

Australia/New Zealand: the “rates stay restrictive” tail got fatter (for different reasons).
Australia printed a growth/labour/PMI cluster that keeps the RBA constrained (even if inflation is improving elsewhere). New Zealand CPI came in firmer, pushing easing optionality out. Net: AUD/NZD sensitivity tilts back toward front-end repricing rather than pure risk beta.

Canada: cross-currents (disinflation + resilient consumption) keep the BoC in patient mode, not forced either way.
BoC core measures were consistent with disinflation continuing, but retail sales were strong enough to prevent a “growth break” narrative. Net: BoC can ease, but the urgency is not accelerating — market should price a gradual path, not a rush.

What did not matter this week: low-frequency confidence/sentiment (GfK), stable policy holds (Norges/BoJ were expected), and single-point activity prints without confirmation (one-offs only mattered when clustered, like UK Friday).
