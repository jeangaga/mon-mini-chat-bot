A. DM WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

The DM week resolved into a backward-looking strength versus current transmission split. February hard data in the UK, Japan, and parts of Canada showed that the region entered the Iran shock with more momentum than feared: UK GDP and services beat strongly, Japan machinery orders rebounded sharply, and Canadian manufacturing and wholesale activity recovered. But the more current and more policy-relevant signals were less clean. New Zealand’s services and core retail demand deteriorated, Canadian permits and housing starts confirmed a genuine construction slowdown, Sweden kept soft realized core inflation but added firmer long-run inflation expectations, and the UK’s strongest growth print was explicitly pre-war. Australia was the main current-data hawkish outlier through labour-market resilience. The resulting regime is not synchronized collapse and not synchronized reflation, but a bidirectional DM tape with wider policy dispersion and a higher bar for simple easing conclusions.

B. DM MACRO SIGNAL SCOREBOARD

Growth: ~
Supporting evidence:
Japan machinery orders rose 13.6% m/m and 24.7% y/y, UK February GDP beat strongly at 0.5% m/m and 1.0% y/y, and Canadian manufacturing and wholesale sales rebounded. Against that, New Zealand PSI kept employment below 50, pointing to softer domestic labour conditions. The regional labour read therefore stayed uneven rather than breaking decisively weaker or re-tightening broadly.

Inflation: ~
Supporting evidence:
Sweden’s realized inflation remained soft, with CPIF at 1.6% y/y and CPIF ex energy at 1.1%, confirming weak domestic core. But Sweden’s 5-year money-market CPIF expectations rose to 2.2%, New Zealand retail/card spending was flattered by fuel inflation, and UK trade remained vulnerable to a higher energy-import bill. The week did not produce broad realized reflation, but it did preserve inflation-risk channels.

Financial Conditions: ~
Supporting evidence:

At the same time, housing and construction-sensitive channels weakened in Canada, and several of the best growth prints were backward-looking rather than evidence of easier current conditions. Financial conditions did not look restrictive enough to explain the slowdown outright, but they were not delivering a clean cyclical re-acceleration either.

Policy Constraint: +
Supporting evidence:
Australia preserved live RBA tightening optionality through resilient labour data; Sweden’s soft realized core was offset by firmer long-run inflation expectations; the UK kept a stagflationary trade and energy-risk profile even after a strong GDP beat; and Canada’s weak construction sequence deepened growth concerns without resolving the BoC’s inflation problem. Across the region, central banks stayed constrained by two-sided risk rather than receiving a clean easing signal.
