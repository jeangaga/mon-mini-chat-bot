A. AUD WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

AUD finished the supplied period as a policy-constrained weak-growth file, not a clean domestic recovery or clean disinflation story. The early investment impulse was real — construction work and private capex surprised strongly, with data-centre / plant-machinery strength offsetting household fragility — but the forward activity tape deteriorated or stayed low-quality: the leading index pointed below trend, the RBA acknowledged slowing consumption, and the June PMI only stabilised near 50 with falling orders, weak confidence and cost pressure still present. The decisive regime shift came from inflation and labour sequencing: April CPI was a false dawn, the RBA held with explicit oil/pass-through hike optionality, May CPI then showed headline fuel relief masking firmer trimmed mean, weighted median, services and non-tradables, and May labour did not loosen enough to neutralize the CPI shock.

The RBA chatterbox confirms the same regime but sharpens the reaction function: June was a hawkish hold, not a live-hike meeting. Bullock drained near-term hike risk by saying a June hike was not actively considered, while the statement, Hauser and Harper kept the inflation/expectations tail alive. The AUD read is therefore conditional rate support rather than growth support: domestic hike-tail pricing remains live if core/non-tradables and labour hold up, but weak demand quality and oil-relief optionality cap the bullish read.

B. AUD MACRO SIGNAL SCOREBOARD

Growth: ~
Supporting evidence:
Construction work rose 3.4% q/q in Q1 and private capex jumped 6.5%, driven by plant/machinery and data-centre investment, so the investment side offset a clean slowdown narrative. But the growth quality stayed weak: the leading index was only a background stabilizer in April, then the May leading index fell to a below-trend six-month annualised rate of -0.17%; the RBA noted slowing consumer spending; and the June PMI composite remained sub-50 at 49.8 with services 49.9, falling orders, weak confidence and manufacturing-output weakness. Net growth is mixed, with corporate-investment support but poor household / survey quality.

Labor: ~
Supporting evidence:
May employment rebounded by 40.3k versus +30.0k expected, participation held at 66.7%, and unemployment fell to 4.4%, which is enough to deny the RBA a clean labour-slack release valve. But the internals were softer: the gain was largely part-time-led, hours worked fell 1.1%, underemployment edged higher, and annual employment growth remained weak. Labour is not overheating, but it is not loosening fast enough to neutralize sticky inflation.

Inflation: -
Supporting evidence:
April CPI already showed the false-dawn pattern: headline CPI cooled to 4.2% y/y versus 4.4% poll, but trimmed mean rose to 3.4% and weighted median stayed at 3.5%. May repeated and intensified the same structure: headline CPI fell to 4.0% y/y and missed poll because fuel dropped sharply, but trimmed mean and weighted median both rose to 3.6%, services inflation rose to 3.7%, and non-tradables stayed at 4.7%. The inflation signal deteriorated because underlying/domestic measures remained too firm despite headline relief.

Financial Conditions: ~
Supporting evidence:
AUD/rates support came from sticky underlying CPI, resilient headline labour and RBA hike optionality; the archive notes AUD edged higher and short-end pricing retained meaningful odds of another hike after May CPI. But financial-condition support was conditional, not broad: the RBA’s hawkishness was explicitly oil/pass-through dependent, Bullock said a June hike was not actively considered, the leading index and PMI weakened the growth leg, and AUD remained capped by weak growth quality and global USD/risk tape in the labour block. The period therefore produced rate-supportive domestic constraint, but not a clean AUD bullish financial-conditions impulse.

Policy Constraint: -
Supporting evidence:
The RBA held at 4.35% but kept an explicit further-hike option, linking fuel inflation to broader goods/services pass-through and embedding risk. The chatterbox confirms a hawkish hold with a live but not imminent hike tail: Bullock narrowed near-term hike risk, Hauser and the statement kept “more work to do” / pass-through vigilance alive, and Harper made inflation-expectations de-anchoring the hawkish trigger. Subsequent data reinforced rather than relaxed that constraint: May CPI showed hawkish core/non-tradables beneath fuel-led headline relief, and May labour did not loosen enough to offset the CPI shock.

A. AUD WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

Australia’s merged 5 Jan–6 Feb window was the regime-defining early-2026 AUD block: headline disinflation initially created ambiguity, but labour, activity and then Q4 core inflation progressively removed the case for RBA patience. November CPI softened at the headline level, yet trimmed-mean momentum did not collapse; December employment then re-tightened sharply, January flash PMIs accelerated, and Q4 trimmed mean beat the RBA’s primary inflation gauge at 0.9% q/q. By the time the RBA met on 4 February, the policy question had shifted from “hold or hike?” to “how long does the new hiking cycle run?”

The February RBA decision crystallised the regime. A unanimous 25bp hike to 3.85%, Bullock’s language on private demand, “somewhat accommodative” financial conditions, sharply stronger credit and above-potential growth, plus forecasts embedding further tightening, turned Australia into the clear DM policy outlier. The external and construction channels were softer, and the early-January CPI print had not forced the decision alone, but the full sequence delivered a coherent macro message: domestic demand and labour were too strong, core inflation too sticky, and the RBA too uncomfortable to treat the February hike as one-and-done.

SIGNAL TENSION CHECK

Main contradiction: Headline CPI disinflation was real in November and the RBA’s post-hike language remained cautious on committing to a full cycle, but the cumulative data sequence — labour re-tightening, PMI acceleration, Q4 trimmed-mean upside and the RBA’s own “somewhat accommodative” financial-conditions assessment — shifted the reaction function decisively toward tightening.

Why it matters: The decision was not made by one print alone. The November CPI grey zone kept optionality alive, the December labour and January PMI data raised the bar for inaction, and Q4 trimmed mean resolved the inflation ambiguity. That sequencing explains why the February hike was unanimous and why the May follow-up probability became real but still data-dependent.

Implication: Australia entered February in a confirmed hiking regime, not a one-and-done adjustment. AUD remained supported by relative policy divergence, but the path and terminal rate still depended on Q1 trimmed mean, labour tightness and whether housing/credit transmission broadened beyond the most rate-sensitive channels.
