A. AUSTRALIA WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS
Australia remained the clear DM policy outlier across the full window. The dominant signal was a second consecutive RBA hike — to 4.10% on 17 March by a 5–4 split — executed explicitly on domestic demand-versus-supply grounds rather than as an oil-shock reaction. Governor Bullock's communication was unusually direct: the Board raised because inflation was already too high before the energy shock, business investment outperformed, and labour underutilisation remained low. That message confirmed Australia as the only DM central bank still tightening on domestic-capacity logic, not external-pressure logic.
However, the window also accumulated the two most important internal checks on that hawkish arc. The Westpac–Melbourne Institute leading index printed negative for a second consecutive month in February (-0.08% m/m after a revised flat January), with the six-month annualised growth rate near flat, signalling that the above-trend momentum of late 2025 had faded materially. February labour, released 19 March, then confirmed that internal tension: employment grew by 48,900, but full-time jobs fell 30,000, part-time surged 79,000, hours worked declined 0.2%, and the unemployment rate rose to 4.3% against a 4.1% consensus. The ABS explicitly noted that more people remained unemployed this February than in recent Februarys and fewer moved directly from unemployment into work. The regime for the full window is therefore hawkish continuation with rising internal resistance: the RBA is still leaning in, but the 5–4 split, the deteriorating leading indicator, and the softening labour quality together constrain confidence around a long further hiking sequence.

January monthly CPI showed core measures — trimmed mean 3.4% YY, weighted median 3.6% YY — holding rather than softening, keeping the inflation case for further tightening alive.
The transmission channels that were showing early stress were construction and housing approvals. Building approvals slumped 7.2% m/m in January against a +5.0% consensus expectation, and construction work done disappointed in Q4. The current account deficit widened materially to -A$21.1bn. Trade data deteriorated in January, with the goods surplus narrowing sharply below consensus. These are exactly the rate-sensitive channels that should show early transmission effects, and they were doing so — but not at a magnitude sufficient to force a pause signal into the May meeting.
The net weekly regime at close of Block 3 is: growth confirmed above potential, labour stable and tight, core inflation sticky, rate-sensitive construction and housing under pressure, external sector a modest headwind. The May hike remains the base case.
