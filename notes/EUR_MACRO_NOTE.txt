Executive Summary – Macro Regime & New Information

Macro regime (still soft-landing / low-trend, but with a cleaner inflation anchor and a more visible “external tails” layer):
Euro area remains a two-speed regime: services keep activity above stall while manufacturing is still structurally weak. The ECB remains on a restrictive plateau with high optionality, and the binding constraint remains underlying/services inflation + wages — but the “headline at/near 2” anchor has strengthened.

What is NEW in the primary window (5–16 Jan):

The inflation anchor improved in a way that is marketable (headline inflation at ~2.0% in Dec), but professional desks did not read this as “all-clear”: the focus stayed on the composition (core/services/wages), and on policy staying restrictive even as headline converges.

Survey momentum turned meaningfully less negative (Sentix jump), consistent with “stabilisation not rebound” — i.e., confidence/expectations improving while the hard-data ceiling remains.

Hard data confirmed a modest industrial stabilisation in November (industrial production rose again m/m), but the profile still looks like “bounce at low levels,” not a manufacturing-led recovery.

External tail risks gained salience in the ECB narrative layer: US political interference with the Fed was explicitly framed by ECB officials as a global stability risk; add the usual euro-area external risks (China trade pressure; tariff risks).

France-specific policy/budget uncertainty remained a second-order growth drag and risk-premium tail, with ECB/BoF voices explicitly warning about deficit discipline and the confidence cost.

What did NOT change:

Services are still the stabiliser; manufacturing remains the drag.

The ECB’s reaction function remains “hold on plateau until underlying inflation / wages are convincingly consistent with 2%.” Headline convergence is necessary but not sufficient.

The regime’s convexity is still spillover: if the goods weakness leaks into labour/services, the ECB can pivot dovish quickly; if services inflation persists, the plateau extends.

Regime-change triggers (what would force a pivot):

Dovish pivot trigger: a clear deterioration in euro-area labour/services demand (employment components, unemployment trend, negotiated wage cooling) alongside core/services inflation momentum rolling decisively lower.

Hawkish tail trigger: services inflation persistence / wage transmission stalls, forcing “hold longer” even with headline near 2%; the “next move hike tail” stays a tail but becomes less dismissible if services persistence reasserts.

External-shock trigger: tariff/trade shocks or financial stability stress (including a USD-volatility episode) tighten financial conditions and force ECB risk-management dovishness despite inflation progress.
