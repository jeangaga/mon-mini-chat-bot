AUD — MACRO NOTE

As of: 27 May 2026
Horizon: 4-week impulse filter into a 3–6 month scenario update; late-April to late-May data carry highest tactical weight, with late-March / early-April used as regime anchor.

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ↓ Active deterioration after false-dawn stabilisation
Labor: ↓ Labour resilience shield weakened, confirmation still needed
Inflation: ~ Headline cooled mechanically; underlying / pass-through risk still live
Financial cond: ~ Front-end hike premium compressed; real-economy transmission rising
Policy bias: ~ Hawkish hold / skip first, with H2 hike risk alive
Political risk: ↑ Hormuz / energy / fuel-excise channel is now central, not background

Executive Summary

Australia has moved from hawkish-with-stagflation-complication into a two-sided stagflation-light regime. The May RBA hike validated the inflation-expectations constraint, but the subsequent data flow weakened the case for a mechanical June follow-through. The RBA raised the cash rate to 4.35% in an 8–1 vote, the May SMP kept inflation above target for longer, and Hunter reasserted the risk that cost pass-through could be faster and more persistent than normal. But the same policy sequence also created room to pause, while late-May PMI and labour data gave the dovish dissenter a genuine empirical opening.

What changed is the policy asymmetry. The base case is no longer “May hike then automatic continuation”; it is skip / hold first, but not done with conviction. OIS and public-bank commentary have moved toward a low June-hike probability, but the institutional distribution is split: CBA / ANZ / Bendigo lean terminal or hold; TD maps a skip-then-August path; Westpac still sees further hikes later; NAB remains closer to a June-hike camp. The note should therefore not frame “another hike requires fresh proof” as consensus dovishness. It is the market-implied near-term baseline, while the inflation hawks retain a credible H2 route.

What did not change is the inflation veto. April CPI cooled at the headline level, but the decline was heavily distorted by the fuel-excise halving and free transport effects, while trimmed mean rose to 3.4% from 3.3%, the highest since late 2024. That keeps the underlying question unresolved. The RBA cannot ignore activity deterioration, but it also cannot relax while headline inflation is policy-distorted lower and underlying inflation is creeping up.

The binding constraint is now second-round pass-through versus real-economy deterioration. Labour and PMIs have weakened enough to make June a likely skip, but not enough to kill the hiking cycle if May / Q2 CPI, expectations and business pricing validate Hunter’s framework. AUD is no longer mainly a clean rates currency. The rate differential still matters, but the near-term driver has shifted toward Hormuz / oil / global risk sentiment, with speculative AUD longs making the currency vulnerable if the risk-asset rally or ceasefire narrative fails.

Growth / Activity

Growth has deteriorated from weak stabilisation into active private-sector contraction. The earlier April PMI rebound above 50 was a false dawn: manufacturing output and new orders were weak, services new business fell, confidence was poor, and input costs accelerated sharply. The final April PMIs confirmed that headline stabilisation was low quality rather than a clean demand recovery.

The late-May flash PMI shifted the regime. Composite output fell to 47.8 from 50.4, services dropped to 47.7, manufacturing slipped close to stagnation, new orders weakened sharply, employment deteriorated and confidence was among the weakest on record. That is a genuine private-sector warning. The caveat is causality: part of the PMI shock may reflect Hormuz / Iran uncertainty and energy-cost sentiment rather than a purely domestic demand collapse. But for the RBA and AUD, the macro effect is the same in the near term: weaker demand, weaker confidence and still-elevated cost pressure.

Hard-data cross-checks are consistent with a worse growth mix. Building approvals fell sharply, with volatility in the apartment component but broader construction-pipeline stress in values. The trade balance swung into deficit as imports surged and exports fell, adding net-exports risk to GDP. Some of the import strength reflects lumpy capital equipment, but the external cushion is weaker and the fuel-import channel is now part of the stagflation mix.

The floor is narrower. Credit was still firm in March, and business conditions had not yet collapsed, but the forward impulse has clearly weakened. Australia is not in a confirmed downturn regime, because one PMI / labour sequence still needs validation and the ABS labour survey transition creates noise. But the direction has shifted away from hawkish resilience and toward a real test of whether the RBA has already tightened enough.

Labor / Employment

Labour has moved from a reason the RBA could tighten to the main argument against mechanical follow-through. The March labour print still looked resilient: employment rose, full-time jobs did the heavy lifting, hours worked increased, and unemployment held at 4.3%. That kept tightening optionality alive into the May decision.

The April labour print changed the balance. Employment fell by roughly 19k, unemployment rose to 4.5%, and participation slipped. Youth unemployment also moved higher. The rise in unemployment was not simply a participation-led artefact, and the direction aligned with the PMI employment signal. That matters because labour resilience had been the cleanest domestic support for the RBA’s inflation-first stance.

The caveat is measurement and confirmation. ABS survey modernisation means the April weakness should not be treated as a fully confirmed labour break, and hours worked were more resilient than headcount. Trend data and the May labour release matter more than the single monthly move. Still, the policy implication has changed: the RBA can no longer assume labour is absorbing higher rates without strain.

Wages do not validate a wage-price spiral. Q1 WPI rose 0.8% q/q and 3.3% y/y, private-sector wage growth slowed to 3.2%, and distributional detail softened. That is wage moderation, not labour-market collapse, but it matters because the RBA’s inflation concern is fuel / expectations / pass-through rather than a clean wage acceleration. The labour channel now argues for caution on further hikes unless inflation broadening becomes clearer.

Inflation

Inflation remains the veto on a dovish turn. Headline CPI re-accelerated sharply through March, the Q1 CPI package forced the RBA to act, and the May SMP kept headline inflation above target for longer. The RBA’s concern is not only current fuel inflation; it is the risk that firms pass higher costs into broader goods and services prices and that short-term expectations drift higher.

The April CPI update adds nuance rather than relief. Headline CPI cooled to 4.2% from 4.6%, but the fall was heavily affected by the fuel-excise cut and other policy distortions. Automotive fuel prices still remained far above February levels, and the July reversal of the excise cut keeps the inflation debate alive into H2. More importantly, trimmed mean rose to 3.4% from 3.3%, undermining any simple “underlying inflation is cooling cleanly” interpretation.

The inflation evidence is still not a full second-round break. Services inflation eased, wages moderated, and Q1 official pipeline data were not uniformly alarming. But institutional inflation hawks are focused less on backward-looking q/q relief and more on the forward pass-through mechanism: fuel, freight, import costs, business pricing and expectations. That keeps the RBA’s hawkish boundary firmer than a pure growth-deterioration read would imply.

The current hierarchy is: energy / Hormuz shock first; expectations and business pass-through second; underlying trimmed mean now edging the wrong way; wages not yet confirming a spiral; services still unresolved. Inflation is not strong enough to force an automatic June hike, but it is strong enough to prevent the May hike from being safely labelled terminal.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest RBA Monetary Policy Board decision was on 5 May 2026, when the Board raised the cash rate target by 25bp to 4.35%. The decision was an 8–1 vote, widening the hawkish majority relative to the earlier 5–4 March split. The May Statement and SMP tied the hike to above-target inflation, fuel-price pressure from the Middle East conflict, pass-through risk into broader goods and services prices, and the danger that expectations could drift higher.

The meeting told us the RBA is still an inflation-first central bank. It acted even though spending assumptions had softened, because the inflation distribution had worsened and financial conditions were judged not sufficiently restrictive. But Bullock’s post-meeting framing also reduced the probability of automatic June follow-through: the new rate was restrictive and gave the Board scope to observe whether the energy shock broadens. The Minutes then revealed a coherent dovish dissenter argument that capacity pressures may have been less extensive than staff assumed.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case is skip / hold at the June meeting, with the cash rate at 4.35% while the Board tests whether the energy shock becomes persistent or whether activity / labour deterioration dominates. This is not a dovish pivot. A more dovish path requires confirmation that April’s labour weakness and May’s PMI contraction are not one-offs, plus stable or lower trimmed mean and contained expectations. A more hawkish path requires May / Q2 CPI, inflation expectations or business pricing to validate Hunter’s “faster and more extensive” pass-through risk. The most plausible hawkish branch is no longer necessarily June; it is an H2 hike path if underlying inflation and expectations keep moving the wrong way.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end has shifted from “May plus possible immediate follow-through” to “June skip, H2 inflation test.” OIS pricing is closer to a low-probability June hike than to a default continuation cycle. But this is not a clean bull-steepening / easing setup because the RBA’s inflation reaction function remains alive and institutional views remain split. Rates pricing is now a contest between near-term growth/labour evidence and later CPI / expectations validation.

AUD rates repricing depends on inflation breadth. A second trimmed-mean up-move, firmer expectations or clearer business pass-through can quickly rebuild hike premium, especially for August / September. Conversely, another weak labour print and sustained PMI contraction with contained CPI would push terminal-rate pricing lower and validate the “May was enough” camp. The market is not trading a clean RBA easing story; it is trading a skip with upside inflation risk.

AUD FX has structurally become more risk-sensitive in the Hormuz regime. The traditional rates-support channel has been partly displaced by global risk sentiment, oil/Hormuz headlines and positioning. The currency can rally on ceasefire / Strait-reopening hopes even when domestic data are weak, but that makes the rally lower quality. Speculative long positioning at multi-year highs increases downside convexity if energy de-escalation stalls, equities roll over, or the domestic data force the market to reprice the RBA path lower.

Financial conditions are restrictive in the real economy but not uniformly tight through credit. March private-sector credit was still firm, which supports the RBA’s concern that policy had not obviously crushed demand. But PMI contraction, weak confidence, approvals stress, labour deterioration and a wider external deficit show the lagged transmission channel is now appearing. The key question is no longer whether policy is restrictive in theory; it is whether the RBA can keep tightening without turning a cost-push shock into a domestic demand break.

Political / Policy Events

The key political / policy channel is external energy, not domestic political instability. Hormuz / Iran risk has become the macro transmission mechanism: fuel costs, freight pressure, import costs, business pricing, household real income and inflation expectations. This is now central to the RBA reaction function and to AUD pricing.

Domestic policy is also relevant through inflation measurement. The fuel-excise halving on 1 April mechanically suppresses headline CPI through the near term and then unwinds later, which means headline inflation will be a less clean guide to policy pressure. Free public transport effects in some states add another policy distortion. The RBA therefore has to look through part of the headline cooling and focus on trimmed mean, services, expectations and business pass-through.

The Federal Budget does not appear to have changed the macro base case materially. The more important policy issue is credibility management: preventing a temporary energy shock from becoming embedded while avoiding unnecessary over-tightening into a weakening services/labour backdrop.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The public tape broadly confirms the core regime: the RBA has shifted into hawkish hold territory, not dovish pivot territory. The May hike was a credibility move against inflation expectations and cost pass-through, while the post-meeting communication created scope to pause and observe rather than pre-commit to June. Rates pricing is closer to skip-first than hike-first, with only limited June hike probability, but the front end still carries H2 upside risk because the RBA’s inflation constraint is not resolved. For AUD, the market is no longer trading a simple domestic rates story: risk sentiment, Hormuz headlines and speculative positioning have become dominant short-run drivers, while the RBA path acts as a secondary convexity channel.

The real debate is not whether Australia is slowing; it is whether the slowdown is strong and clean enough to overwhelm the pass-through risk. CBA / ANZ / Bendigo-style views treat May as likely terminal or at least sufficient for now; TD-type framing keeps a skip-then-August path; Westpac and NAB keep a more hawkish distribution alive because fuel, freight, business pricing and expectations may broaden faster than normal. The late-May PMI and labour prints support the dovish dissenter, but the April CPI update strengthens the hawkish boundary: headline relief was policy-distorted, while trimmed mean rose. That makes “hold” the cleaner near-term call, but not a relaxation signal.

The scenario breakers are narrow. A confirmed Hormuz reopening would attack the inflation impulse directly and could make the terminal-rate view dominant while supporting AUD through risk appetite first and rates second. A second firm trimmed-mean print or higher expectations would validate Hunter’s pass-through warning and rebuild H2 hike pricing even if June is skipped. A May labour rebound would turn April into a survey-transition artefact and restore the inflation-first framework; a second weak labour print would move the market closer to “May was enough.” AUD positioning is the accelerant: long speculative exposure makes the currency vulnerable if the ceasefire / risk-on narrative fails before domestic data repair the growth story.

Bottom Line

Australia is no longer a clean hawkish DM outlier, but it is also not a clean easing candidate. The May hike validated the inflation-expectations constraint; the May PMI / April labour sequence weakened the case for automatic follow-through; the April CPI update kept underlying inflation risk alive despite headline relief. The near-term policy base case is a June hold / skip, with H2 hike risk dependent on trimmed mean, expectations and business pass-through. AUD should be treated as a risk-sensitive stagflation currency: supported by risk-on / Hormuz de-escalation and residual RBA credibility, but vulnerable to crowded longs, weak domestic data and any failure of the energy-de-escalation narrative. Base case: RBA holds at 4.35% in June, keeps hawkish language, and preserves H2 optionality while markets trade AUD through Hormuz risk sentiment first and domestic rates second.

Executive Summary

Australia is no longer best described as a balanced “verification phase” between weak surveys and firmer hard data. The dominant regime is still hawkish Australia, but now with a clearer stagflation complication. The March PMI collapse mattered because it punctured the clean resilience story; the April flash PMI rebound mattered because it prevented that shock from becoming a confirmed hard-downshift story. The result is not clean reacceleration, but weak stabilisation: services activity moved back above 50, the composite returned to 50.1, and output stopped falling, yet domestic demand stayed soft, confidence fell to its lowest in almost two and a half years, and price pressures intensified further.

What has not changed is the inflation constraint. The RBA’s March statement said inflation picked up materially in the second half of 2025, that greater capacity pressures were part of the story, that higher fuel prices would add to inflation if sustained, and that short-term inflation expectations had already risen. Since then, consumer inflation expectations rose to 5.9% in April from 5.2% in March, while Westpac’s March-quarter CPI preview pointed to 0.9% q/q trimmed mean and a much hotter headline path than the backward-looking February monthly CPI had implied. That keeps the regime inflation-dominant even as growth becomes less clean.

The base case therefore shifts back toward the public-market center of gravity: a May hike remains the modal near-term outcome, not a hold-first baseline. Markets were pricing roughly a 72% chance of another hike at the May meeting as of 20 April; CBA framed the March split as a debate about timing rather than direction; Westpac moved to a 4.85% peak with hikes in May, June and August; and ANZ had already shifted to a May hike call on the grounds that inflation risks were skewed to the upside. The right way to frame the next 3–6 months is not “pause unless proven otherwise,” but “one more hike is the central case, then the debate shifts to whether that is terminal or merely the start of a longer higher-for-longer phase.”

Growth / Activity

The growth story has improved at the margin relative to the late-March scare, but not enough to restore the old above-potential narrative. The April flash PMI showed the composite output index rising to 50.1 from 46.6, with services back to 50.3 from 46.3 and manufacturing PMI at 51.0. That matters because it means the March services-led contraction signal was not immediately confirmed. But the internals are still weak enough to block any optimistic read: total new business fell for a second month, domestic demand remained soft, sentiment dropped to its lowest in almost two and a half years, and manufacturing output still fell for a third straight month.

So the growth regime is not broadening collapse; it is shallow stabilisation from a weaker starting point. That also means the earlier early-April “good” headlines in approvals and trade still do not deserve much cyclical weight, because they were composition-distorted in the supplied weekly archive. The cleaner update is that March’s scare has been softened, not erased. Australia still looks weaker than the January–early March outlier-hawkish phase, but stronger than a pure survey-led downturn call would imply.

The sector mix remains important. The floor is still coming less from broad-based private demand than from labour income, still-firm credit availability, and services avoiding a second straight contraction print. The weak pockets remain housing, construction and other rate-sensitive domestic sectors, while manufacturing is being hit by supply-chain disruption and higher freight/fuel costs. That is why growth now looks more like a fragile floor than a convincing expansion regime.

Labor / Employment

Labour is the clearest reason the RBA still has cover to tighten. The March labour report showed employment up 17.9k, unemployment steady at 4.3%, participation at 66.8%, full-time employment up 52.5k, part-time down 34.6k, and hours worked up 0.5%. That combination did not deliver a blowout, but it did re-establish labour resilience after February’s poorer quality mix and materially reduced the case for reading the March PMI shock as the start of a rapid domestic break.

The more important point is interpretive. Labour is no longer the clean acceleration story of January, but it is also not deteriorating fast enough to force plateau logic by itself. The public tape has treated the March report as the last relatively clean pre-shock hard-data read and as evidence that the RBA can keep focusing on inflation risk. That means labour now functions less as a reason to stop and more as a reason why the Board can still justify one more move if Q1 CPI validates it.

Inflation

Inflation is still the binding macro constraint, and the note needs a sharper split between headline shock and core persistence. The RBA’s March statement already said fuel prices would add to inflation if sustained and that short-term inflation expectations had risen. Hauser then made the policy problem more explicit in April: the nightmare scenario is inflation up and activity down, with the key issue being how the shock feeds through over the next two to three years rather than only in the immediate headline print. That is materially different from treating the energy shock as temporary headline noise.

The second distinction is between current hard inflation data and the path the market is now trading. February monthly CPI at 3.7% y/y and trimmed mean at 3.3% y/y did not provide policy relief; it merely left the issue unresolved. Since then, Westpac has projected March-quarter CPI at 1.5% q/q with 0.9% q/q trimmed mean, and headline inflation peaking at 5.4% y/y in the June quarter, while also arguing that pass-through beyond fuel is already becoming visible. Even if that profile proves too hot, it captures why the market is not trading a benign inflation fade.

Inflation expectations are the extra transmission channel that now matters much more than in the earlier note. Melbourne Institute’s expected inflation rate rose to 5.9% in April from 5.2% in March. That does not guarantee generalized second-round inflation, but it does mean the RBA is looking at more than just spot fuel. The current inflation hierarchy is therefore: headline energy shock first, expectations and pass-through second, and only then the question of how much slowing activity offsets the price impulse.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest RBA Monetary Policy Board decision remains the 17 March 2026 meeting, where the Board lifted the cash rate target by 25bp to 4.10%. The decision was made by majority, five votes to four. The statement said inflation had picked up materially in the second half of 2025, that greater capacity pressures were evident, that fuel prices would add to inflation if sustained, and that short-term inflation expectations had already risen. The next Board meeting is scheduled for 4–5 May 2026.

That March decision still defines the reaction function. The key issue is not whether the Board has become dovish; it is that the degree of restrictiveness remains uncertain even after two consecutive hikes. CBA’s reading of Bullock’s press conference was that the split was about timing, not about the direction rates needed to go. That keeps the reaction function hawkish, but conditional: the Board is still trying to decide whether it has tightened enough, not whether it should pivot.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case is a 25bp hike in May to 4.35%, followed by a long hold while the Board assesses whether inflation persistence has been re-embedded or merely shocked higher temporarily. That is the modal pricing path in markets and the center of gravity across major public-bank commentary, even if the distribution above it is wide. A softer-than-feared Q1 CPI on 29 April, renewed labour-quality deterioration, and clearer evidence that the April PMI rebound is only a one-month bounce would be the ingredients for a hold instead. A more hawkish path into June and August would require Q1 trimmed mean near the hotter bank forecasts, continued lift in expectations, and proof that current settings are not restraining labour and credit enough.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

Rates are not trading a neutral hold; they are trading an active tightening cycle with May as the immediate focal point. ASX data showed the market pricing about a 72% chance of another hike at the May meeting as of 20 April. That means the front end is centered first on the 29 April Q1 CPI release and then on whether a May move is terminal or the first leg of a longer higher-for-longer phase. The rates debate is now more about peak and persistence than about whether 4.10% was already restrictive enough.

For FX, AUD is being treated more as a hawkish-rates currency than the previous version allowed, but that is not the whole story. Relative-rate support is real while the market prices further RBA tightening and the RBA homepage showed AUD/USD around 0.7155 on 23 April. But the currency is still vulnerable to “unhappy hikes”: if tighter policy is increasingly interpreted as a response to an inflationary energy shock that damages growth and China-sensitive demand, AUD can lose the clean carry/resilience profile even without a dovish repricing of the cash-rate path.

Financial conditions remain restrictive in housing, construction, mortgage-sensitive demand and sentiment, but the broader question of overall restrictiveness is still unresolved. That is exactly what keeps the Board hawkish. Policy has clearly tightened enough to hurt the rate-sensitive pockets; it has not clearly tightened enough to produce a broad domestic demand or labour capitulation.

Political / Policy Events

There were no regime-changing domestic political or fiscal developments in the last 4 weeks that altered the macro base case directly. The relevant political-economy channel remains external: the Middle East conflict, the risk around Strait of Hormuz flows, and the transmission from fuel into expectations, business costs and household real income. Hauser’s April remarks made clear that the external energy shock is part of the RBA’s policy map, not a side issue.

A secondary domestic policy nuance is that the May meeting now sits between a politically noisy inflation backdrop and an important inflation release, which raises the premium on expectations management. The Board is not just reacting to spot inflation; it is trying to prevent the shock from changing behaviour, wage-setting and medium-term pricing norms. That is why politics matters now through energy and inflation psychology, not through domestic political instability.

The April PMI rebound weakens the pure stagflation-collapse narrative, yet the same survey also showed domestic demand still soft and price pressures worsening. The unresolved issues are narrow but decisive. First, whether March-quarter CPI validates the hotter core path now embedded in some bank forecasts. Second, whether the rise in inflation expectations proves sticky enough to force the RBA to err on the side of over-tightening. Third, whether AUD continues to trade mainly off rates or starts to price the China / commodity / risk-sentiment downside branch more heavily even without a dovish policy reprice.

Bottom Line

Australia remains a hawkish DM outlier, but the clean-outlier story has evolved into a hawkish-with-stagflation-complication regime. The March growth scare has been softened by the April PMI rebound, yet labour resilience, rising expectations and the RBA’s own inflation language keep the policy constraint clearly on the upside. The market is not positioned for a neutral hold; it is positioned for May as the modal next step and then a fight over whether that hike is terminal. AUD still has genuine relative-rate support, but the currency is increasingly exposed to the quality of the hiking cycle rather than just the level of rates. Base case: 25bp in May to 4.35%, followed by a long hold, with upside risk to a higher terminal rate if Q1 CPI and expectations validate the more hawkish branch.

Executive Summary

The main change in the 4-week impulse is not that Australia has stopped being hawkish; it is that the hawkish story has become materially messier and more market-sensitive. The dominant market narrative has shifted from “Australia as the clean DM hawkish outlier” toward “Australia in a stagflationary bind”: inflation risk has re-intensified through the Middle East energy shock and possible second-round pass-through, while the domestic forward-growth pulse has simultaneously weakened sharply through the March PMI collapse and a severe deterioration in consumer confidence. That is now the public tape, not just an internal macro interpretation.

What has not changed is the inflation constraint. The RBA still sees inflation as too high, capacity pressure as real, and policy as needing to remain biased toward restraint. But the market narrative around policy has changed in an important way. The debate is no longer cleanly “plateau at 4.10% or hike once more if data surprise.” The public tape has moved toward May hike as the modal next step, with the real disagreement now around whether that move is terminal or whether second-round fuel pass-through and a possibly higher neutral-rate backdrop force a more extended tightening phase. That is a materially more hawkish market narrative than the one embedded in the prior draft.

The binding constraint is therefore twofold: first, inflation may not just remain sticky but may re-accelerate through headline and then core channels; second, growth and demand may already be weakening enough to make each additional hike more dangerous. That is the catch-22. For rates and FX, the market narrative is now more explicit: the front end is still anchored by Q1 trimmed mean and May pricing, while AUD is no longer a pure hawkish-rates trade and is increasingly framed as a dual-driver currency — supported by relative rates, but vulnerable to growth, China, and broader risk sentiment. The base case is no longer plateau-first. Base case is May hike as the modal near-term market outcome, with the terminal debate unresolved.

Growth / Activity

The growth regime has moved from backward-looking validation into a much weaker forward pulse, and the market narrative now treats that deterioration as serious. The strongest ex-post support for the RBA’s hiking logic was the Q4 GDP beat, which confirmed that late-2025 growth had indeed been running above potential. But that is no longer the operative tactical signal. The operative signal is that the forward pulse has deteriorated through multiple channels at once: leading momentum weakened again, construction and approvals stayed soft, consumer confidence collapsed, and March flash PMIs broke decisively lower.

The hierarchy matters. Manufacturing near 50 is not the binding issue. The key development is that services fell into contraction, dragging the composite to 47.0, with weaker new orders and softer confidence. That is particularly important in Australia because the old hawkish narrative depended on domestic demand and services resilience continuing to hold the floor while housing and construction remained the weak pocket. Once services join the weak side, the story shifts from “transmission is contained” to “transmission may be broadening.”

The market narrative has now started to call this what it is: a stagflationary growth scare rather than a clean cyclical slowdown. That does not mean recession is the base case. The economy still has residual support from public demand and a labor market that remains firm in level terms. But it does mean the old “economy still comfortably above potential” framing no longer deserves primacy. The floor is weaker, the demand mix is more fragile, and confidence has rolled over hard enough that the burden of proof has shifted toward stabilization rather than resilience.

The clearest weak pocket remains rate-sensitive domestic demand — housing, construction, mortgage-sensitive sectors — but that is no longer the whole story. The new risk is that weakness has spread into broader services demand and household confidence. That is the central growth change of the last 2–4 weeks.

Labor / Employment

Labor has moved from clearly tight to compositionally softer, and that nuance matters more now because the market is increasingly asking whether policy is overtightening into labor deterioration. The labor market is still firm enough in level terms to prevent a dovish pivot narrative. But it no longer provides clean cover for a straightforward multi-hike extension.

The sequence remains important. December labor re-acceleration and the still-low unemployment rate were central to opening the hiking cycle. By March, the texture had changed. February employment still rose strongly, but full-time employment fell, part-time surged, hours worked declined, and unemployment rose to 4.3%. That is not collapse. It is an early-softening signal. It says the labor market is no longer uniformly tight across the quality metrics that matter for wage and services inflation persistence.

The public tape broadly treats this correctly: labor is still firm in level, softer in quality. That means labor has not yet become the dominant reason to pause, but it has become an increasingly important reason not to extrapolate the hawkish path too easily. It also means the market is focusing less on one payroll headline and more on whether the labor mix continues to deteriorate alongside weaker confidence and softer services demand.

The key labor implication for policy is not that the RBA has lost the labor argument altogether. It is that labor no longer resolves the trade-off in the RBA’s favor. If the next labor prints show unemployment drifting higher and the full-time / hours mix staying weak, labor becomes a real support for plateau logic later in the cycle. If labor re-stabilizes while inflation remains hot, the hawkish market narrative regains momentum quickly.

Inflation

Inflation remains the binding macro constraint, but the market narrative around inflation has become more hawkish than the prior note allowed. The old framing was “headline disinflation has improved, but core remains sticky.” That is still true as a backward-looking statement. It is no longer sufficient as a market-facing statement.

The public tape now treats the energy shock not as a tail but as a central risk to the policy path. Headline inflation is expected by some major domestic institutions to re-accelerate materially through mid-2026, and the core debate is whether that energy impulse stays mostly in headline or bleeds into broader non-energy prices. That second-round pass-through question is now the key inflation narrative. The market is not simply waiting to confirm that inflation is sticky; it is actively debating whether inflation is about to get worse again before growth has had time to absorb the prior tightening.

That is why February CPI softness was not meaningful policy clearance. It was pre-shock, backward-looking, and still left trimmed mean sticky enough to keep the RBA uneasy. The unresolved issue is no longer just whether core is slowly improving. It is whether Q1 trimmed mean confirms that the disinflation process has stalled or reversed in practice. That is why the late-April CPI release has become the single most important domestic macro catalyst.

A further nuance the draft had underweighted is that not all energy scenarios are the same. A pure price shock is straightforwardly inflationary. A genuine fuel shortage could eventually become demand-destructive and therefore less inflationary further out. That bifurcation matters because it means the inflation path is not mechanically one-way, even if the near-term bias has clearly shifted upward.

So the correct inflation hierarchy now is:

headline re-acceleration risk has moved center-stage,
core remains sticky and may rise further through pass-through,
the scale of second-round effects is still unresolved,
and policy cannot assume the inflation problem is fading on its own.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The last RBA meeting, on 17 March 2026, delivered a 25bp hike to 4.10% on a 5–4 split. The move itself mattered, but the reaction function mattered more. The majority still judged inflation and domestic capacity pressure strong enough to warrant further tightening. The minority judged the growth and uncertainty side serious enough to justify holding. That split is the clearest expression of the current policy bind.

The market interpretation of that meeting has evolved. Initially, the split could be read as “hawkish but close to peak.” The newer public tape is more nuanced. It still reads the split as evidence of rising internal resistance, but it does not interpret it as a clean plateau signal. Instead, it reads it as a timing disagreement inside a still-hawkish reaction function, with May remaining very much live and perhaps now the modal next step depending on Q1 inflation.

A further nuance now matters: the debate over whether policy is clearly restrictive has reopened. If neutral has drifted higher — because inflation expectations, energy risk, or global rate structures have shifted — then 4.10% may not be delivering as much restraint as the market previously assumed. That makes the policy path more dangerous in both directions: more hikes may be needed to regain control, but those hikes may hit an economy that is already visibly weakening in the forward indicators.

Current policy assumption

Base case has shifted. It is no longer plateau first at 4.10%. The public-market base case is now May hike as the modal near-term outcome, with the more important disagreement centered on terminal rate rather than on whether the RBA hikes once more. A more dovish path would require a softer-than-expected Q1 trimmed mean, further labor-quality deterioration, and evidence that the March PMI / confidence shock is not a one-off but a broader demand rollover. A more hawkish path would require visible second-round fuel pass-through into core, inflation expectations pressure, and evidence that current settings are not sufficiently restrictive to bring inflation back to target. The reaction function remains inflation-dominant, but growth, labor quality, and the level of restrictiveness now matter much more than they did a month ago.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is now trading a much more explicit market narrative than before. The first question is May. The second question is no longer “then plateau or cut later?” but “is May terminal, or is this the start of a more extended higher-for-longer phase?” That is an important change. The terminal debate is live, with public views spanning from one more hike to a materially higher peak. That means the rates market is not debating direction so much as degree.

What matters for repricing is clear. Q1 trimmed mean is the binding domestic print. Evidence of second-round pass-through matters almost as much. Labor quality, services stabilization, and confidence matter as constraints rather than primary drivers. In other words, the front end is still trading inflation first — but it is no longer ignoring the growth cost of doing so.

For FX, the market narrative has also become more two-sided. AUD still has structural support from relative rates and from the fact that the RBA remains more hawkish than much of DM. But the public tape is now explicit that AUD is not trading as a pure rates-differential currency. It is increasingly a dual-driver currency: relative-rate support on one side, and growth / China / risk-sentiment sensitivity on the other. That means AUD can still outperform when delayed easing and inflation persistence dominate, but it weakens quickly when the market shifts toward global risk aversion, weaker domestic demand, or softer China-linked growth expectations.

There is also an underappreciated nuance here: AUD strength itself can partly offset tradable inflation, acting as a form of “tightening in stealth.” The market does not treat that as a substitute for rate hikes, but it does treat it as a partial offset in the inflation equation. That should not be overstated, but it does complicate a one-way inflation pass-through narrative.

Financial conditions remain restrictive mainly through housing, construction, mortgage-sensitive demand, and confidence. There is still no sign of generalized funding stress. The more interesting question now is not whether financial conditions are tight somewhere. It is whether the RBA itself believes overall settings are tight enough.

Political / Policy Events

In that sense, political risk is no longer merely a tail. It is now embedded in the macro narrative via the inflation channel.
