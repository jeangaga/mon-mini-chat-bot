India — HSBC Services PMI (Mar)

Release Date: 2026-04-06 | Local Time: 07:00 IST
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

HSBC: SERVICES - BUSINESS ACTIVITY: 57.5 | Prior: 57.2 | Poll: 57.4 | Surprise: 0.10

Professional Market Commentary

The institutional/public tape was constructive on the headline but materially less clean on the internals. The final March reading was revised up versus flash and remained well above 50, but commentary converged on a softer momentum story: weakest services expansion in 14 months, domestic new business losing speed, export demand acting as the main offset, and input-cost pressure jumping to the highest rate in nearly four years.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is still an expansion print, but the quality of the expansion deteriorated. The key hierarchy is: headline resilience first, domestic demand slowdown second, export offset third, and cost shock fourth. That order matters because the policy signal is not “services strong,” but rather “services still growing while the mix becomes more externally reliant and more inflationary.” Margin compression also matters: firms are not fully passing through the cost shock, which prevents an immediate CPI-style inflation inference but confirms a squeeze building inside the sector.

State transition: continuation with deterioration sub-signal

Context from prior regional live file:
No prior EM release text was available in the current regional live file at this checkpoint, so this print functions as an opening-week anchor rather than a confirmation or reversal of an already-written EM sequence.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For EM week purposes, this is supportive of the India template now visible across the region: growth still holds above stall speed, but the oil/freight/input-cost shock is progressively tightening the RBI’s easing window. Domestic softness alone would keep cut optionality alive; domestic softness plus rising cost pressure pushes the decision into an oil-path conditional, not a clean growth-support reaction function. June becomes the next live verification point rather than April changing the regime.

The tape treated this as a clear deterioration event, even if short-form coverage softened the language by calling it near-stagnation. Comment — Economist Layer

This is the cleanest stagflationary activity signal in the EM file so far. The internal hierarchy is straightforward: composite sub-50 matters most; services near-stagnation confirms the private-demand slowdown is not just manufacturing noise; new orders falling again confirms weakening forward demand; and cost acceleration means the soft patch does not mechanically reopen easing space. The sequential drop is large enough that the “marginal contraction” framing understates the signal. This is not a one-line soft print. It is a regime-quality deterioration because the demand side is rolling over at the same time price pressure is moving the wrong way.

South Africa — Std Bank Whole Economy PMI (Mar)

Release Date: 2026-04-07 | Local Time: 09:15 SAST
Importance: ****

Reuters Data

Std Bank Whole Econ PMI: 50.8 | Prior: 50.0

Professional Market Commentary

The headline was framed as a return to growth, but the better-anchored tape emphasized a bifurcated report: output and employment improved, yet new orders fell again, export sales recorded their sharpest decline in more than two years, delivery times lengthened, and business confidence slipped into net pessimism. This was one of the clearest cases this week where a headline-above-50 framing risked overstating resilience.

Comment — Economist Layer

The print is analytically important because it separates current activity from forward demand. Output likely benefited from inventory rebuilding, new projects and short-horizon operational adjustments, but the forward-looking components deteriorated sharply. Export weakness and longer supplier lead times are the key macro signals because they embed the external-shock channel directly into the survey. The collapse in confidence is the decisive detail: a headline above 50 with record pessimism is not a clean recovery signal. It is a fragile current-output improvement sitting on top of weaker demand and worse forward conditions.

State transition: false dawn

Context from prior regional live file:
No prior EM release text was available in the current regional live file at this checkpoint, so this block serves as the first written South Africa soft-activity signal of the week.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This complicates the SARB map rather than easing it. A simple growth slowdown would have supported a softer policy read; instead, South Africa gets a misleadingly positive headline with negative internals and fresh cost/logistics pressure. For strategy, the print is more valuable as a warning against superficial headline interpretation than as a bullish activity signal. The next verification test is whether hard data confirm the internal weakness. That test arrived quickly with manufacturing.

Brazil — Trade Balance (Mar)

Release Date: 2026-04-07 | Local Time: 20:00 BRT
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Trade Balance: 6.405B | Prior: 4.208B | Revised: 4.038B | Poll: 7.350B | Surprise: -0.9450B

Professional Market Commentary

The public/official tape split between two valid framings: lowest March surplus since 2020 and, simultaneously, still a large surplus supported by very strong oil exports. The better interpretation is compositional: exports were high, but imports were also record-strong, and the March balance was flattered by a geopolitical oil windfall rather than a broad-based structural improvement in Brazil’s external position.

Comment — Economist Layer

The main signal is not the surplus level itself but what drove it. Oil shipments and China-related demand provided a temporary lift, while the import side remained heavy. That means the underlying external picture is weaker than the headline suggests. The country’s trade mix is becoming more commodity-dependent at the margin, and that matters because commodity strength can cushion the top line without improving domestic cyclical quality. The record import bill is also not a clean demand-positive signal in this context; part of it reflects input and vehicle imports rather than a simple domestic boom.

State transition: false dawn

Context from prior regional live file:
No prior EM release text was available in the current regional live file at this checkpoint, so this trade block is treated as an opening external-account marker for the week.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For BRL and Brazil macro mapping, this is only modestly positive. Commodity/oil support helps near-term external resilience, but the composition is too nonrecurring to upgrade the structural story. For Copom, the trade print matters less through growth and more through the inflation/import-composition angle: if the oil windfall fades and the import bill stays high, the buffer looks thinner than the headline surplus implies.

Gross FX Reserves: 58.42B | Prior: 55.29B

Professional Market Commentary

The tape around Turkey was one of the week’s few clearly positive surprises. Public reporting emphasized a roughly $11 billion jump in net reserves in a single week and a broader reversal higher in total reserves after the earlier war-related drawdown. The split between a much larger net increase and a smaller gross increase pointed to balance-sheet improvement that may have included liability or swap effects, not just simple reserve accumulation.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is best read as stabilisation, not full repair. The net build is large enough to matter, especially because Turkey’s binding constraint is reserve adequacy and credibility under an orthodox framework. The gap between gross and net changes implies that the quality of the improvement likely includes a reduction in reserve liabilities or other balance-sheet normalization, which is better than a small gross build would look on its own. But one strong week does not erase the fragility exposed during the early-shock period.

State transition: stabilization
