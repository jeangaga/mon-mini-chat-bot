SOUTH AFRICA — M3 Money Supply YY / Pvt Sector Credit Ext (Feb)
Release Date: 30 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 08:00

Reuters Data

M3 Money Supply YY: 8.39% | Prior: 7.44%
Pvt Sector Credit Ext: 10.50% | Prior: 8.83% | Poll: 9.10% | Surprise: 1.400%

Professional Market Commentary

Accessible market coverage treated the release as a sign that domestic liquidity and lending growth were still firm even as the SARB had just shifted to a more cautious hold under the Middle East oil shock. The visible institutional framing was not that this forces an immediate policy move, but that it reduces comfort around any near-term easing because fuel-driven inflation risk is already rising while credit creation is accelerating.

Comment — Economist Layer

The key signal is not money growth alone; it is the joint re-acceleration in broad money and a sharp upside surprise in private credit. Credit growth at 10.5% y/y moves materially above January’s 8.83% and above consensus, which is more important than the M3 pickup because it suggests financing conditions were not yet transmitting as restrictive enough to cool domestic borrowing meaningfully. In the current South African macro setup, that matters because the SARB had already warned that oil and rand weakness could push fuel inflation above 18% in Q2. Stronger credit in that environment is not a clean growth-positive; it is a policy-constraint signal. State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior EM trajectory for South Africa was already moving away from a benign disinflation story and toward a cautious-hold regime, with external energy risk and rand weakness becoming more important. This release fits that shift by showing that domestic monetary/credit dynamics were not cooling enough to offset the imported inflation problem.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For rates and ZAR, this is mildly hawkish on the margin because it narrows the room for SARB dovish re-engagement. The release does not force a policy response in isolation, but alongside the bank’s post-Iran-war inflation warnings it reinforces the idea that South Africa’s next clean easing window has moved further out unless growth data deteriorates more sharply.

BRAZIL — IGP-M Inflation Index (Mar)
Release Date: 30 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 13:00

Reuters Data

IGP-M Inflation Index: 0.52% | Prior: -0.73% | Poll: 0.52% | Surprise: 0.000%

Professional Market Commentary

The official FGV release and market summaries framed March as a reversal from February’s deflationary print back into positive wholesale-price territory. Commentary highlighted that producer-side and fuel-derivative pressure was beginning to re-enter the index, which matters more for signal extraction than the in-line consensus outcome itself.

Comment — Economist Layer

An in-line print does not mean a neutral signal. IGP-M swung from -0.73% to +0.52% m/m, pushing the year-to-date measure back positive while the 12-month rate remained negative. That combination says near-term price pressure is reappearing before the annual base has fully normalized. Because IGP-M is wholesale-sensitive and quick to absorb commodity and fuel shocks, this is best read as pipeline reflation rather than consumer-demand overheating. In the current global setup, the relevant point is that Brazil is once again showing oil/derivatives pass-through in fast-moving price measures. State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior Brazil/EM trajectory was already one of disinflation progress becoming less clean as external energy risks rose. This release reinforces that complication at the pipeline level rather than overturning the broader inflation story outright.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For BCB, this is not meeting-changing by itself, but it is another reason to avoid aggressive easing. Pipeline measures turning up while the central bank is already stressing caution around the oil shock support a shallower easing path rather than a clean re-opening of cuts.

CHINA (MAINLAND) — NBS Manufacturing PMI (Mar)
Release Date: 31 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 03:30

Reuters Data

NBS Manufacturing PMI: 50.4 | Prior: 49.0 | Poll: 50.1 | Surprise: 0.30

Professional Market Commentary

Official and wire coverage framed the March rebound as a post-holiday recovery in production and demand, with the headline returning to expansion and the strongest reading in a year. But the institutional narrative was not unambiguously bullish: output and new orders improved materially, while export orders and employment remained below 50, and the surge in input and output prices was explicitly tied to the Middle East conflict’s effect on oil, chemicals, and logistics.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a genuine improvement print, but not a clean regime upgrade. The best part of the report is breadth: production and new orders moved back above 50, non-manufacturing also returned to expansion, and the composite improved. The limiting features are equally important: employment is still contracting, new export orders remain sub-50, and the price subindices surged hard. That means the signal is “activity stabilization plus cost-push complication,” not simply “China re-accelerating.” In macro hierarchy, the rebound in demand/production is real enough to reject an outright false print, but the price impulse stops this from becoming a clean easing-friendly recovery story. State transition: stabilization.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior China/EM trajectory was one of uneven domestic demand but persistent industrial and export resilience. March NBS PMI fits as a stabilization step within that regime, not yet as proof of a broader domestic-demand inflection.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For PBOC and China-linked EM FX, the print reduces immediate hard-landing anxiety but complicates the policy mix. Stronger activity is supportive for the regional growth floor, yet the visible oil/logistics cost transmission into Chinese factory prices narrows the clean disinflation backdrop that would have made further easing easier to justify.

This is not classic demand overheating; the visible composition points to fuel and energy. That distinction matters because energy-led inflation is precisely the type that can complicate an easing cycle without saying much good about underlying growth. So the correct interpretation is not “inflation shock,” but “cleaner evidence that imported energy pressure is re-entering the CPI basket.” State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior Poland/CEE trajectory was one of weak demand and improving disinflation that had opened room for NBP easing. March CPI does not fully break that story, but it clearly complicates it and weakens the confidence of a smooth follow-through cutting path.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For NBP and PLN, this is near-term supportive for rates and currency because it limits confidence in further quick cuts. The key next test is whether April data confirms that energy/fuel pass-through is persisting; if yes, the market will have to shift from “easing continuation” toward “pause with renewed upside inflation caution.”
