POLAND — Retail Sales / Retail Sales at Constant Prices (Feb)
Release Date: 23 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 09:30

Reuters Data

Retail Sales YY: 4.3% | Prior: 3.9% | Poll: 5.4% | Surprise: -1.10%
Retail Sales Cons Price: 5.0% | Prior: 4.4% | Poll: 6.1% | Surprise: -1.10%

Professional Market Commentary

Official and market framing treated this as a still-positive consumer print, but one that disappointed versus expectations for a stronger February rebound. GUS reported real retail sales up 5.0% y/y, with broad category gains, while open institutional commentary described the result as middling rather than strong and noted weather as a likely drag.

Comment — Economist Layer

The signal is mildly constructive, not cleanly bullish. Real retail demand is still expanding and accelerated from January, so the household side is not rolling over. But the miss versus consensus matters because this was supposed to be an easier beat in a benign-inflation setting. The right read is consumer resilience with less upside impulse than expected.

State transition: stabilization

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior Poland sequence in the EM live file had already become one of orderly disinflation and a somewhat improved external cushion, with the current-account surprise adding resilience but not changing the core macro story. February retail fits that setup: demand is still expanding, but not in a way that forces a growth re-upgrade.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For PLN and Poland rates, this is not the kind of demand beat that re-prices the easing debate. It modestly supports the idea that domestic demand is holding together, but the miss versus poll keeps the release in the “helpful, not regime-changing” bucket. The next verification test remains whether labor and industry keep the soft-landing structure intact.

That keeps the CBRT reaction function biased toward protection rather than renewed easing confidence.

TUESDAY 24 MAR 2026

INDIA — HSBC Mfg Flash PMI / HSBC Services Flash PMI / HSBC Composite Flash PMI (Mar)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 06:00

Reuters Data

HSBC Mfg Flash PMI: 53.8 | Prior: 56.9 | Poll: 56.8 | Surprise: -3.00
HSBC Services Flash PMI: 57.2 | Prior: 58.1 | Poll: 58.3 | Surprise: -1.10
HSBC Composite Flash PMI: 56.5 | Prior: 58.9 | Poll: 59.0 | Surprise: -2.50

Professional Market Commentary

Institutional tape framed this as a meaningful March slowdown, but not a collapse. The key narrative was softer domestic demand, still-strong export orders, and a sharp rise in input costs linked to energy and commodity pressures. The important nuance is that “multi-year low” framing is directionally right on momentum, but can overstate severity given that all three indices remain comfortably above 50.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a genuine deterioration in momentum, but still within expansion territory. The internal hierarchy matters: domestic demand softened materially; export orders remained unusually strong; input costs accelerated sharply; and firms appear to be absorbing part of the cost shock through margins rather than fully passing it on. So the signal is not simple growth weakness. It is a more difficult mix: softer domestic demand, resilient external demand, and a renewed cost squeeze. That makes the release more stagflationary than recessionary. Manufacturing looks more exposed to the shock mix than services, but both sides decelerated enough to challenge the prior upswing narrative.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior India sequence in the EM live file had been one of resilient activity, contained-but-firmer inflation, and no clear case for a near-term policy pivot. March flash PMIs weaken the activity side of that setup, but they do not replace it with a contraction story. The key change is that growth softness is now arriving alongside stronger cost pressure and margin compression.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the RBI, this is not a clean easing signal. Slower domestic demand would normally help doves, but the input-cost surge and pass-through risk reduce the room for an easy growth-first response. For INR, the release reinforces the vulnerability to imported energy pressure: activity is still expanding, but the macro mix has become less comfortable.

TAIWAN — Industrial Output YY (Feb)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 09:00

Reuters Data

Industrial Output YY: 17.83% | Prior: 28.51% | Revised: 27.88%

Professional Market Commentary

The better institutional framing is that the headline deceleration overstates the underlying softness. Official commentary still points to very strong AI- and semiconductor-linked manufacturing demand, while prior Taiwan reporting had already warned that January strength was partly flattered by Lunar New Year timing. The tape should therefore be read as moderation from an extreme January pace, not as evidence of a fundamental demand break.

Comment — Economist Layer

The headline slowdown from 27.88% to 17.83% y/y is mechanically large, but the composition makes it much less bearish than the one-line print suggests. Manufacturing remained very strong, the weakness was concentrated outside the core tech engine, and the holiday-adjusted read still points to unusually strong output levels. The important analytical distinction is therefore: AI/semiconductor-linked production remains structurally strong; traditional sectors remain much weaker; and the February y/y slowdown is heavily influenced by calendar effects. This is continuation, not inflection. The real macro message is deepening divergence inside Taiwan’s industrial base rather than broad industrial cooling.

State transition: continuation

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior EM live file had not yet contained a direct Taiwan hard-data block, but the broader regional context already pointed to resilient Asia tech demand. February industrial output now confirms that pulse directly. What matters is not the softer headline alone, but that the tech-manufacturing core remains very strong after proper calendar adjustment.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For TWD and the broader EM Asia tech complex, this remains supportive. It preserves the distinction between economies still benefiting from AI capex and external tech demand, and economies already feeling more direct growth or balance-of-payments stress. The Middle East energy shock is a secondary watch item for non-tech manufacturing, not yet the dominant story for Taiwan’s industrial core.

MEXICO — IGAE Economic Activity (Jan)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 13:00

Reuters Data

IGAE Econ Activity YY: -0.3% | Prior: 3.3% | Poll: 1.6% | Surprise: -1.90%

Professional Market Commentary

Institutional framing should be firmer here: this was not a small miss, but a real growth downside shock. The official release showed seasonally adjusted activity down 0.9% m/m and only 0.5% y/y, while the original annual series printed -0.3%. The main tape read is that December’s stronger annual print overstated underlying momentum and January stripped that distortion away.

Comment — Economist Layer

This materially worsens the Mexico growth picture. The right hierarchy is: the seasonally adjusted monthly fall is large; the original annual series turned negative; weakness was not confined to one narrow industrial category; and manufacturing remained a major drag while services were only marginally positive. That means the slowdown is broader than the earlier industrial-output miss alone suggested. This is the kind of print that upgrades concern from “sector weakness” to “economy-wide momentum loss.” The growth story is now clearly weaker entering the Banxico meeting.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior Mexico sequence had already become more cautious after the industrial-output miss and the earlier interruption to the disinflation story. January IGAE confirms that the industrial weakness was not just noise. Instead of offsetting the prior downside, the broader activity proxy validates and deepens it.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For Banxico, this is the strongest growth-side argument for a dovish lean that Mexico has printed recently. But it does not stand alone because inflation also updated the same day. The strategic implication is not “easy cut,” but “growth is weakening quickly enough that inflation tolerance and communication discipline now matter even more.”

Reuters Data

1st Half-Mth Core Infl MM: 0.22% | Prior: 0.22% | Poll: 0.22% | Surprise: 0.000%
1st Half-Mth Infl MM: 0.62% | Prior: 0.25% | Poll: 0.37% | Surprise: 0.250%

Professional Market Commentary

The tape should lean more explicitly into the policy bind. Headline inflation overshot meaningfully, but the composition still leaves Banxico a possible “look-through” argument because volatile food and travel categories drove much of the surprise while core m/m stayed in line. So the release is not a clean inflation re-acceleration, but it is still bad enough to tighten the policy trade-off materially.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is an uncomfortable inflation print for Banxico. The main tension is between headline urgency and core restraint: headline inflation accelerated sharply; non-core drove much of the surprise; core m/m held at 0.22%; and annual core remained sticky rather than benign. So the “temporary factors” argument is still available, but it is narrower than before because headline is now clearly above the upper tolerance band and the core anchor is not soft enough to fully neutralize the shock. Combined with the IGAE release, this is a genuine stagflationary mix: weaker growth, but not enough inflation relief.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior Mexico inflation sequence had already interrupted the smooth-disinflation narrative by taking headline CPI back above 4% while leaving core/services sticky. Early-March inflation intensifies that interruption. The growth side is now weaker too, which makes the policy trade-off more acute rather than easier.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For Banxico, this raises the bar for a straightforward cut narrative. A move is still possible if policymakers emphasize the volatile composition of the headline shock, but any dovish interpretation now needs stronger conditional language. For MXN and front-end rates, the message is two-way risk, not clean easing validation.
