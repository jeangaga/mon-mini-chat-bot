A. SOUTH AFRICA / ZAR — 3-WEEK MACRO SYNTHESIS
South Africa’s three-week tape shifted from apparent headline resilience to a weaker and more policy-constrained ZAR regime. The activity side never validated a clean recovery: whole-economy PMI moved above 50, but internals were poor; manufacturing production then confirmed weak hard-data momentum; mining beat, but with base-effect and PGM concentration caveats; and February retail sales later missed sharply, weakening the household-demand cushion. The result is not a collapse call, but a clear downgrade from “mixed resilience” to “fragile activity with narrow offsets.”
The inflation and policy side prevents a simple dovish read. Reserves declined from record highs but remained a buffer rather than a stress signal, while March CPI looked benign at headline level but turned less comfortable in composition, with core/services reaccelerating before the April fuel shock enters the data. For ZAR, the regime is therefore asymmetric: weak growth limits domestic-demand optimism, but SARB easing room stays constrained by inflation composition, fuel/import risk, and the need to preserve credibility. ZAR is not a pure weak-growth short; it is a fragile-growth / constrained-policy currency where external shock sensitivity matters more than the 3.1% CPI headline.

A. SOUTH AFRICA WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS
This grouped window delivered the first complete South Africa macro picture of the monitoring sequence, covering inflation, retail, mining, the SARB rate decision, and a further reserve update. The December CPI prints on January 21 confirmed the benign inflation regime: headline at 3.6% y/y (consensus 3.6%) and 0.2% m/m were exactly in line, keeping inflation well within the SARB's 3–6% tolerance band and removing any near-term pressure for hawkish action. The December retail sales on the same day disappointed at +2.6% y/y (consensus 3.1%), consistent with restrictive rates biting on consumer spending. The November mining production print on January 20 was the week's most alarming single data point: -2.7% y/y (consensus +3.9%, prior +6.1%) was a massive miss and a sharp reversal from prior strength, injecting a hard-data growth downside shock that immediately complicated any clean dovish read on the inflation data. The SARB repo rate hold at 6.75% on January 29 was fully in line, confirming the patient posture. The January reserve update on February 5 then provided the window's closing signal: net reserves at $74.9B (prior $71.1B) and gross at $80.2B (prior $75.9B) showed a further improvement of approximately $3.7B net — the second consecutive reserve build. The regime across this window is therefore: inflation benign and within band, consumer demand softening under restrictive rates, mining delivering a hard-data growth shock, SARB patient at peak with gradual easing the directional bias, and external buffers improving for a second consecutive month.
