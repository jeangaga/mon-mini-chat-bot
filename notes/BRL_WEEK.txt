A. BRAZIL / BRL — 3-WEEK MACRO SYNTHESIS

Brazil’s three-week tape moved into a softer-growth / still-constrained-inflation regime rather than a clean easing setup. The activity side moderated: services PMI fell to the edge of stagnation, composite slipped below 50, retail y/y slowed sharply, services activity stayed resilient in level terms but softer than expected, and IBC-Br remained positive but decelerating. The growth message is therefore not recession, but fading thrust under high rates.

The policy constraint is that the inflation and external mix did not validate a simple dovish repricing. IGP-DI flipped sharply positive, IPCA beat consensus with food and transport doing most of the work, and later external data showed the current-account deficit wider than expected with FDI below forecast. Brazil is still externally financeable and not in a Turkey-style reserve-stress regime, but the cushion narrowed. For BRL, the tape is mildly negative at the margin: not crisis, not hard landing, but less growth comfort, renewed consumer-price pressure, and a COPOM reaction function that cannot treat soft activity as a clean easing signal.

Growth: -
Supporting evidence:

Services activity later rose only 0.1% m/m versus 0.5% expected, retail sales slowed to just +0.2% y/y, and IBC-Br rose 0.6% m/m but decelerated from January. The aggregate read is positive-but-moderating hard activity with survey deterioration, not reacceleration.

Inflation: -

Financial Conditions: ~

The March trade surplus was supported by oil exports but flattered by a record import bill, while March current account missed at -$6.036bn and FDI undershot at $6.037bn. Policy Constraint: -

Softer PMIs and weak retail support the growth case for cuts, but IPCA above consensus, IGP-DI pipeline pressure, food/transport pass-through, and mild external deterioration prevent a clean dovish reaction function. Brazil remains “rates biting, but inflation/fiscal/external constraints still dominate.”

A. BRL WEEK — MACRO SYNTHESIS

Brazil’s January window established the policy bind that would frame the rest of Q1: growth was clearly softening under restrictive rates, but not collapsing enough to force an emergency BCB pivot. The opening week showed hard and soft activity deterioration, with industrial output y/y turning negative, services PMI nearly stalling, and composite PMI moving below 50. Inflation was decelerating on a y/y basis but still not frictionless, while the trade balance provided a useful external cushion.

The 12–30 Jan block then complicated the bearish growth read. Services output missed, but retail sales beat strongly and IBC-Br rebounded 0.70% m/m, shifting the regime from “clean downturn” toward “choppy stabilization.” The Selic hold at 15.00% confirmed peak-rate conditions but no easing trigger yet. The correct merged-window read is therefore restrictive-rate growth softening, consumer/activity resilience preventing a hard-landing call, inflation progress still insufficient for an immediate dovish pivot, and BCB easing direction building but timing/pacing still gated by IPCA credibility.
