UNITED STATES — National Activity Index (Feb)
Release Date: 23 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 13:30

Reuters Data

National Activity Index: -0.11 | Prior: 0.18 | Revised: 0.20

Professional Market Commentary

The scout improves the tape here by shifting the emphasis away from the noisy headline and toward the smoother trend read. The dominant accessible framing was that February moved back below zero, but the more important signal remained the three-month average near trend rather than any recession-style break. Professional commentary also treated the employment bucket as the notable soft spot inside the release, while the headline itself was not considered market-moving in isolation.

Comment — Economist Layer

The stronger read is: softer breadth, but not a structural growth break. The scout is useful because it identifies the internal hierarchy correctly. February’s monthly CFNAI slipped back into negative territory after January’s positive print, but the smoothed signal remained close to trend, which means the release is better read as continuation of below-trend expansion than as a fresh recession warning. The important new nuance is that the weakness appears more employment-led than demand-collapse-led, which fits the broader late-March session better than an undifferentiated “growth is breaking” narrative. State transition: continuation with softer breadth.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file had already ended with a mixed but not recessionary configuration: manufacturing surveys had softened unevenly, hard production data held up better, housing stayed fragile, and PPI materially worsened the inflation pipeline. In that sequence, CFNAI does not create a new regime; it extends the “growth below trend, not collapsing” story while adding one more hint that labor softness is broadening at the margin.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the Fed, this remains background information rather than a direct reaction-function input. The more relevant strategic use is corroborative: it leans against a re-acceleration growth story, but it does not offset the inflation and cost-pressure problem already present elsewhere in the file. In market terms, it is soft-growth confirmation, not a standalone dovish trigger.

UNITED STATES — Construction Spending MM (Jan)
Release Date: 23 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 15:00

Reuters Data

Construction Spending MM: -0.3% | Prior: 0.8% | Poll: 0.1% | Surprise: -0.40%

Professional Market Commentary

The official construction release showed a weaker January headline driven by private-sector softness, with private construction down 0.6%, residential down 0.8%, nonresidential down 0.4%, and public construction up 0.6%. The institutional read remained that the miss was private-led and therefore more economically relevant than the aggregate headline alone.

Comment — Economist Layer

This remains a genuine growth miss, and the composition is the key. Private and residential weakness matter more than the aggregate because those are the buckets most sensitive to financing conditions and domestic confidence. Public construction cushioned the total, but that is offsetting noise rather than cyclical repair. In the context of the prior USD housing sequence, this release still says that interest-sensitive domestic demand was weakening before the Tuesday PMI slowdown arrived. State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The previous USD live file already described housing as a weak pocket that was stabilizing only conditionally. NAHB had improved from a low base, pending home sales looked like a fragile rate-window bounce, and MBA mortgage rates had already started moving back up. January construction spending fits that pre-existing structure rather than changing it.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is modestly dovish on growth, but only at the margin. Strategically it matters less as a direct Fed signal than as confirmation that tighter financing conditions are still transmitting into private domestic activity. Unless later-week claims crack more decisively, housing/construction remains a vulnerable growth channel rather than a policy game-changer.

UNITED STATES — Unit Labor Costs Revised / Productivity Revised (Q4)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 13:30

Reuters Data

Unit Labor Costs Revised: 4.4% | Prior: 2.8% | Poll: 3.5% | Surprise: 0.90%
Productivity Revised: 1.8% | Prior: 2.8% | Poll: 2.0% | Surprise: -0.20%

Professional Market Commentary

The scout materially improves this block by separating the quarterly hawkish surprise from the less alarming annual trend. Reuters and BLS both support that distinction: the Q4 revision was clearly worse than expected, but the move was heavily tied to weaker revised GDP growth and stronger compensation, while the broader productivity trend remained relatively solid. Institutional commentary quoted by Reuters was notably more measured than the headline reaction, emphasizing that the annual labor-cost trend stayed within a tolerable range even if the quarterly pace was inconsistent with a clean 2% inflation path.

Comment — Economist Layer

The scout’s key contribution is the bifurcation: deterioration in the quarter, continuation in the trend. That is the right economist-layer conclusion. The Fed-relevant problem is real because 4.4% unit labor costs versus a 3.5% poll is an upside surprise, and the downward productivity revision removes some of the cushion for absorbing compensation gains. But it is also true that this is partly a backward-looking GDP-revision story rather than a fresh real-time inflation shock. The right signal, then, is not “new cost regime,” but “last-mile disinflation still lacks enough margin for error.” State transition: deterioration in the quarterly signal, continuation in the broader trend.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file had already shifted into a worse inflation-growth mix after the hot PPI block and hawkish-hold FOMC. This revised productivity/ULC release deepens that tension rather than changing its nature. It strengthens the idea that softer activity alone is not enough to reopen easy policy optionality if cost transmission remains uncomfortable.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Strategically this is hold-supportive and cut-negative, but less regime-changing than the raw quarterly surprise suggests. The scout is right to separate structural from backward-looking inflation channels: this release tightens the Fed’s trade-off, but it does not by itself prove a new wage-cost spiral. Markets can react hawkishly to the surprise while macro interpretation remains more conditional.

UNITED STATES — S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash / Svcs PMI Flash / Comp PMI Flash (Mar)
Release Date: 24 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 14:45

Reuters Data

S&P Global Mfg PMI Flash: 52.4 | Prior: 51.6 | Poll: 51.3 | Surprise: 1.10
S&P Global Svcs PMI Flash: 51.1 | Prior: 51.7 | Poll: 51.5 | Surprise: -0.40
S&P Global Comp PMI Flash: 51.4 | Prior: 51.9

Professional Market Commentary

This is the block most improved by the scout, because the dominant professional framing is now clearer: war-shock-driven stagflation corridor, not recession. Reuters and S&P both support the same narrative arc. Composite activity slowed to an 11-month low, services led the weakness, manufacturing outperformed, employment fell for the first time in over a year, and both input and output price gauges accelerated sharply. The scout is also right that the “stagflation” label is not journalist exaggeration here; it comes from S&P Global’s own chief economist and from S&P’s own growth/inflation proxy language.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is the most important release in the file so far because it integrates growth, labor, and inflation in one current-state snapshot. The internal hierarchy matters. Manufacturing improved and therefore remains a partial buffer, but services — the larger macro bucket — softened to an 11-month low, and employment slipped into contraction for the first time in 13 months. That is the leading soft-growth signal. At the same time, price measures accelerated sharply, with S&P linking the move explicitly to the Middle East war through energy and supply channels. The scout usefully sharpens one more distinction: the war shock explains part of the March inflation impulse, but it may also be amplifying pre-existing service-side softness rather than creating it from scratch. State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file had already converged toward an uncomfortable mix: hard production data were holding up better than soft surveys, housing was fragile, and PPI had already pushed the inflation pipeline hotter. The March flash PMI now unifies those fragments into a more coherent regime question: softer private-sector momentum, especially in services and employment, but with inflation pressure still too firm for a clean dovish turn.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a clean growth-down, yields-down release. It is a genuine Fed-dilemma print. The strategist split now needs to distinguish between war-shock inflation and underlying domestic cost pressure, because both are present in the week but through different channels. If the war shock fades quickly, the Fed can remain patient. If later-week data keep inflation expectations or import prices elevated while claims stay only stable, the restrictive-hold bias hardens further.

Context from prior regional live file:

For markets, this slightly reduces the downside-growth read from the regional survey bucket. For policy, it does very little on its own because the real issue this week is not whether manufacturing collapsed — it did not — but whether softer growth can coexist with still-uncomfortable inflation. Richmond manufacturing says the factory side remains more buffer than drag.
