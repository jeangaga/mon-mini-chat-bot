MONDAY 13 APR 2026

United States — Existing Home Sales (Mar)
Release Date: 13 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Existing Home Sales (SAAR): 3.98M | Prior: 4.09M | Revised: 4.13M | Poll: 4.06M | Surprise: -0.080M.

Professional Market Commentary

The official NAR release and the public institutional tape converged on a weak-start framing for the spring selling season: sales fell 3.6% m/m to a nine-month low, all four regions declined m/m, inventory improved only modestly, and prices kept rising because supply still remains structurally scarce. The professional read was not “housing recovery interrupted” so much as “housing still trapped by affordability, weak confidence, and renewed mortgage-rate pressure.”

Comment — Economist Layer

The key signal is transaction freeze rather than price correction. Inventory rose to 1.36 million units and months’ supply increased to 4.1, yet the median existing-home price still rose 1.4% y/y to $408,800, which means the market is clearing through lower volumes rather than through broad price adjustment; the upward February revision matters mechanically, but the move from a revised 4.13M to 3.98M is still a real deterioration in a rate-sensitive sector. NAR’s cut to its 2026 existing-home sales growth forecast, now 4% instead of the prior 14%, reinforces that this is no longer being treated as a temporary wobble.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD sequence had already shown housing as a weak, rate-sensitive downside channel rather than a recovering demand engine. March existing-home sales confirms that trajectory and intensifies it through a sharper transaction drop despite a firmer February base. The housing bucket therefore remains a downside growth channel without yet delivering meaningful price-clearing disinflation.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For macro mapping, this is a clean growth-downside transmission channel rather than a direct Fed mover on its own. It strengthens the case that tighter financial conditions are now biting in the most mortgage-sensitive part of the economy, but because prices remain sticky, housing is not delivering the kind of fast disinflation relief that would ease the policy constraint. The next verification test is whether builder sentiment and later hard activity data show the weakness broadening beyond resale transactions.

TUESDAY 14 APR 2026

United States — NFIB Business Optimism Index (Mar)
Release Date: 14 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 06:00 ET
Importance: **

Reuters Data

NFIB Small Business Optimism Index: 95.8 | Prior: 98.8.

Professional Market Commentary

The official NFIB release showed a clear sentiment deterioration: optimism fell 3.0 points below the 52-year average and the Uncertainty Index rose to 92, well above its long-run average. Reuters’ public framing tied the weakening in sentiment to the oil-price shock around the Iran conflict, while the NFIB release itself emphasized worsening profit trends and a materially more uncertain operating backdrop rather than any clean cyclical rebound story.

Comment — Economist Layer

The important signal is not the headline level alone but the jump in uncertainty. Small-business optimism at 95.8 is below its long-run norm, while uncertainty at 92 implies firms are becoming materially less willing to commit on capex, hiring, and inventory decisions; that makes this more useful as a forward operating-conditions warning than as a contemporaneous hard-growth signal. It does not prove labor-market breakage, but it extends the confidence deterioration from households into owner-operator businesses, where hiring intentions often soften before layoffs do.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already shown collapsing household sentiment and softer labor-demand signals without a claims-based labor break. NFIB extends that same sequence into the small-business channel by showing weaker operating confidence and sharply higher uncertainty. The regime implication is broader growth fragility rather than a labor-market rupture already visible in hard data.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a top-tier policy release, but it matters for regime mapping. If claims and regional surveys soften alongside NFIB, the Fed faces a deeper stagflation bind: confidence and hiring appetite would be deteriorating even as the energy-price channel keeps inflation risks alive. The next verification test is whether the uncertainty spike starts to feed into hiring plans, compensation intentions, and broader labor-leading indicators.

Reuters Data

PPI Final Demand YY: 4.0% | Prior: 3.4% | Poll: 4.7% | Surprise: -0.70%
PPI Final Demand MM: 0.5% | Prior: 0.7% | Revised: 0.5% | Poll: 1.1% | Surprise: -0.60%
PPI ex Food/Energy YY: 3.8% | Prior: 3.9% | Revised: 3.8% | Poll: 4.2% | Surprise: -0.40%
PPI ex Food/Energy MM: 0.1% | Prior: 0.5% | Revised: 0.3% | Poll: 0.5% | Surprise: -0.40%
PPI ex Food/Energy/Trade YY: 3.6% | Prior: 3.5%
PPI ex Food/Energy/Trade MM: 0.2% | Prior: 0.5%
PPI Machine Manufacturing: 196.2 | Prior: 195.9 | Revised: 195.8.

Professional Market Commentary

The institutional tape treated this as a soft print with a harder underlying story. Official BLS details showed headline pressure came overwhelmingly from goods, especially energy and gasoline, while final-demand services were unchanged; Reuters and the broader market read the undershoot as tactical relief against expectations, but not as a clean inflation-clear signal because March likely captures only the first phase of the oil shock flowing through the producer system.

Comment — Economist Layer

The internal hierarchy matters more than the headline miss. Final-demand goods rose 1.6% m/m, with energy up 8.5% and gasoline up 15.7%, while services were flat; core ex food and energy at 0.1% m/m is soft, but the broader ex food, energy, and trade measure still rose 0.2% m/m, and the BLS detail shows some PCE-relevant service categories such as airline passenger services and outpatient care moving higher. The clean read is therefore not “inflation problem solved,” but “pipeline shock confirmed while broad pass-through remains incomplete,” which buys tactical breathing room for March while still pointing to a harder April-May inflation window if services begin to absorb fuel and logistics costs.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already tightened the inflation constraint through ISM prices paid, headline CPI, and higher household inflation expectations, even as core and median measures looked less extreme. March PPI confirms that split by showing an official upstream goods-and-energy shock without a full services breakout yet. The regime therefore remains a harder stagflation bind, but not a fully generalized inflation re-acceleration across all core buckets.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the Fed, this is a constraint-tightening release, but not a clean hawkish-acceleration trigger. It removes some near-term panic because the print undershot badly versus consensus, yet it does not reopen a fast easing path because the energy shock is now visible in official upstream prices and the forward risk is that services catch up later. The next gating variable is not whether March PPI was soft versus poll; it is whether April producer-services and March core PCE show that the goods shock is starting to infect the stickier core.
