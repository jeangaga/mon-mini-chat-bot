MONDAY 30 MAR 2026

UNITED STATES — Dallas Fed Manufacturing Outlook Survey (Mar)
Release Date: 30 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 09:30 CT

Reuters Data

General Business Activity: -0.2 | Prior: 0.2

Professional Market Commentary

Official Dallas Fed framing was that Texas manufacturing activity continued to rise, but more slowly and with materially higher uncertainty. The accessible tape around the release emphasized that the near-zero headline understated the deterioration in forward-looking components, especially company outlook and uncertainty, while respondent commentary tied the macro backdrop to freight-cost pressure, supply disruption and conflict-related uncertainty.

Comment — Economist Layer

The headline alone reads as flat, but the internals are weaker than that. Dallas Fed subcomponents show production at 6.8, new orders at 6.1, capacity utilization at 7.2 and shipments at 1.8, all down from February, while company outlook fell to -3.5 and outlook uncertainty jumped to 26.0 from 6.5. That makes the real signal a forward deterioration rather than an outright collapse in current activity. Current output remained modestly positive, but the willingness to extrapolate demand forward weakened sharply. This is also a more stagflationary factory mix than the headline suggests, because price-pressure measures stayed elevated while confidence deteriorated.
State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file had already moved toward a slower-growth / higher-constraint mix, with manufacturing relatively better than services but not strong enough to resolve the broader macro tension. Dallas manufacturing fits that sequence: it does not create a fresh factory recession signal, but it weakens the idea that manufacturing can offset softer domestic demand.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Strategically, this is not a standalone Fed mover, but it matters as a setup for ISM. If national manufacturing follows Dallas in showing softer orders and worse confidence while prices stay firm, the factory bucket stops acting as a cushion and instead joins the broader soft-growth / inflation-constraint regime.

The dominant housing tape treated January as a backward-looking benchmark rather than a current signal. State transition: deterioration.

Professional Market Commentary

The accessible tape treated March as a cooling print after February’s outsized surge. This is not a contraction signal, but it is a meaningful loss of momentum. At 52.8, Chicago remains above 50 for a third month, so the recovery from the long contraction phase is still real. But the drop from 57.7 and the miss versus consensus argue that February was at least partly a local peak rather than the start of a cleaner upswing. The right read is expansion of lower quality, not renewed industrial strength.
State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The sector is still holding above contraction, but the pace no longer argues for a broad growth re-acceleration.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

The strategic importance is almost entirely as an ISM lead. If ISM weakens in orders and employment, Chicago will look like an early national warning. If ISM holds up, this can remain a post-surge mean reversion rather than a broader regime break.

UNITED STATES — JOLTS Job Openings / Hires / Quits / Layoffs (Feb)
Release Date: 31 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET

Reuters Data

Job Openings: 6.882M | Prior: 6.946M | Revised: 7.240M | Poll: 6.918M
Hires: 4.849M
Quits: 2.97M
Layoffs and Discharges: 1.721M

Professional Market Commentary

The professional tape was clear and consistent: this was a weak report once judged against the revised January base. Reuters emphasized the drop in openings and the slide in hiring to its lowest level since the pandemic onset, while broader commentary converged on a low-hire, low-fire labor market with falling worker confidence and early signs that labor demand is becoming more frozen than merely normalized.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is the strongest deterioration signal in the file so far. The key analytical point is not the small miss versus the openings consensus by itself; it is the full flows picture once revisions are incorporated. January openings were revised up to 7.240M, so February’s 6.882M is a much larger deterioration than the unrevised prior suggests. Hires fell to 4.849M, the lowest since the pandemic start, and quits fell to 2.97M, the lowest since August 2020. That means worker mobility is weakening and firms are not replacing attrition aggressively. Layoffs remain low in level terms, so this is not yet a classic recession-cleansing labor market, but low-fire stops being reassuring when low-hire becomes entrenched. This is now a genuine labor-market freeze signal, not just a softer-opening signal.
State transition: deterioration.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file only had softer labor hints through PMI employment and broader activity composites, while claims had not yet broken decisively. JOLTS moves labor from “cooling at the margin” to “multi-indicator deterioration in the flows that matter for future payroll support.”

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is the week’s clearest dovish-growth input, but not yet a clean easing trigger because the inflation and energy side of the U.S. file still constrains the Fed. If claims and payrolls validate this labor freeze, front-end pricing can lean more convincingly toward growth damage. If they do not, the Fed can still treat this as cooling inside a restrictive-hold regime.

UNITED STATES — Conference Board Consumer Confidence / Present Situation / Expectations (Mar)
Release Date: 31 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET

Reuters Data

Consumer Confidence: 91.8 | Prior: 91.0 | Poll: 88.0
Present Situation: 123.3 | Prior: 118.7
Expectations: 70.9 | Prior: 72.6

Professional Market Commentary

The dominant tape read was that the headline beat was real, but the internals were worse. Reuters and AP both highlighted the top-line rise to 91.8, yet also stressed that the expectations index remained below the Conference Board’s recession-warning threshold of 80 for a 14th straight month and that inflation concerns intensified as energy costs rose. Official Conference Board commentary likewise said the headline improvement came from present conditions, while inflation expectations and other forward-looking details deteriorated.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is a false-dawn headline and an important one. The confidence beat versus consensus matters for current-conditions optics, and the present situation index rose materially to 123.3. But the more policy-relevant signal remains forward deterioration: expectations fell to 70.9, extending the run below 80 to fourteen months, while households also reported stronger inflation anxiety. That split says consumers do not yet describe current conditions as collapsing, but they are increasingly uneasy about the forward income / price / labor mix. In practical macro terms, present behavior is holding better than forward confidence, which is exactly the structure that can persist briefly before discretionary demand weakens.
State transition: false dawn.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD file had already settled into a softer-growth / firmer-price-pressure configuration. Consumer confidence does not overturn that. Instead it sharpens it by showing that current conditions remain relatively resilient while the forward-looking expectations layer stays recessionary.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Strategically this blocks a simple “growth down equals Fed relief” story. The headline beat helps near-term resilience optics, but the worse expectations and higher inflation anxiety keep policy constraint elevated. If retail sales disappoint, the present-versus-forward split will close in a more growth-negative direction; if spending holds up, the Fed is left with sticky demand optics under a worse inflation psychology backdrop.

Release Date: 31 Mar 2026 | Local Time: 09:30 CT

Reuters Data

Texas Service Sector Outlook (General Business Activity): -13.3 | Prior: -3.2
Dallas Fed Services Revenues: 1.3 | Prior: 4.1

Professional Market Commentary

There was no meaningful Tier 1 public house debate on the services print itself, and coverage was far lighter than for Dallas manufacturing. The official Dallas Fed services framework remains the key anchor: the survey measures revenue, employment, prices and general business conditions across the state service sector, which now also includes retail. Against that backdrop, the March tape points to a much worse services signal than the manufacturing headline attracted attention to.

Comment — Economist Layer

This is the most under-covered release in the current U.S. file and one of the most important. The general business activity index fell to -13.3 from -3.2, while revenues slowed to 1.3 from 4.1. That is not a mild wobble; it is a sharp re-acceleration of contraction in the broader Texas services economy. Relative to Dallas manufacturing at -0.2, it also reveals a strong internal divergence: energy-adjacent industrial activity is holding far better than the consumer-facing services sector. Because the service sector is the larger macro bucket, that divergence matters. A weak services read paired with stalling revenue growth fits much better with the deteriorating labor-flow data and with the forward expectations weakness in consumer surveys than with any simple soft-landing narrative.
State transition: escalation.

Context from prior regional live file:

The prior USD live file had not yet received a comparably strong services-specific regional deterioration signal. It already leaned toward slower domestic demand, but this release strengthens that view materially by showing that the larger service-sector bucket is worsening more sharply than the factory side.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

Strategically, this matters more than the media weight on the release suggests. If national services and payroll-related releases validate the same pattern, the U.S. week will shift from “slower but resilient” to “headline resilience masking a broader service-economy downshift.” That would be a more growth-negative configuration for rates and cyclicals, even if inflation constraint stays elevated.
