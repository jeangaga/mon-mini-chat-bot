MONDAY 13 APR 2026

United States — Existing Home Sales (Mar)
Release Date: 13 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET
Importance: ***

Reuters Data

Existing Home Sales (SAAR): 3.98M | Prior: 4.09M | Revised: 4.13M | Poll: 4.06M | Surprise: -0.080M

Professional Market Commentary

The official NAR release and the public institutional tape converged on the same broad framing: March was a weak start to the spring selling season, with sales down 3.6% m/m to a nine-month low, inventory improving only modestly, and prices still rising because supply remains structurally tight. The professional read was not “demand recovery interrupted,” but “housing remains trapped by affordability, weak confidence, and renewed mortgage-rate pressure.”

Comment — Economist Layer

The key signal is transaction freeze, not price correction. Sales fell in all four regions m/m, the Northeast was especially weak, months’ supply rose to 4.1, yet the median price still rose 1.4% y/y to a record March level of $408,800. That combination says the market is clearing at a lower transaction volume rather than through broad price adjustment. The February revision up matters mechanically, but it does not soften the message: the sequential move from a revised 4.13M to 3.98M is a real deterioration in a rate-sensitive sector. NAR’s own 2026 forecast downgrade, from +14% to +4% for existing-home sales growth, reinforces that the weakness is no longer being treated as a temporary wobble.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already moved the housing bucket into a fragile, rate-sensitive downside channel: mortgage rates were still restrictive, purchase applications remained below year-ago levels, and the archive explicitly described housing demand as soft even after temporary rate relief. March existing-home sales upgrades that signal from “fragile spring” to “clear transaction freeze.”

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For macro mapping, this is a clean growth-downside transmission channel rather than a direct Fed mover by itself. It strengthens the case that tighter financial conditions are now biting in the most mortgage-sensitive part of the economy, but because prices remain sticky, housing is not delivering the kind of fast disinflation relief that would ease the policy constraint. The next test is whether April builder sentiment and later hard activity data show the weakness broadening beyond resale transactions.

TUESDAY 14 APR 2026

United States — NFIB Business Optimism Index / Small Business Economic Trends (Mar)
Release Date: 14 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 06:00 ET
Importance: **

Reuters Data

NFIB Small Business Optimism Index: 95.8 | Prior: 98.8

Professional Market Commentary

The official NFIB release showed a clear sentiment deterioration: optimism fell 3.0 points below the 52-year average and the Uncertainty Index rose to 92, far above its long-run average. Reuters’ public framing emphasized oil-shock pressure and a worsening operating backdrop for small firms, while the NFIB release itself highlighted weaker profit trends and much higher uncertainty rather than a clean cyclical rebound story.

Comment — Economist Layer

The important signal here is not the headline level alone but the jump in uncertainty. Small-business optimism at 95.8 is back below its long-run norm, while uncertainty at 92 implies firms are becoming materially less willing to commit on capex, hiring, and inventory decisions. This fits better as a forward-operating-conditions warning than as a contemporaneous hard-growth signal. It does not prove labor-market breakage, but it does extend the confidence deterioration from households into owner-operator businesses, which matters because that is where hiring intentions often soften before layoffs do.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already shown collapsing household sentiment, softer labor-demand indicators, and a more restrictive inflation-expectations backdrop without a full layoffs signal. NFIB extends that same sequence into the small-business channel: the problem is no longer just consumer mood, but a broader deterioration in operating confidence.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

This is not a top-tier policy release, but it matters for regime mapping. If jobless claims and regional surveys begin to soften alongside NFIB, the Fed will face a deeper stagflation bind: confidence and hiring appetite would be deteriorating even as the energy-price channel keeps inflation risks alive. The next verification test is whether April NFIB internals and April labor-leading data show the uncertainty spike feeding into hiring and capex behavior.

United States — PPI Final Demand / PPI ex Food & Energy / PPI ex Food, Energy & Trade / Machine Manufacturing (Mar)

Importance: ****

Reuters Data

PPI Final Demand YY: 4.0% | Prior: 3.4% | Poll: 4.7% | Surprise: -0.70%
PPI Final Demand MM: 0.5% | Prior: 0.7% | Revised: 0.5% | Poll: 1.1% | Surprise: -0.60%
PPI ex Food/Energy YY: 3.8% | Prior: 3.9% | Revised: 3.8% | Poll: 4.2% | Surprise: -0.40%
PPI ex Food/Energy MM: 0.1% | Prior: 0.5% | Revised: 0.3% | Poll: 0.5% | Surprise: -0.40%
PPI ex Food/Energy/Trade YY: 3.6% | Prior: 3.5%
PPI ex Food/Energy/Trade MM: 0.2% | Prior: 0.5%
PPI Machine Manufacturing: 196.2 | Prior: 195.9 | Revised: 195.8

Professional Market Commentary

The institutional tape treated this as a “soft print, hard story” release. Official BLS details showed headline pressure came overwhelmingly from goods, especially energy and gasoline, while final-demand services were unchanged. Reuters and other market commentary read the undershoot as tactical relief versus expectations, but not as a clean inflation-clear signal, because March likely captures only the first phase of the oil shock flowing through the producer system.

Comment — Economist Layer

The internal hierarchy matters more than the headline miss. Final-demand goods rose 1.6% m/m, with energy up 8.5% and gasoline up 15.7%, while services were flat. That means the upstream inflation shock is real, but it is still narrow in transmission. Core ex food and energy at 0.1% m/m is soft, yet the broader “super-core” ex food, energy, and trade still rose 0.2% m/m and some PCE-relevant services categories, including airline passenger services and outpatient care, moved higher. So the cleanest read is not “inflation problem solved,” but “pipeline shock confirmed while broad pass-through remains incomplete.” Relative to expectations, this buys tactical breathing room; relative to the March shock path, it still points to a harder April–May inflation window if services begin to absorb fuel and logistics costs.

State transition: deterioration

Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already tightened the inflation constraint through ISM prices paid, headline CPI, and higher Michigan inflation expectations, while median and trimmed inflation stayed softer. March PPI fits exactly that split: upstream goods and energy pressure are now confirmed at the producer level, but the broad services core has not yet fully rolled over.

HF Take — Strategist Layer

For the Fed, this is a constraint-tightening release, but not a clean hawkish acceleration trigger. It removes some near-term panic because the print undershot badly versus consensus, yet it does not reopen a fast easing path because the energy shock is now visible in official upstream prices and the forward risk is that services catch up later. The next gating variable is not whether March PPI was soft versus poll; it is whether April producer-services and March core PCE show that the goods shock is starting to infect the stickier core.
