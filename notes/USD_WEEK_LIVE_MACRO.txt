United States — Existing Home Sales (Mar)
Release Date: 13 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET
Importance: ***


Reuters Data


Existing Home Sales (SAAR): 3.98M | Prior: 4.09M | Revised: 4.13M | Poll: 4.06M | Surprise: -0.080M. 


Professional Market Commentary


The official NAR release and the public institutional tape converged on a weak-start framing for the spring selling season: sales fell 3.6% m/m to a nine-month low, all four regions declined m/m, inventory improved only modestly, and prices kept rising because supply remained structurally scarce. The read was not “housing recovery interrupted” so much as “housing still trapped by affordability, weak confidence, and renewed mortgage-rate pressure.” 


Comment — Economist Layer


The key signal is transaction freeze rather than price correction. Inventory rose to 1.36 million units and months’ supply increased to 4.1, yet the median existing-home price still rose 1.4% y/y to $408,800, which means the market is clearing through lower volumes rather than through broad price adjustment; the upward February revision matters mechanically, but the move from a revised 4.13M to 3.98M is still a real deterioration in a rate-sensitive sector. NAR’s cut to its 2026 existing-home sales growth forecast, now 4% instead of the prior 14%, reinforces that this is no longer being treated as a temporary wobble. 
State transition: deterioration
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD sequence had already shown housing as a weak, rate-sensitive downside channel rather than a recovering demand engine. March existing-home sales confirms that trajectory and intensifies it through a sharper transaction drop despite a firmer February base. The housing bucket therefore remains a downside growth channel without yet delivering meaningful price-clearing disinflation. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


For macro mapping, this is a clean growth-downside transmission channel rather than a direct Fed mover on its own. It strengthens the case that tighter financial conditions are now biting in the most mortgage-sensitive part of the economy, but because prices remain sticky, housing is not delivering the kind of fast disinflation relief that would ease the policy constraint. The next verification test is whether builder sentiment and later hard activity data show the weakness broadening beyond resale transactions.

United States — NFIB Business Optimism Index (Mar)
Release Date: 14 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 06:00 ET
Importance: **


Reuters Data


NFIB Small Business Optimism Index: 95.8 | Prior: 98.8. 


Professional Market Commentary


The official NFIB release showed a clear sentiment deterioration: optimism fell 3.0 points below the 52-year average and the Uncertainty Index rose to 92, well above its long-run average. The public tape emphasized worsening profit trends and a materially more uncertain operating backdrop rather than any clean cyclical rebound story. 


Comment — Economist Layer


The important signal is not the headline level alone but the jump in uncertainty. Small-business optimism at 95.8 is below its long-run norm, while uncertainty at 92 implies firms are becoming less willing to commit on capex, hiring, and inventory decisions; that makes this more useful as a forward operating-conditions warning than as a contemporaneous hard-growth signal. It does not prove labor-market breakage, but it extends the confidence deterioration from households into owner-operator businesses, where hiring intentions often soften before layoffs do. 
State transition: deterioration
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already shown softer household sentiment and weaker labor-demand signals without a claims-based labor break. NFIB extends that same sequence into the small-business channel by showing weaker operating confidence and sharply higher uncertainty. The regime implication is broader growth fragility rather than a labor-market rupture already visible in hard data. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


This is not a top-tier policy release, but it matters for regime mapping. If claims and regional surveys soften alongside NFIB, the Fed faces a deeper stagflation bind: confidence and hiring appetite would be deteriorating even as the price pipeline stays firm. The next verification test is whether the uncertainty spike feeds into hiring plans, compensation intentions, and broader labor-leading indicators.

PPI ex Food/Energy/Trade MM: 0.2% | Prior: 0.5%
PPI Machine Manufacturing: 196.2 | Prior: 195.9 | Revised: 195.8. 


Professional Market Commentary


The institutional tape treated this as a soft print with a harder underlying story. Official BLS details showed headline pressure came overwhelmingly from goods, especially energy and gasoline, while final-demand services were unchanged; the public market read was tactical relief against expectations, not a clean inflation-clear signal. 


Comment — Economist Layer


The internal hierarchy matters more than the headline miss. Final-demand goods rose 1.6% m/m, with nearly half of that rise tied to gasoline, while final-demand services were flat because a decline in trade margins offset firmer transportation and warehousing; core ex food and energy at 0.1% m/m is therefore genuinely soft, not just superficially soft. 
JGM note: core producer prices rose only 0.1% m/m versus 0.5% consensus, partly because trade-service margins narrowed and partly because core goods prices were only about 0.2% m/m, with finished core goods closer to 0.1%. That is an encouraging sign that tariff-related pressure may be easing at the margin and that pipeline inflation is not yet running out of control even if March core PCE still prints around 0.28% m/m.
The clean read is therefore not “inflation problem solved,” but “pipeline shock confirmed while the non-energy core remains more contained than feared.” That buys the Fed tactical breathing room for March, even if the April-May window stays harder should services begin to absorb fuel and logistics costs. 
State transition: deterioration
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already tightened the inflation constraint through ISM prices paid, headline CPI, and higher household inflation expectations, even as core and median measures looked less extreme. March PPI confirms that split by showing an official upstream goods-and-energy shock without a full services breakout yet. The regime therefore remains a harder stagflation bind, but not a fully generalized inflation re-acceleration across all core buckets. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


For the Fed, this is a constraint-tightening release, but not a clean hawkish-acceleration trigger. It removes some near-term panic because the print undershot badly versus consensus, yet it does not reopen a fast easing path because the energy shock is now visible in official upstream prices and the forward risk is that services catch up later. The next gating variable is whether March core PCE and April producer-services data show that the goods shock is starting to infect the stickier core.

Professional Market Commentary


The public institutional framing was that the soft headline import print flattered the inflation picture. So March did not deliver a clean imported-inflation break at all: it delivered a softer-than-feared top line while the underlying nonfuel imported-cost structure kept firming. 
State transition: false dawn
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had already shifted the inflation story toward an energy-led headline shock with firmer producer-price pressure underneath. March import prices extend that same trajectory by showing that imported nonfuel cost pressure is also moving higher even though the headline monthly surprise looked soft. The regime implication is that external price transmission remains firmer than the top-line miss suggests. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


For the Fed, this does not reopen an easing lane. Reuters’ running estimate from CPI, PPI, and import prices pointed to roughly 0.7% m/m headline PCE and 0.3% m/m core in March, which would keep the inflation constraint tight even after the soft import headline. The next verification tests are the March PCE release itself and April import prices, which will show whether the survey-timing gap turns into a visibly harder imported-inflation print. 
United States — NAHB / Wells Fargo Housing Market Index (Apr)
Release Date: 15 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 10:00 ET
Importance: ***


Reuters Data


NAHB / Wells Fargo HMI: 34 | Prior: 38 | Poll: 37 | Surprise: -3.0. 


Professional Market Commentary


Official NAHB language and Reuters were aligned: builder sentiment fell sharply as spring demand failed to improve enough to offset elevated mortgage rates, rising material-cost uncertainty, and broader economic caution. The takeaway was not simply “builders cautious,” but “housing still deteriorating on both demand and cost fronts.” 


Comment — Economist Layer


This is a genuine housing-quality deterioration print. Current sales conditions fell to 37, sales expectations fell to 42, traffic dropped to 22, and 36% of builders were still cutting prices while 60% used incentives; that combination says builders are not seeing a spring demand release valve. At the same time, NAHB said 62% of builders reported supplier cost increases due to higher fuel prices, which means the sector is facing demand weakness and input-cost pressure simultaneously rather than one clean cyclical problem. 
State transition: deterioration
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD housing sequence had already been weak and only intermittently stabilized by brief rate relief. April HMI reverses even that fragile stabilization and shows the builder side deteriorating alongside existing-home resale activity. The regime implication is that housing is broadening from a resale weakness story into a fuller sector drag. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


This matters more than a typical sentiment survey because it validates the housing slowdown across both resale and new-build channels. For policy, it strengthens the growth-downside case, but because prices, incentives, and cost uncertainty are moving together rather than simply collapsing, it does not translate into fast housing-led disinflation. The next verification test is whether starts, permits, and mortgage applications continue to weaken from here.

Professional Market Commentary


The official Philadelphia Fed release was much stronger than expected on activity, new orders, and shipments, but the composition was not cleanly growth-positive. Orders rose to 33.0, shipments rose to 34.0, the employment index fell to -5.1, and both price indexes moved higher, with prices paid at 59.3 and prices received at 33.5.

That makes it analytically important as a divergence signal, not as a standalone invitation to upgrade the full U.S. manufacturing regime. 
State transition: false dawn
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD sequence had already moved toward a modest manufacturing stabilization story, but not one strong enough to resolve the wider macro tension. April Philly strengthens the survey side of that story through stronger orders and shipments, yet the negative employment reading and hotter prices mean it still does not settle the broader growth-versus-inflation contradiction. HF Take — Strategist Layer

United States — Industrial Production / Capacity Utilization / Manufacturing Output (Mar)
Release Date: 16 Apr 2026 | Local Time: 09:15 ET
Importance: ***


Reuters Data


Industrial Production MM: -0.5% | Prior: 0.2% | Revised: 0.7% | Poll: 0.1% | Surprise: -0.60%
Capacity Utilization SA: 75.7% | Prior: 76.3% | Revised: 76.1% | Poll: 76.3% | Surprise: -0.60%
Manufacturing Output MM: -0.1% | Prior: 0.2% | Revised: 0.4% | Poll: 0.1% | Surprise: -0.20%. 


Professional Market Commentary


The official Fed data and Reuters framed this as a genuine hard-data setback after a stronger February base. Total industrial production fell 0.5%, manufacturing output slipped 0.1%, mining fell 1.2%, and utilities fell 2.3%; Reuters also highlighted a 3.7% drop in motor vehicle output, while total capacity utilization fell to 75.7%. 


Comment — Economist Layer


This is weaker hard output, but not an across-the-board industrial collapse. The negative headline was amplified by utilities, mining, and autos, while manufacturing itself was only modestly down; still, that is a deterioration because February was revised up to a much stronger 0.7%, so March is falling from a firmer base rather than simply correcting a soft one. 
JGM note: non-auto manufacturing still rose about 0.1%, which argues against a severe interruption of underlying factory growth despite the energy shock. The breadth of gains across industries still looked reasonably solid, and manufacturing output rose at roughly a 3% annualized pace in Q1 after a 3.2% annualized decline in Q4, even though the level of output was only about 0.5% above a year earlier.
That leaves the best read as softer March hard activity, not a decisive factory recession. But it also means manufacturing no longer offers a clean offset to housing weakness, because the sector’s underlying trend is only modestly positive while the headline is now vulnerable to energy and transport distortions. 
State transition: deterioration
Context from prior regional live file:
The prior frozen USD week had left the industrial side in a modestly constructive position, with earlier production and manufacturing readings helping offset housing weakness. March reverses part of that progress by taking the headline back into contraction and softening manufacturing output off a stronger prior base. The regime implication is that hard activity no longer provides as clean a non-housing counterweight as it did earlier in the sequence. 


HF Take — Strategist Layer


For cross-asset mapping, this matters because it broadens the growth-softening story beyond housing while still leaving manufacturing softer rather than broken. That should discourage any simplistic “factory boom” narrative even after the strong Philly Fed survey; the next verification test is whether national manufacturing and capex-related releases validate the survey optimism or instead confirm that March marked a wider hard-data downshift.
