As of date: 7 Apr 2026

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

This board reflects the March 19 BoJ hold, the March CPI and service-price sequence, the March PMIs, and the 1 April Tankan: normalization is intact, but the inflation-growth trade-off has become less comfortable and more shock-sensitive.

Executive Summary

Japan remains in a live normalization regime, but the frame now needs to be sharper than “normalization alive, breadth soft.” What changed over the last four weeks is that the Middle East/oil shock became a live BoJ reaction-function variable, not just background noise, while the Q1 Tankan re-opened the hawkish side of the distribution. Large manufacturers improved to 17, large non-manufacturers held at 36, large-enterprise all-industry fixed investment plans printed at 3.3%, and one-year inflation expectations rose to 2.6%. At the same time, the official detail still shows small-enterprise all-industry fixed investment at -8.1%, which means the breadth concern is not invented, but it is secondary to the more hawkish headline read.

What did not change is the directional policy story: the BoJ is still moving along a gradual tightening path, not backing away from it. The binding issue is no longer whether normalization survives; it is whether oil/yen pass-through and firms’ price-setting behavior pull the Bank forward faster than softer domestic breadth pushes it back. That is a more uncomfortable mix than a simple “hold because growth is soft” reading.

The current policy assumption is therefore hawkish hold with a genuinely live April meeting, not a passive waiting regime. April is not the only plausible outcome—June or July remains a credible fallback, and some economists still sit later in the year—but the public tape has clearly shifted away from treating April as remote. Rates should trade timing risk around that live meeting rather than a flat later-year path, while JPY still needs either stronger domestic confirmation or help from lower global yields / policy support to produce durable appreciation.

Growth / Activity

The growth picture is not a clean domestic slowdown. The Tankan headline was supportive, not defensive: large manufacturers rose to 17, large non-manufacturers stayed very strong at 36, and large-enterprise capex plans remained constructive. March PMIs also stayed above 50 in both manufacturing and services. That combination is enough to keep Japan out of a broad contraction narrative and is exactly why the BoJ still has room to keep tightening on the table.

But the internal quality of the cycle is still weaker than the hawkish headline suggests. Factory output fell 2.1% m/m in February, services confidence dropped to its lowest level since September 2020, and the official Tankan detail still shows small-enterprise fixed investment plans in negative territory. So the right growth label is not “broad reacceleration”; it is resilient headline activity with softer domestic breadth underneath.

Labor / Employment

Labour remains the foundational support for the regime. The BoJ’s own March discussion leaned heavily on continued wage gains at large firms and on the need to monitor how far those gains spread to small and micro firms. That is the right framing: labour is not delivering a fresh upside shock, but it is still tight enough to keep the wage channel alive and to stop the Bank from reading softer headline CPI as a clean dovish turn. Service-price data reinforce the same point, with corporate service inflation rising 2.7% y/y in February on tight-labour pass-through.

Inflation

The inflation picture is more hawkish than the headline CPI optics imply. Nationwide core CPI slipped below 2% in February, and Tokyo core CPI slowed to 1.7% in March, but those prints were heavily shaped by fuel subsidies. At the same time, Tokyo inflation excluding fresh food and fuel was 2.3%, the BoJ’s new core gauge excluding special factors printed 2.2%, and service-sector inflation rose to 2.7%. The right read is softer headline inflation, but policy-relevant inflation still around or above target.

The bigger change is that the inflation story is no longer only about services persistence; it is also about oil/yen pass-through as a possible accelerant. The BoJ’s March statement explicitly highlighted crude oil prices and the Middle East as risks to the inflation outlook, and the March Summary of Opinions went further by warning about second-round effects, the possibility of prolonged price pressure, and even a 1970s-type stagflation complication if the shock persists. That makes the inflation-growth trade-off materially less benign than a simple subsidy-distorted pause story.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest BoJ policy meeting was 19 March 2026. The Bank held the policy rate at 0.75% by an 8–1 vote, kept real rates described as “significantly low,” and preserved the condition that rates would rise further if the outlook for economic activity and prices evolves in line with its forecasts. The formal Takata dissent toward 1.0% matters because it confirms the direction of travel is still higher even though the Bank chose not to move immediately.

The March Summary of Opinions is what makes the reaction function more nuanced. It says the next move depends on wages, prices, financial conditions, and the Middle East shock; it also says that if there is no meaningful deterioration in the economy or in wage-setting among small and medium-sized firms, the Bank should raise rates without hesitation. That is not a central bank searching for excuses to wait indefinitely; it is a central bank assessing whether the inflation side is becoming more urgent than the growth side is deteriorating.

Current policy assumption: the base case is a hawkish hold with April genuinely live, not a passive hold-until-later regime. The modal market distribution now runs from April to June/July rather than from June to a distant later-year move, even though October remains part of the economist consensus set. A stronger yen/oil pass-through impulse, firmer wage evidence, or continued inflation-expectation firming would make April more plausible. A bigger domestic-demand hit from the energy shock would push the Bank back toward June/July or later.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end should be read through timing risk around the next BoJ step, not through a dormant path. Markets were already assigning around a 60% chance of an April hike in late March, and Reuters reported that the March Tokyo CPI sequence still left the market seeing about a 70% chance of an April move even as headline core softened. That tells you the public rates tape is pricing the inflation-pass-through risk and Tankan strength more heavily than a plain growth-softening story.

For FX, the yen remains weak enough that the currency itself can become a policy variable without being a formal target. Reuters reported that authorities stepped up intervention warnings as USD/JPY moved near 160, while Ueda separately signaled that exchange-rate moves matter more now because they transmit into prices and growth forecasts more directly. The practical implication is that BoJ normalization alone still does not guarantee sustained JPY strength, but yen weakness can help pull forward the next hike if pass-through intensifies enough.

Financial conditions are therefore best described as still accommodative enough for tightening to continue, but no longer easy in a way that lets the Bank ignore spillovers. The Summary of Opinions explicitly says current conditions remain accommodative, while also warning that the Bank must not fall behind the curve if second-round price effects intensify.

Political / Policy Events

Domestic politics is not the dominant near-term driver, but it is not irrelevant either. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has pushed through two dovish BoJ board nominees and has reportedly expressed reservations about additional tightening. That does not negate the current tightening bias, but it does add a medium-term political friction point, especially if the growth side deteriorates and the case for patience strengthens.

Trigger Map

More dovish / easier policy / hikes pushed back
This requires the energy shock to land mainly through the growth channel: softer household demand, weaker services confidence, and broader margin compression, with underlying services inflation easing rather than broadening. In that state, April drops out and the path shifts toward June/July or later.

Hold-longer / mildly hawkish reweighting
This is the central case: inflation pass-through stays live, labour remains tight, Tankan-style large-firm resilience holds, but the Bank still wants one more round of evidence on wages, beginning-of-period price hikes, and the domestic-demand hit from oil.

More constructive growth / risk-on scenario
This needs the growth side to re-stabilize rather than merely survive: softer domestic breadth would have to stop worsening while inflation expectations and services pricing remain firm. That combination would let the BoJ normalize without the policy dilemma becoming stagflationary.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

The broad public tape confirms the core regime: the BoJ is still normalizing, April is genuinely live, and the Middle East shock is being read at least partly as an inflation accelerant rather than a simple excuse to wait. Large-firm Tankan strength, a 3.3% large-enterprise capex plan, firmer corporate inflation expectations, and official emphasis on low real rates all support a still-hawkish central bank rather than a cautious retreat.

The key tension is that the public narrative is somewhat more hawkish on timing than the pure macro breadth story would imply. Some institutions have moved to an outright April hike call, while official discussion also warns that prolonged oil and yen pass-through could require the Bank to move without long intervals between adjustments. But that does not erase the softer side of the picture: factory output has already weakened, services confidence has slipped, and the Tankan detail still contains a small-enterprise investment warning.

What remains unresolved is whether this becomes a straightforward inflation-following hike cycle or a stagflationary policy dilemma. The live scenario breakers are clear: a deeper oil shock or renewed yen weakness could pull the Bank forward, while broader household and SME weakness could push it back. The political layer is secondary but real as well, because dovish board appointments and explicit government discomfort with tighter policy could matter more if the growth side weakens further.

Bottom Line

Japan is still in a tightening regime, but the regime is now being driven less by clean domestic breadth and more by the interaction between labour resilience, underlying inflation, oil/yen pass-through, and a still-live April decision. The public tape is more hawkish on near-term timing than a simple growth-softening read would suggest. Tankan was supportive at the headline level, not a broad warning signal, though the small-firm detail still argues against treating the economy as uniformly strong. The BoJ base case is no longer “wait and see”; it is “ready to move if inflation-risk evidence dominates the growth hit.” Base case: hawkish hold with April genuinely live, June/July fallback if the domestic side softens further, and normalization still intact through 2026.
