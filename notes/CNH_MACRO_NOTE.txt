CHINA / CNH MACRO NOTE

As of date: 23 Apr 2026
Horizon: 3–6 months

4-WEEK MACRO SIGNAL BOARD

Growth: ~ Better headline floor; still supply- and policy-led rather than household-led
Domestic demand / employment: ↓ Income, credit, and confidence transmission remain weak
Inflation: ~ Upstream firmer; commodity shock now matters through distribution, not overheating
Financial cond: ~ Liquidity ample; household and property transmission still broken
Policy bias: Hold first; fiscal / quasi-fiscal support and targeted easing before broad LPR cuts
Political risk: ↑ External oil / tariff / trade channels matter more than domestic politics

Executive Summary

China remains in a lopsided stabilization regime, but the last month sharpened what the real constraint is. Q1 headline data were better than feared, with GDP at 5.0%, industrial production firm, and the April 20 LPR review delivering an eleventh straight hold at 3.00% / 3.50%. That was enough to keep the hard-landing story off the table and broad easing delayed. But the internals were still weak in the places that matter for medium-horizon durability: March retail sales slowed to 1.7%, unemployment edged up to 5.4%, credit missed across TSF, loans, and M2, and the household channel remained soft enough to confirm that the recovery is not self-sustaining.

What changed over the last 4 weeks is not the existence of the imbalance but the clarity around its mechanism. The economy is not liquidity-starved; it is transmission-poor. Growth is still being carried by exports, industry, and fiscal / policy-supported supply, while household income, credit demand, and confidence remain too weak to validate a real consumption handoff. That makes the core macro problem less “insufficient easing” than “who receives the income generated by growth.” Household income is still growing more slowly than GDP, household borrowing has weakened sharply, the property wealth channel remains impaired, and labor insecurity is still strong enough to keep precautionary behavior elevated.

The inflation story has also become more nuanced. PPI turned positive at 0.5% year-on-year for the first time in 41 months while CPI slowed to 1.0% and fell 0.7% month-on-month. That does not describe demand-led reflation. It describes a system in which upstream costs are firmer while downstream absorption remains weak. If higher commodity prices are absorbed through subsidies or fiscal support, the shock partly shifts income toward households and stabilizes consumption; if passed through, it becomes the wrong kind of inflation and deepens the weak-consumption equilibrium. That distributional choice is now part of the macro outlook.

The binding constraint is therefore threefold: weak household transmission, structurally overbuilt supply in targeted sectors, and rising external exposure through oil and trade. Base case: China stays in patient-hold mode, fiscal and quasi-fiscal support remain the main cyclical lever, and any next monetary move is still more likely to come through RRR or targeted tools than through a near-term LPR cut. For CNH, the bias remains mildly constructive under policy management and softer-dollar conditions, but a stronger currency story still needs more domestic-growth validation than China has delivered so far.

Growth / Activity

Headline growth is holding up better than feared, but the composition is still narrow. The floor continues to come from manufacturing, exports, public investment, and targeted strategic sectors rather than from a broad-based domestic demand recovery. Q1 therefore looks less like reacceleration than like continued stabilization under a policy-engineered supply-side floor.

The key distinction remains level versus quality. Q1 data were good enough to keep policymakers patient, but not good enough to justify a clean medium-horizon upgrade. Export contribution was important in the early part of the quarter, import strength was partly flattered by front-loading and pricing effects, and domestic demand never joined the recovery decisively. March retail weakness matters because it confirms that once the external and policy-supported pieces are stripped out, the household side is still soft.

The deeper structural issue is that China has redirected a meaningful share of the post-property adjustment into manufacturing capacity, especially in strategic sectors such as solar, EVs, batteries, and related industrial chains. That has preserved growth and employment in the near term, but it has also shifted the overcapacity problem rather than solved it. Local governments and SOEs still have incentives to subsidize production to hit growth and employment targets, which keeps volume strong but raises the risk of price competition, weak profitability, and future nominal-growth drag.

That is why the correct 3–6 month read remains stabilization with a visible quality problem. Growth is not collapsing, but it is still not broad enough to validate a self-sustaining domestic cycle. Until household demand, services, and private-sector follow-through improve together, strong headline prints should still be discounted on quality grounds.

Domestic Demand / Employment

Domestic demand remains the main reason the macro story cannot be upgraded more aggressively. March retail sales at 1.7%, weak household borrowing, and unemployment at 5.4% all point in the same direction: consumption is no longer in free fall, but it is still not strong enough to carry the cycle. The more important point is not only that spending is soft, but why it is soft.

Household income still appears to be lagging headline growth. Disposable income growth remains modest relative to overall GDP performance, which means the household share of growth is not improving enough to support a true consumption rebound. This is the core reason China still looks like growth without consumption traction. Liquidity exists, but it is not reaching households in a way that changes behavior.

Credit data reinforce the same diagnosis. March TSF and loan flows disappointed, but the most important weakness remains in household credit. That is the cleanest sign that the problem is confidence and transmission rather than the price of money alone. The property channel remains impaired, the wealth effect is still weak, and labor-market insecurity is still strong enough to suppress borrowing demand. In that environment, forcing banks to lend more to households is not a full solution because the missing ingredient is confidence, not only credit supply.

Employment pressure is therefore best understood through insecurity rather than outright collapse. The economy is still absorbing labor through manufacturing, public investment, and targeted sectors, but household and white-collar confidence remain weak, wage pressure looks soft, and the fear of job loss remains macro-relevant because it keeps saving elevated and consumption cautious. That is why services and consumption still have not matched the resilience seen in supply-side sectors.

The domestic side remains the key falsifier for the whole medium-horizon view. Another soft retail-credit-employment sequence would move the story from incomplete recovery to genuine re-softening. A cleaner improvement in household income, services demand, and labor confidence would be the first real sign that the cycle is broadening.

Inflation

The inflation story is still about composition and distribution, not overheating. March PPI at 0.5% year-on-year and CPI at 1.0%, with a 0.7% month-on-month fall in CPI, show that upstream prices have turned while downstream demand remains soft. That is not a normal reflation regime. It is a cost-push mix in which producers and policymakers still have to decide how much of the shock is absorbed and how much is passed on.

That choice matters. If higher oil and commodity prices are cushioned through subsidies, administered pricing, or broader fiscal support, the state effectively transfers some of the shock away from households and protects real income. If the shock is passed through more fully, households face higher inflation without stronger demand, which would further weaken consumer sentiment and consumption. The commodity shock is therefore a distributional and policy problem as much as an inflation problem.

The industrial side complicates the picture further. China still has excess capacity in several strategic sectors, and as long as capacity exceeds demand, price competition remains intense. That keeps a deflationary pulse alive in parts of the goods sector even as upstream costs rise. The result is squeezed corporate profitability rather than healthy nominal reflation. This is one reason the positive PPI turn should not be over-read as the start of a durable reflation regime.

The unresolved question is persistence. If upstream pressure fades and domestic demand stays weak, room for later support reopens more clearly. If commodity pressure persists while demand remains soft, the policy mix becomes more awkward because broad easing becomes harder to justify while household purchasing power stays under pressure. Inflation therefore remains a delay-to-easing issue, not a tightening story.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest policy meeting was the 20 April 2026 LPR review. The PBOC left the one-year LPR at 3.00% and the five-year LPR at 3.50%, extending the hold to eleven straight months. The immediate backdrop was Q1 GDP at 5.0%, firmer industrial output, and the first positive PPI reading in more than three years, all of which reduced the urgency for broad easing. At the same time, the weak March retail and credit detail made clear that the hold was not a sign of confidence in a repaired domestic cycle, but of preference for patience and targeted support.

The reaction function is best understood through five constraints. First, headline growth is strong enough to buy time. Second, fiscal and industrial policy are still doing more cyclical work than headline monetary easing. Third, weak household transmission means lower rates alone are not the missing ingredient. Fourth, upstream inflation and commodity uncertainty make broad easing less clean than it looked earlier in the year. Fifth, a structurally weak banking and property transmission channel still makes quantity tools and targeted support more attractive than another visible LPR cut.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is hold first, with fiscal, quasi-fiscal, and targeted tools carrying most of the support burden while policymakers watch whether domestic weakness becomes persistent enough to force a stronger response. The next easing step is still more likely to be an RRR move or targeted credit / support measures than a near-term LPR cut. A more dovish path would require another weak retail-credit-employment sequence, softer Q2 activity, and some easing in the current cost-push inflation mix. A less dovish path would require growth near target, continued industrial resilience, and a judgment that fiscal backing plus supply-side strength are enough to maintain stability. The central bank is reacting most to growth quality, transmission failure, and the distributional consequences of external cost shocks, not to headline growth alone.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is trading delayed easing, not tightening. The market no longer sees China as a near-term hard-landing / imminent-easing story, but it also does not see a clean domestic recovery that would justify a materially less dovish medium-term bias. That leaves rates anchored by patience, with re-pricing driven mainly by whether domestic softness persists or stabilizes.

For CNH, the story has become more constructive than a pure capped-stability framework, but it is still not a fully autonomous bull case. The better read is managed mild upside under policy supervision, helped by broader dollar weakness, the absence of a domestic hard-landing signal, and some tolerance from policymakers for appreciation. But the stronger-CNH case still depends on more than external resilience. A currency move driven only by soft USD and exports is less durable than one backed by firmer domestic growth and better household transmission.

China also looks relatively more insulated than some peers from an oil shock because of its energy mix, coal usage, and administrative pricing buffers. That resilience is real, but only relative. Industrial processes, transport, refining margins, and import costs still leave the economy exposed to a sustained oil rise. So the oil shock does not break China immediately, but it can still worsen margins, complicate easing, and reduce the quality of growth.

Financial conditions therefore remain easy in aggregate but selective in impact. Liquidity is ample, policy management is active, and the currency is supported at the margin, but the property and household channels are still too weak for an unqualified domestic-risk-on call. In cross-asset terms, that still argues for expressing China more through Hong Kong / externally geared and policy-favored channels than through pure onshore domestic-demand beta.

Political / Policy Events

There has been no regime-changing domestic political shock in the last month. The more important message from policy remains continuity: no broad pivot, no wholesale rescue, and no return to a 2015–16 style capacity-cleanup framework. Policy is still selective, sector-directed, and focused on stabilization rather than redistribution at scale.

That matters because the industrial-policy tilt remains central. Strategic sectors such as technology, infrastructure, new energy, EVs, batteries, and related supply chains are still being prioritized, while household demand repair remains more indirect and incomplete. The political implication is that policymakers still appear more comfortable supporting production and employment through targeted sectors than engineering a large immediate transfer toward households.

External policy risk is more important than domestic political noise. Trade frictions, tariffs, chip and rare-earth tensions, and the broader oil / Middle East channel all matter because exports and strategic industry are doing too much of the macro work. Base case remains tactical status-quo management rather than rupture, but the external side is still the faster route to scenario disruption than domestic politics alone.

Exports slowed to 2.5%, imports jumped 27.8%, and the surplus compressed to $51.13 billion. The key message was not simply a smaller surplus, but that the external floor is becoming less clean and more exposed to commodity pricing, front-loading, and trade-policy volatility.

Q1 GDP at 5.0% confirmed that the floor still holds, but the surrounding detail showed why the regime remains narrow. This release locked in the right macro distinction: better headline resilience, but still not a broad household-led recovery.

The eleventh straight hold confirmed the reaction function after the mixed April data. It told the market that policymakers still prefer patience, fiscal lead, and targeted tools over broad easing while the domestic side remains weak but not collapsing.

Trigger Map

Bottom Line

China is still stabilizing, but it is stabilizing through supply, industry, and policy support rather than through a repaired household sector. The economy is not short of liquidity; it is short of income transmission, confidence, and a functioning property-consumption channel. That keeps the PBOC patient, keeps broad easing delayed, and leaves the real macro question centered on whether policy starts shifting more income toward households. CNH can stay mildly constructive under policy management and softer-dollar conditions, but the stronger currency case still needs domestic validation. Base case: headline resilience persists, but the regime remains narrow, externally exposed, and structurally constrained by weak household transmission.

Executive Summary

China has moved into policy-supported stabilization, but the recovery is still being carried more by exports, public-channel investment, and official-sector improvement than by broad domestic-demand repair. The strongest macro shift in the last month was the Jan-Feb hard-data upside surprise — industrial output 6.3%, retail sales 2.8%, fixed-asset investment 1.8% — alongside a very strong external sector, with Jan-Feb exports up 21.8% year on year and the trade surplus at $213.6 billion. March official manufacturing then returned to expansion at 50.4, confirming better headline momentum.

What changed is that the market no longer trades China as a near-term hard-landing / imminent-easing story. The bigger repricing event was stronger hard data plus the 20 March LPR hold, reinforced by oil-shock inflation risk and reduced easing bets in China rates markets. What did not change is equally important: property remains the clearest structural drag, labor and private-demand confirmation are still incomplete, and the recovery still looks government- and export-led rather than self-sustaining.

The binding constraint is therefore not “activity still collapsing,” but “can stabilization broaden without a larger policy push?” Base case: the PBOC stays patient, keeps structural and quantity tools available, and waits for clearer evidence on labor, property, private credit demand, and the durability of the oil-cost shock. For CNH, hard-landing downside is smaller than in January, but the currency still lacks a clean appreciation story because near-term trading is dominated by USD direction, official stability management, and geopolitical energy risk rather than by marginal domestic survey swings.

Growth / Activity

The 4-week impulse was positive first, then more conditional. The dominant growth upgrade came from the Jan-Feb activity block and the trade data: output, retail, and investment all beat expectations, while exports surged and imports also held firm. That sequence reduced hard-landing risk decisively and justified the market move away from imminent-easing expectations.

The late-March PMI sequence did not cancel that improvement, but it did qualify it. Official manufacturing rose to 50.4, the strongest in a year, while the private manufacturing PMI eased to 50.8 from 52.1 and services slowed to 52.1 from 56.7. The right read is moderation from a strong February private-sector base rather than a full regime reversal: official and large-enterprise activity improved, private-sector momentum stayed positive but cooled, and services lost speed. That is enough to keep the “uneven” label, but not enough to call renewed deterioration.

The sector map remains clear. Exports and manufacturing competitiveness are doing much of the cyclical work, public support is keeping investment positive, and property is still the weakest structural pocket. This is why the medium-horizon story is stabilization with incomplete handoff, not broad domestic reflation.

Domestic Demand / Employment

Domestic demand has improved from the January trough, but not enough to validate a self-sustaining cycle. Retail sales rebounded in Jan-Feb, yet the broader transmission chain still looks incomplete: urban unemployment rose to 5.3% in the early-year activity block, official PMI employment remained in contraction, and the March services survey reported the sharpest staffing cuts in six months.

The deeper problem is still where credit and support are flowing. Policy has been more effective at stabilizing public investment, strategic manufacturing, and headline activity than at reviving household demand or broad private-sector animal spirits. That is why stronger activity prints have not yet translated into a clean consumption-and-employment confirmation loop.

One nuance matters here: private manufacturing employment improved in March even as the headline PMI cooled, so the labor picture is not uniformly worsening. But the broader domestic signal is still that hiring, household confidence, and property-linked demand have not turned strongly enough to reclassify the recovery as self-propelling.

Inflation

China is no longer a clean disinflationary offset. The key shift in the last month was oil- and logistics-driven cost-push pressure entering the factory side more forcefully, with official input and output prices at four-year highs and private-sector manufacturing reporting the fastest input-cost inflation since March 2022. Wall Street and bank commentary have responded by marking up PPI expectations and pushing back easing assumptions.

But the inflation story is still the wrong kind of inflation from a growth perspective. It is cost-push, margin-squeezing, and externally driven much more than it is demand-led domestic reflation. Services pricing remained much softer in March, and public commentary from ING and Huang Yiping still points to scope to absorb some imported inflation without abandoning easing optionality altogether. So the right formulation is: easing room has narrowed, not closed.

The unresolved issue is whether this remains a temporary oil-and-shipping distortion or becomes persistent enough to keep PPI positive and delay structural easing materially. That question matters more for the policy path than one month of PMI divergence does.

Last Central Bank Meeting + Current Policy Assumption

The latest policy meeting was the 20 March 2026 Loan Prime Rate decision. The PBOC left the 1-year LPR at 3.00% and the 5-year LPR at 3.50%, extending the long hold pattern. More importantly, the broader official message has stayed consistent: monetary policy remains “appropriately loose,” liquidity should stay ample, the yuan should remain broadly stable, and the central bank still retains flexibility to use RRR and rate cuts when needed. The 31 March MPC statement also highlighted stronger external shocks while maintaining the easing-capable stance.

The reaction function should now be framed less through LPR timing alone and more through three constraints: FX stability, bank-margin / transmission concerns, and the preference for structural or quantity tools before headline rate cuts. The market is waiting for clearer evidence that growth comes under pressure again, or that domestic-demand transmission remains too weak, before expecting another meaningful easing step. Imported-cost pressure has raised the bar, but it has not created a tightening regime.

CURRENT POLICY ASSUMPTION

Base case over the next 3–6 months is hold first, with structural support and liquidity management still active, while any next material easing step is more likely to come through RRR or targeted tools than through an early headline LPR cut. A more dovish path would require renewed weakening in retail, labor, property, and private PMIs, or a clear fade in the oil-cost shock. A less dovish path would come from sustained hard-data resilience, official/private PMI convergence above 50, and persistent cost-push inflation that keeps rate cuts politically and macro-financially awkward. The PBOC is reacting most to transmission quality, external shocks, and exchange-rate stability rather than to headline activity alone.

Rates / FX / Financial Conditions

The front end is trading delayed easing, but the better structural description is that markets now expect any future support to come more through RRR, liquidity operations, and targeted tools than through quick LPR cuts. That is why the March hold mattered more as confirmation of patience than as a standalone policy shock.

For CNH, the medium-term floor still comes from the external balance and the reduced hard-landing risk. That means domestic growth-quality nuances matter more for medium-horizon valuation than for day-to-day price action. The bar for a sustained CNH appreciation story remains high unless domestic-demand validation improves materially or the dollar weakens sharply.

Financial conditions are therefore not restrictive in a cyclical sense. Liquidity is ample, but transmission remains selective. China’s problem is not the absolute level of money availability; it is that policy traction still shows up more reliably in public investment, manufacturing, and exports than in household demand and private-sector confidence.

Political / Policy Events

The key domestic policy event was the March growth-and-fiscal package: Beijing set a 2026 growth target of 4.5%–5% and a fiscal deficit target around 4% of GDP, while leaning again on infrastructure, public services, and consumption support. That keeps the policy floor credible, but it also confirms that China is still relying heavily on public channels and export strength rather than on a spontaneous private-demand repair.

There has been no regime-relevant domestic political shock in the last month. The more important policy-risk channel is external: an export-led growth model that is working better than expected, but is also more exposed to trade friction, shipping disruption, and political backlash abroad. EU trade frictions and the broader scrutiny of Chinese export strength are therefore more relevant to the 3–6 month CNH outlook than domestic political noise.

Debate / Public Tape Tension

Public macro tape broadly agrees on the core architecture: China has stabilized, but mostly through exports, public support, and better official-sector momentum rather than through a full private-demand repair. Public consensus is more cautious than a hard “quality deterioration” call: private manufacturing and services both slowed, but both remained in expansion, and cost-push inflation is viewed by many desks as a delay-to-easing story rather than a full policy constraint. Bottom Line

China is no longer in a near-term hard-landing regime. It is in policy-supported stabilization, with the external sector and public channels doing most of the work while property, labor, and private demand still lag. The oil shock and March price data have made the easing path less clean, but not closed it. The PBOC still looks more likely to manage through structural tools, liquidity, and patience than through quick headline rate cuts. Base case: stabilization persists, but the recovery remains incomplete and CNH stays more tied to USD, fixing discipline, and external risk than to a clean domestic reflation story.

1) Executive Summary – Macro Regime & New Information (written last)

Macro regime (1–2 lines)
China remains in an “external cushion / domestic drag” regime. External demand (trade) is outperforming and reducing near-term stimulus urgency, while domestic momentum remains conditional on credit transmission and the property channel.

What changed in the PRIMARY window (Jan 5–Jan 16)

External impulse materially stronger than priced: exports and imports both beat expectations, and the trade surplus remains exceptionally large (Jan 14).

Credit impulse improved: TSF and new yuan loans beat consensus (Jan 15), signaling policy traction — but without evidence of broad-based acceleration.

Inflation constraint unchanged: CPI remains very low; PPI is less negative but still deflationary — reflation is incremental, not regime-changing.

What did NOT change (anchors)

The binding constraint remains domestic demand, especially consumption and property.

The political external-risk channel tied to a persistent surplus remains intact.

Reaction-function interpretation (policy asymmetry)

Policy asymmetry remains dovish-but-controlled: low inflation allows accommodation, but the binding constraint is credit transmission and FX stability, not liquidity levels.

Stronger trade + credit data reduce the probability of an immediate “shock stimulus”, but do not eliminate convexity if domestic data deteriorate.

Explicit regime-change triggers

Hawkish / less easing trigger: faster-than-expected reflation (PPI toward zero, core CPI acceleration) or FX pressure.

Dovish / stronger support trigger: renewed domestic demand weakness (retail sales, IP, FAI) or renewed property stress that impairs confidence and credit transmission.
